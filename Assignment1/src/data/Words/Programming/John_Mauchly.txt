Presper Eckert">J. Presper Eckert</a>, designed <a href="/wiki/ENIAC" title="ENIAC">ENIAC</a>, the first general purpose electronic <a href="/wiki/Digital_computer" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital computer">digital computer</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/EDVAC" title="EDVAC">EDVAC</a>, <a href="/wiki/BINAC" title="BINAC">BINAC</a> and <a href="/wiki/UNIVAC_I" title="UNIVAC I">UNIVAC I</a>, the first commercial computer made in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</p>/n<p>Together they started the first computer company, the <a href="/wiki/Eckert-Mauchly_Computer_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Eckert-Mauchly Computer Corporation">Eckert-Mauchly Computer Corporation</a> (EMCC), and pioneered fundamental computer concepts including the <a href="/wiki/Stored_program" class="mw-redirect" title="Stored program">stored program</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subroutines" class="mw-redirect" title="Subroutines">subroutines</a>, and programming languages. Their work, as exposed in the widely read <i><a href="/wiki/First_Draft_of_a_Report_on_the_EDVAC" title="First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC">First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC</a></i> (1945) and as taught in the <a href="/wiki/Moore_School_Lectures" title="Moore School Lectures">Moore School Lectures</a> (1946), influenced an explosion of computer development in the late 1940s all over the world.</p>/n<p></p>/n<div id="toc" class="toc">/n<div class="toctitle">/n<h2>Contents</h2>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Biography"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Mauchly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>John W. Mauchly was born on August 30, 1907, to Sebastian and Rachel (Scheidermantel) Mauchly in Cincinnati, Ohio. He moved with his parents and sister, Helen Elizabeth (Betty), at an early age to Chevy Chase, Maryland, when Sebastian Mauchly obtained a position at the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Institution_of_Washington" class="mw-redirect" title="Carnegie Institution of Washington">Carnegie Institution of Washington</a> as head of its Section of Terrestrial Electricity. As a youth, Mauchly was interested in science, and in particular with electricity, and as a young teenager was known to fix neighbors' electric systems. Mauchly attended E.V. Brown Elementary School in Chevy Chase and McKinley Technical High School in Washington, DC. At McKinley, Mauchly was extremely active in the debate team, was a member of the national honor society, and became editor-in-chief of the school's newspaper, Tech Life. After graduating from high school in 1925, he earned a scholarship to study engineering at <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins University</a>. He subsequently transferred to the Physics Department, and without completing his undergraduate degree, instead earned a Ph.D. in physics in 1932.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>/n<p>From 1932 to 1933, Mauchly served as a research assistant at Johns Hopkins University where he concentrated on calculating energy levels of the formaldehyde spectrum. Mauchly's teaching career truly began in 1933 at <a href="/wiki/Ursinus_College" title="Ursinus College">Ursinus College</a> where he was appointed head of the physics department, where he was, in fact, the only staff member.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>/n<p>In the summer of 1941, Mauchly took a Defense Training Course for Electronics at the University of Pennsylvania Moore School of Electrical Engineering. There he met the lab instructor, J. Presper Eckert (1919-1995), with whom he would form a long-standing working partnership. Following the course, Mauchly was hired as an instructor of electrical engineering and in 1943, he was promoted to assistant professor of electrical engineering. Following the outbreak of World War II, the <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Corps_(United_States_Army)" title="Ordnance Corps (United States Army)">United States Army Ordnance Department</a> contracted the Moore School to build an electronic computer which, as proposed by Mauchly and Eckert, would accelerate the recomputation of artillery firing tables.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>/n<p>In 1959, Mauchly left Sperry Rand and started Mauchly Associates, Inc. One of Mauchly Associates' notable achievements was the development of the Critical Path Method (CPM) which provided for automated construction scheduling. Mauchly also set up a consulting organization, Dynatrend, in 1967 and worked as a consultant to Sperry UNIVAC from 1973 until his death in 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>/n<p>John Mauchly died on January 8, 1980, in Abington, Pennsylvania, during heart surgery and following a long illness. His first wife, Mary Augusta Walzl, a mathematician, whom he married on December 30, 1930, drowned in 1946. John and Mary Mauchly had two children, James (Jimmy) and Sidney. In 1948, Mauchly married <a href="/wiki/Kathleen_Antonelli" title="Kathleen Antonelli">Kathleen Kay McNulty</a> (1921-2006), one of the six original ENIAC programmers; they had five children Sara (Sallie), Kathleen (Kathy), John, Virginia (Gini), and Eva.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Moore_School">Moore School</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Mauchly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Moore School">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>In 1941 Dr. Mauchly took a course in wartime electronics at the <a href="/wiki/Moore_School_of_Electrical_Engineering" title="Moore School of Electrical Engineering">Moore School of Electrical Engineering</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>. There he met <a href="/wiki/J._Presper_Eckert" title="J. Presper Eckert">J. Presper Eckert</a>, a recent Moore School graduate. Mauchly accepted a teaching position at the Moore School, which was a center for wartime computing. Eckert encouraged Mauchly to believe that vacuum tubes could be made reliable with proper engineering practices. The critical problem that was consuming the Moore School was ballistics: the calculation of firing tables for the large number of new guns that the U.S. Army was developing for the war effort.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ENIAC">ENIAC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Mauchly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: ENIAC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/ENIAC" title="ENIAC">ENIAC</a></div>/n<p>In 1942 Mauchly wrote a memo proposing the building of a general-purpose electronic computer.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The proposal, which circulated within the Moore School (but the significance of which was not immediately recognized), emphasized the enormous speed advantage that could be gained by using digital electronics with no moving parts. Lieutenant <a href="/wiki/Herman_Goldstine" title="Herman Goldstine">Herman Goldstine</a>, who was the liaison between the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> and Moore School, picked up on the idea and asked Mauchly to write a formal proposal. In April 1943, the Army contracted with the Moore School to build the Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer (<a href="/wiki/ENIAC" title="ENIAC">ENIAC</a>). Mauchly led the conceptual design while Eckert led the hardware engineering on ENIAC. A number of other talented engineers contributed to the confidential "Project PX".</p>/n<p>Because of its high-speed calculations, ENIAC could solve problems that were previously unsolvable. It was roughly a thousand times faster than the existing technology. It could add 5,000 numbers or do 357 10-digit multiplications in one second.</p>/n<p>ENIAC could be programmed to perform sequences and loops of addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, square-root, <a href="/wiki/Input/output" title="Input/output">input/output</a> functions, and conditional branches. Programming was initially accomplished with patch cords and switches, and reprogramming took days. It was redesigned in 1948 to allow the use of <a href="/wiki/Stored_program" class="mw-redirect" title="Stored program">stored programs</a> with some loss in speed.</p>/n<p>In 2002, for his work on ENIAC he was inducted, posthumously, into the <a href="/wiki/National_Inventors_Hall_of_Fame" title="National Inventors Hall of Fame">National Inventors Hall of Fame</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="EDVAC">EDVAC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Mauchly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: EDVAC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>The ENIAC design was frozen in 1944 to allow construction. Eckert and Mauchly were already aware of the limitations of the machine and began plans on a second computer, to be called EDVAC. By January 1945 they had procured a contract to build this <a href="/wiki/Stored-program_computer" title="Stored-program computer">stored-program computer</a>. Eckert had proposed a <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> <a href="/wiki/Delay_line_memory" title="Delay line memory">delay line memory</a> to store both program and data. Later that year, mathematician <a href="/wiki/John_von_Neumann" title="John von Neumann">John von Neumann</a> learned of the project and joined in some of the engineering discussions. He produced what was understood to be an internal document describing the EDVAC.</p>/n<p>The term <a href="/wiki/Von_Neumann_architecture" title="Von Neumann architecture">von Neumann architecture</a> arose from von Neumann's paper, <i><a href="/wiki/First_Draft_of_a_Report_on_the_EDVAC" title="First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC">First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Dated June 30, 1945, it was an early written account of a general purpose stored-program computing machine (the <a href="/wiki/EDVAC" title="EDVAC">EDVAC</a>). Goldstine, in a move that was to become controversial, removed any reference to Eckert or Mauchly and distributed the document to a number of von Neumann's associates across the country. The ideas became widely known within the very small world of computer designers.</p>/n<p>Besides the lack of credit, Eckert and Mauchly suffered additional setbacks due to Goldstine's actions. The ENIAC patent <span><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US3120606">U.S. Patent 3,120,606</a></span>, issued in 1964<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> was filed on June 26, 1947, and granted February 4, 1964, but the public disclosure of design details of EDVAC in the <i>First Draft</i> (which were also common to ENIAC) was later cited as one cause for the <a href="/wiki/Honeywell_v._Sperry_Rand" class="mw-redirect" title="Honeywell v. Sperry Rand">1973 invalidation of the ENIAC patent</a>.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Moore_School_Lectures">The Moore School Lectures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Mauchly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Moore School Lectures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Moore_School_Lectures" title="Moore School Lectures">Moore School Lectures</a></div>/n<p>In March 1946, just after the ENIAC was announced, the Moore School decided to change their patent policy, in order to gain commercial rights to any future and past computer development there. Eckert and Mauchly decided this was unacceptable; they resigned. However they had already been contracted to do one more thing at the Moore School: to give a series of talks on computer design.</p>/n<p>The course "The Theory and Techniques for Design of Digital Computers", ran from July 8 to August 31, 1946. Eckert gave 11 of the lectures; Mauchly and Goldstine each delivered 6. "The Moore School Lectures", as they came to be known, were attended by representatives from the army, the navy, MIT, the National Bureau of Standards, Cambridge University, Columbia, Harvard, the Institute for Advanced Study, IBM, Bell Labs, Eastman Kodak, General Electric, and National Cash Register. A number of the attendees were to later go on to develop computers, such as Maurice Wilkes, of Cambridge, who built EDSAC.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eckert-Mauchly_Computer_Corporation">Eckert-Mauchly Computer Corporation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Mauchly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Eckert-Mauchly Computer Corporation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>In 1947 Eckert and Mauchly formed the first computer company, the <a href="/wiki/Eckert-Mauchly_Computer_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Eckert-Mauchly Computer Corporation">Eckert-Mauchly Computer Corporation</a> (EMCC); Mauchly was president. They secured a contract with the <a href="/wiki/National_Bureau_of_Standards" class="mw-redirect" title="National Bureau of Standards">National Bureau of Standards</a> to build an "EDVAC II", later named <a href="/wiki/UNIVAC" title="UNIVAC">UNIVAC</a>. UNIVAC, the first computer designed for business applications, had many significant technical advantages such as magnetic tape for mass storage. As an interim product, the company created and delivered a smaller computer, <a href="/wiki/BINAC" title="BINAC">BINAC</a>, but were still in a shaky financial situation. They were purchased by <a href="/wiki/Remington_Rand" title="Remington Rand">Remington Rand</a> and became the UNIVAC division.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software">Software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Mauchly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Very early in the history of EMCC, John Mauchly assumed responsibility for programming, coding, and applications for the planned computer systems. His early interaction with representatives of the Census Bureau in 1944 and 1945, and discussion with people interested in statistics, weather prediction, and various business problems in 1945 and 1946 focused his attention on the need to provide new users with the software to accomplish their objectives. He knew it would be difficult to sell computers without application materials, and without training in how to use the systems. And so, EMCC began to assemble a staff of mathematicians interested in coding in early 1947. (from Norberg)</p>/n<p>Mauchly’s interest lay in the application of computers, as well as to their architecture and organization. His experience with programming the ENIAC and its successors led him to create <a href="/wiki/Short_Code" class="mw-redirect" title="Short Code">Short Code</a> (see <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://doi.ieeecomputersociety.org/10.1109/MAHC.1988.10004">"The UNIVAC SHORT CODE"</a>), the first programming language actually used on a computer (predated by Zuse’s conceptual <a href="/wiki/Plankalkul" class="mw-redirect" title="Plankalkul">Plankalkul</a>). It was a pseudocode interpreter for mathematical problems proposed in 1949 and ran on the UNIVAC I and II. Mauchly's belief in the importance of languages led him to hire <a href="/wiki/Grace_Murray_Hopper" class="mw-redirect" title="Grace Murray Hopper">Grace Murray Hopper</a> to develop a compiler for the UNIVAC.</p>/n<p>John Mauchly has also been credited for being the first one using the verb "to <a href="/wiki/Computer_program" title="Computer program">program</a>" in his 1942 paper on electronic computing, although in the context of ENIAC, not in its current meaning.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Mauchly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Dr. Mauchly stayed involved in computers for the rest of his life. He was a founding member and president of the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">Association for Computing Machinery</a> (ACM) and also helped found the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Industrial_and_Applied_Mathematics" title="Society for Industrial and Applied Mathematics">Society for Industrial and Applied Mathematics</a> (SIAM), serving as its fourth president. The Eckert-Mauchly Corporation was bought by <a href="/wiki/Remington_Rand" title="Remington Rand">Remington Rand</a> in 1950 and for ten years Dr. Mauchly remained as Director of Univac Applications Research. Leaving in 1959 he formed Mauchly Associates, a consulting company that later introduced the <a href="/wiki/Critical_path_method" title="Critical path method">critical path method</a> (CPM) for construction scheduling by computer. In 1967 he founded Dynatrend, a computer consulting organization. In 1973 he became a consultant to Sperry Univac.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Mauchly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Mauchly received numerous award and honors. He was a life member of the <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Institute" title="Franklin Institute">Franklin Institute</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Engineering" title="National Academy of Engineering">National Academy of Engineering</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Advancement_of_Management" class="mw-redirect" title="Society for Advancement of Management">Society for Advancement of Management</a>. He was elected a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Radio_Engineers" title="Institute of Radio Engineers">IRE</a>, a predecessor society of <a href="/wiki/IEEE" class="mw-redirect" title="IEEE">IEEE</a>, in 1957, and was a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Statistical_Association" title="American Statistical Association">American Statistical Association</a>. He received an LLD (Hon) degree from the University of Pennsylvania and aDSc(Hon) degree from <a href="/wiki/Ursinus_College" title="Ursinus College">Ursinus College</a>. He was a recipient of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Philadelphia_Award&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Philadelphia Award (page does not exist)">Philadelphia Award</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Scott_Medal" title="Scott Medal">Scott Medal</a>, the Goode Medal of AFIPS (American Federation of Information Processing Societies), the Pennsylvania Award, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emanual_R._Piore_Award&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Emanual R. Piore Award (page does not exist)">Emanual R. Piore Award</a>, the <a href="/wiki/The_Franklin_Institute_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="The Franklin Institute Awards">Howard N. Potts Medal</a>, and numerous other awards.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patent_controversy">Patent controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Mauchly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Patent controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">/n<tr>/n<td class="mbox-image">/n<div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div>/n</td>/n<td class="mbox-text">/n<div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>. <span class="hide-when-compact">Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=John_Mauchly&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span> <small><i>(December 2008)</i></small> <small class="hide-when-compact"><i>(<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div>/n</td>/n</tr>/n</table>/n<p>Mauchly and Eckert's patent on the ENIAC was <a href="/wiki/Honeywell_v._Sperry_Rand" class="mw-redirect" title="Honeywell v. Sperry Rand">invalidated by U.S. Federal Court decision</a> in October, 1973 for several reasons. Some had to do with the time between publication (the <i>First Draft</i>) and the patent filing date (1947). The federal judge who presided over the case ruled that "the subject matter was derived" from the earlier <a href="/wiki/Atanasoff%E2%80%93Berry_Computer" class="mw-redirect" title="Atanasoff–Berry Computer">Atanasoff–Berry Computer</a> (ABC). This statement has become the center of a controversy.</p>/n<p>Critics note that while the court said that the ABC was the first electronic digital computer, it did not define the term <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a>. It had originally referred to a <a href="/wiki/Human_computer" title="Human computer">person who computes</a>, but was adapted to apply to a machine.</p>/n<p>Critics of the court decision also note that there is, at a component level, nothing in common between the two machines. The ABC was binary; the ENIAC was decimal. The ABC used regenerative drum memory; The ENIAC used electronic decade counters. The ABC used its tubes to implement a binary serial adder while the ENIAC used tubes to implement a complete set of decimal operations. The ENIAC's general-purpose instruction set, together with the ability to automatically sequence through them, made it a general-purpose computer.</p>/n<p>Proponents for the court decision emphasize that the testimony established that Mauchly definitely had complete access to Atanasoff's machine and the documents describing it. Letters he wrote to Atanasoff show that he was at one time at least considering building on Atanasoff's approach.</p>/n<p>Mauchly consistently maintained that it was the use of high-speed electronic flip-flops in cosmic-ray counting devices at <a href="/wiki/Swarthmore_College" title="Swarthmore College">Swarthmore College</a> that gave him the idea for computing at electronic speeds.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
