Google, Inc.</i></td>/n</tr>/n<tr>/n<th scope="row">Argued</th>/n<td>April 16, 2012</td>/n</tr>/n<tr>/n<th colspan="2" style="text-align:center;white-space:nowrap">Court membership</th>/n</tr>/n<tr>/n<th scope="row">Judge(s) sitting</th>/n<td><a href="/wiki/William_Haskell_Alsup" title="William Haskell Alsup">William Haskell Alsup</a></td>/n</tr>/n</table>/n<p><i><b>Oracle America, Inc. v. Google, Inc.</b></i> is a dispute related to <a href="/wiki/Oracle_America" class="mw-redirect" title="Oracle America">Oracle</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Copyright_law_of_the_United_States" title="Copyright law of the United States">copyright</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_patent_law" title="United States patent law">patent</a> claims on Google's <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android operating system</a>. In May 2012, the jury in this case found that Google did not infringe on Oracle's patents, and the trial judge ruled that the structure of the <a href="/wiki/Java_API" class="mw-redirect" title="Java API">Java APIs</a> used by Google was not copyrightable.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The parties agreed to zero dollars in <a href="/wiki/Statutory_damages" title="Statutory damages">statutory damages</a> for a small amount of copied code.<sup id="cite_ref-damages_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-damages-4">[4]</a></sup> On May 9, 2014, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Federal_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit">Federal Circuit</a> partially reversed the district court ruling, ruling in Oracle's favor on the copyrightability issue, and <a href="/wiki/Remand_(court_procedure)" title="Remand (court procedure)">remanding</a> the issue of <a href="/wiki/Fair_use" title="Fair use">fair use</a> to the district court.<sup id="cite_ref-cnet_appeals_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnet_appeals-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cafc_13-1021_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cafc_13-1021-6">[6]</a></sup> A petition for <a href="/wiki/Certiorari" title="Certiorari">certiorari</a> was denied by the United States Supreme Court on June 29, 2015. A second trial began May 9, 2016, in which Oracle sought <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>8.8 billion in damages.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> On May 26, 2016, the trial jury sided in favor of Google, ruling the action to be fair use.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>/n<p></p>/n<div id="toc" class="toc">/n<div class="toctitle">/n<h2>Contents</h2>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Background"><span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>/n</ul>/n</div>/n<p></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oracle_America,_Inc._v._Google,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p><a href="/wiki/Java_(software_platform)" title="Java (software platform)">Java</a> was originally developed at <a href="/wiki/Sun_Microsystems" title="Sun Microsystems">Sun Microsystems</a> starting in 1991. It included a new <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming language</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_machine#Process_virtual_machines" title="Virtual machine">virtual machine</a>, and a set of <a href="/wiki/Library_(computing)" title="Library (computing)">libraries</a> for use with the language.<sup id="cite_ref-java_earlyhistory_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-java_earlyhistory-9">[9]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Android, Inc. was founded in 2003 by <a href="/wiki/Andy_Rubin" title="Andy Rubin">Andy Rubin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rich_Miner" title="Rich Miner">Rich Miner</a>, Nick Sears, and Chris White to develop a <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phone</a> platform.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AndroidInc_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AndroidInc-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> purchased Android in 2005 and continued developing the <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android operating system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AndroidInc_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AndroidInc-11">[11]</a></sup> Google released a beta of the Android platform on November 5, 2007, noting that it would use some Java technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Sun CEO <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_I._Schwartz" title="Jonathan I. Schwartz">Jonathan Schwartz</a> responded the same day, congratulating Google and saying they had "strapped another set of rockets to the community's momentum&#160;–  and to the vision defining opportunity across our (and other) planets."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Google released the Android <a href="/wiki/Software_development_kit" title="Software development kit">software development kit</a> (SDK) on November 12, 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Amongst other APIs, Android included <a href="/wiki/Apache_Harmony" title="Apache Harmony">Apache Harmony</a> implementations of some of the APIs<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> from the <a href="/wiki/Java_Platform,_Standard_Edition" title="Java Platform, Standard Edition">Java SE</a> specification.<sup id="cite_ref-groklaw_1202_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-groklaw_1202-17">[17]</a></sup> Google negotiated with Sun about possible partnership and licensing deals for Java, but no agreement was reached.<sup id="cite_ref-groklaw_1202_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-groklaw_1202-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-groklaw_1203_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-groklaw_1203-18">[18]</a></sup></p>/n<p><a href="/wiki/Oracle_Corporation" title="Oracle Corporation">Oracle</a> purchased Sun in January 2010, and continued developing Java.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Oracle continued discussing a possible licensing deal, but an agreement again was not reached.<sup id="cite_ref-groklaw_1203_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-groklaw_1203-18">[18]</a></sup> Oracle sued Google for copyright and patent infringement in August 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="District_Court">District Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oracle_America,_Inc._v._Google,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: District Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>The case was assigned to Judge <a href="/wiki/William_Haskell_Alsup" title="William Haskell Alsup">William Alsup</a>, who split the case into three phases: copyright, patent, and damages.</p>/n<p>The copyright phase consisted of several distinct claims of infringement: a nine-line rangeCheck function, several test files, the <a href="/wiki/Structure,_sequence_and_organization" title="Structure, sequence and organization">structure, sequence and organization</a> of the Java <a href="/wiki/Application_programming_interface" title="Application programming interface">application programming interface</a> (API), and the API documentation. Oracle alleged infringement of 37 separate Java APIs.<sup id="cite_ref-groklaw_1202_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-groklaw_1202-17">[17]</a></sup> After extensive pre-trial briefing, this phase began on April 16, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> At the end of this phase, the jury ruled that the API was infringed, but deadlocked on Google's <a href="/wiki/Fair_use" title="Fair use">fair use</a> defense for this claim. They also found that rangeCheck was infringed, but that neither the documentation nor the other literal code was.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>/n<p>The patent phase began on May 7, 2012 with the same jury.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> By the time of trial, Oracle's patent case comprised claims from two patents, 6061520 (Method and system for performing static initialization),<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and RE38104 (Method and apparatus for resolving data references in generated code).<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Google pursued a non-infringement defense. For the 6061520 patent, they argued that they were using parsing for optimizing static initialization, rather than "simulating execution" as the claim required. For the RE38104 patent, they argued that the instruction did not include a symbolic reference. The jury found non-infringement on all patent counts.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> As a result of these rulings and a stipulation, there was no jury damages phase. The parties agreed to zero dollars in <a href="/wiki/Statutory_damages" title="Statutory damages">statutory damages</a> for a small amount of copied code.<sup id="cite_ref-damages_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-damages-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Holding">Holding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oracle_America,_Inc._v._Google,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Holding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>The court's decision was published on May 31, 2012. It upheld the jury verdict on rangeCheck, though it was described as "overblown".<sup id="cite_ref-groklaw_1202_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-groklaw_1202-17">[17]</a></sup> In response to a motion for a <a href="/wiki/Judgment_as_a_matter_of_law" title="Judgment as a matter of law">judgment as a matter of law</a>, the court ruled that the other literally-copied files also infringed, setting aside that portion of the jury verdict.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>/n<p>However, on the primary copyright issue of the APIs, the court ruled that "So long as the specific code used to implement a method is different, anyone is free under the Copyright Act to write his or her own code to carry out exactly the same function or specification of any methods used in the Java API. It does not matter that the declaration or method header lines are identical." The ruling found that the structure Oracle was claiming was not copyrightable under section 102(b) of the <a href="/wiki/Copyright_Act_of_1976" title="Copyright Act of 1976">Copyright Act</a> because it was a "system or method of operation."</p>/n<p>The court also drew on <i><a href="/wiki/Baker_v._Selden" title="Baker v. Selden">Baker v. Selden</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Whelan_v._Jaslow" title="Whelan v. Jaslow">Whelan v. Jaslow</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Computer_Associates_v._Altai" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer Associates v. Altai">Computer Associates v. Altai</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gates_Rubber_v._Bando_Chemical_Industries&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gates Rubber v. Bando Chemical Industries (page does not exist)">Gates Rubber v. Bando Chemical Industries</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lotus_v._Borland" class="mw-redirect" title="Lotus v. Borland">Lotus v. Borland</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hutchins_v._Zoll&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hutchins v. Zoll (page does not exist)">Hutchins v. Zoll</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Feist_v._Rural" class="mw-redirect" title="Feist v. Rural">Feist v. Rural</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls_v._Phoenix_Control_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Johnson Controls v. Phoenix Control Systems (page does not exist)">Johnson Controls v. Phoenix Control Systems</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Brown_Bag_Software_v._Symantec_Corp." title="Brown Bag Software v. Symantec Corp.">Brown Bag Software v. Symantec Corp.</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Atari_Games_Corp._v._Nintendo_of_America_Inc." title="Atari Games Corp. v. Nintendo of America Inc.">Atari v. Nintendo</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sega_v._Accolade" title="Sega v. Accolade">Sega v. Accolade</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Sony_v._Connectix" class="mw-redirect" title="Sony v. Connectix">Sony v. Connectix</a></i>.</p>/n<p>Based on reviewing this case history, the court noted that:</p>/n<blockquote>/n<p>...the above summary of the development of the law reveals a trajectory in which enthusiasm for protection of "structure, sequence and organization" peaked in the 1980s, most notably in the Third Circuit’s <i>Whelan</i> decision. That phrase has not been re-used by the Ninth Circuit since <i>Johnson Controls</i> in 1989, a decision affirming preliminary injunction. Since then, the trend of the copyright decisions has been more cautious. This trend has been driven by fidelity to Section 102(b) and recognition of the danger of conferring a monopoly by copyright over what Congress expressly warned should be conferred only by patent. This is not to say that infringement of the structure, sequence and organization is a dead letter. To the contrary, it is not a dead letter. It is to say that the <i>Whelan</i> approach has given way to the <i>Computer Associates</i> approach, including in our own circuit. See <i><a href="/wiki/Sega_Enters.,_Ltd._v._Accolade,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Sega Enters., Ltd. v. Accolade, Inc.">Sega Enters., Ltd. v. Accolade, Inc.</a></i>, 977 F.2d 1510, 1525 (9th Cir. 1992); <i><a href="/wiki/Apple_Computer,_Inc._v._Microsoft_Corp." title="Apple Computer, Inc. v. Microsoft Corp.">Apple Computer, Inc. v. Microsoft Corp.</a></i>, 35 F.3d 1435, 1445 (9th Cir. 1994).<sup id="cite_ref-groklaw_1202_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-groklaw_1202-17">[17]</a></sup></p>/n</blockquote>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appeals_Court">Appeals Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oracle_America,_Inc._v._Google,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Appeals Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Oracle appealed to the Federal Circuit Court of Appeals, and Google filed a cross-<a href="/wiki/Appeal" title="Appeal">appeal</a> on the literal copying claim.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-groklaw_appeals_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-groklaw_appeals-30">[30]</a></sup> The hearing was held on December 4, 2013,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/cafc/13-1021/13-1021-2014-05-09.html">judgment</a> was released on May 9, 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>/n<p>The court noted that Copyright Act provides protection to "original works of authorship fixed in any tangible medium of expression" (p.&#160;17). The legislative history explains that literary works include "computer programs to the extent that they incorporate authorship in the programmer's expression of original ideas, as distinguished from the ideas themselves" (p.&#160;18). To qualify for copyright protection a work must be original. 17 U.S.C. § 102(a). The court was therefore "first to assess whether the expression is original to the programmer" (p.&#160;24), something that Google had already conceded (p.&#160;21). This led the court to conclude "that the overall structure of Oracle's API packages is creative, original, and resembles a taxonomy" (p.&#160;14). It therefore reversed the district court on the central issue, holding that the "<a href="/wiki/Structure,_sequence_and_organization" title="Structure, sequence and organization">structure, sequence and organization</a>" of an API is copyrightable. It also ruled for Oracle regarding the small amount of literal copying, holding that it was not <i><a href="/wiki/De_minimis" title="De minimis">de minimis</a></i>. The case was remanded to the district court for reconsideration only on the basis of the <a href="/wiki/Fair_use" title="Fair use">fair use doctrine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cafc_13-1021_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cafc_13-1021-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Supreme_Court_petition">Supreme Court petition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oracle_America,_Inc._v._Google,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Supreme Court petition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>In October 2014, Google petitioned the U.S. Supreme Court to hear the case.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Oracle responded to the petition in December.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Following a discussion of the petition at the Court's January 9 conference,<sup id="cite_ref-SCOTUS_docket_14-410_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCOTUS_docket_14-410-36">[36]</a></sup> the Court issued a <a href="/wiki/Call_for_the_Views_of_the_Solicitor_General" class="mw-redirect" title="Call for the Views of the Solicitor General">Call for the Views of the Solicitor General</a>, asking for the U.S. Solicitor General's input on whether the petition should be granted.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> On May 26, 2015 the U.S. Solicitor General recommended that the petition should be denied, expressing agreement with the appeals court ruling.<sup id="cite_ref-No._14-410_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-No._14-410-38">[38]</a></sup> The Court denied the certiorari petition on June 29, 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-Order_List_.2806.2F29.2F2015.29_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Order_List_.2806.2F29.2F2015.29-39">[39]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_trial">Second trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oracle_America,_Inc._v._Google,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Second trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>As a result of the successful appeal, a new district court trial began on May 9, 2016 on the question of whether Google's actions were <a href="/wiki/Fair_use" title="Fair use">fair use</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Closing arguments were completed on May 23, 2016 and the jury of eight women and two men began deliberations. Oracle was seeking damages of up to US$9 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> On May 26, 2016, the jury found that Android does not infringe Oracle-owned copyrights because its re-implementation of 37 Java APIs is protected by fair use. Oracle announced its intention to appeal,<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> but before doing so it attempted unsuccessful motions to <a href="/wiki/Renewed_JMOL" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewed JMOL">disregard the jury verdict</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> and then to hold a re-trial.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Oracle officially filed its appeal on October 26, 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oracle_America,_Inc._v._Google,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<ul>/n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Oracle_Corporation_v._SAP_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Oracle Corporation v.
