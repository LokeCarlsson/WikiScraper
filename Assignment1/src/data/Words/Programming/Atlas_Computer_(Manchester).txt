class="mw-body-content">/n									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>/n								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about a British supercomputer. For the early American codebreaking computer also known as Atlas, see <a href="/wiki/UNIVAC_1101" title="UNIVAC 1101">UNIVAC 1101</a>.</div>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:University_of_Manchester_Atlas,_January_1963.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/University_of_Manchester_Atlas%2C_January_1963.JPG/220px-University_of_Manchester_Atlas%2C_January_1963.JPG" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/University_of_Manchester_Atlas%2C_January_1963.JPG/330px-University_of_Manchester_Atlas%2C_January_1963.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/University_of_Manchester_Atlas%2C_January_1963.JPG/440px-University_of_Manchester_Atlas%2C_January_1963.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1788" data-file-height="1188" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:University_of_Manchester_Atlas,_January_1963.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nThe University of Manchester Atlas in January 1963</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>The <b>Atlas Computer</b> was a joint development between the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_University_of_Manchester" title="Victoria University of Manchester">University of Manchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ferranti" title="Ferranti">Ferranti</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plessey" title="Plessey">Plessey</a>. It was a second-generation machine, using <a href="/wiki/Discrete_device" class="mw-redirect" title="Discrete device">discrete</a> <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_junction_transistor#Germanium_transistors" title="Bipolar junction transistor">germanium</a> <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistors</a>.</p>/n<p>The first Atlas, installed at Manchester University and officially commissioned in 1962, was one of the world's first <a href="/wiki/Supercomputers" class="mw-redirect" title="Supercomputers">supercomputers</a>, considered to be the most powerful computer in the world at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-LavingtonP41_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LavingtonP41-1">[1]</a></sup> It was said that whenever Atlas went offline half of the United Kingdom's computer capacity was lost.<sup id="cite_ref-LavingtonPP44-45_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LavingtonPP44-45-2">[2]</a></sup>Two other Atlas machines were built: one for <a href="/wiki/BP" title="BP">British Petroleum</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a>, and one for the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Computer_Laboratory" title="Atlas Computer Laboratory">Atlas Computer Laboratory</a> at Chilton near <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>.</p>/n<p>A derivative system was built by Ferranti for <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge University</a>. Called the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(computer)" title="Titan (computer)">Titan</a>, or Atlas 2, it had a different memory organisation and ran a <a href="/wiki/Time-sharing" title="Time-sharing">time-sharing</a> operating system developed by Cambridge University Computer Laboratory. Two further Atlas 2s were delivered: one to the <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">CAD</a> Centre in Cambridge (later called CADCentre, then <a href="/wiki/AVEVA" class="mw-redirect" title="AVEVA">AVEVA</a>), and the other to the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_Weapons_Establishment" title="Atomic Weapons Establishment">Atomic Weapons Research Establishment</a> (AWRE), Aldermaston.</p>/n<p>The University of Manchester's Atlas was decommissioned in 1971,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavington199843_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavington199843-3">[3]</a></sup> but the last was in service until 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavington199844_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavington199844-4">[4]</a></sup> Parts of the Chilton Atlas are preserved by <a href="/wiki/National_Museums_Scotland" title="National Museums Scotland">National Museums Scotland</a> in Edinburgh; the main console itself was rediscovered in July 2014 and is at <a href="/wiki/Rutherford_Appleton_Laboratory" title="Rutherford Appleton Laboratory">Rutherford Appleton Laboratory</a> in Chilton, near <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>. CADCentre's Atlas 2 was decommissioned in late 1976.</p>/n<p></p>/n<div id="toc" class="toc">/n<div class="toctitle">/n<h2>Contents</h2>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a links</span></a></li>/n</ul>/n</div>/n<p></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlas_(computer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Development of MUSE—a name derived from <a href="/wiki/Microsecond" title="Microsecond">microsecond</a> engine—began at Manchester University in 1956. The aim was to build a computer that could operate at processing speeds approaching one&#160;microsecond per instruction, about one&#160;million instructions per second.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The prefix <i>micro-</i> (abbreviation <i>µ</i>) in the SI or <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> denotes a factor of 10<sup>−6</sup> (one millionth).</p>/n<p>At the end of 1958 Ferranti agreed to collaborate with Manchester University on the project, and the computer was shortly afterwards renamed Atlas, with the joint venture under the control of <a href="/wiki/Tom_Kilburn" title="Tom Kilburn">Tom Kilburn</a>. The first Atlas was officially commissioned on 7 December 1962, and was considered at that time to be equivalent to four <a href="/wiki/IBM_7094" class="mw-redirect" title="IBM 7094">IBM 7094s</a><sup id="cite_ref-LavingtonP41_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LavingtonP41-1">[1]</a></sup> and nearly as fast as the <a href="/wiki/IBM_7030" class="mw-redirect" title="IBM 7030">IBM 7030</a> Stretch, then the world's fastest supercomputer.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_description">Technical description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlas_(computer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technical description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardware">Hardware</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlas_(computer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hardware">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>The machine had many innovative features, but the key operating parameters were as follows (the store size relates to the Manchester installation; the others were larger):</p>/n<ul>/n<li><a href="/wiki/48-bit" title="48-bit">48-bit</a> <a href="/wiki/Word_size" class="mw-redirect" title="Word size">word size</a>. A word could hold one floating-point number, one instruction, two 24-bit addresses or signed integers, or eight 6-bit characters.</li>/n<li>24-bit (2&#160;million words, 16 million characters) <a href="/wiki/Address_space" title="Address space">address space</a> that embraced supervisor ('sacred') store, V-store, fixed store and the user store</li>/n<li>16<a href="/wiki/Kibi-" class="mw-redirect" title="Kibi-">K</a> words of <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_core_memory" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnetic core memory">core store</a> (equivalent to 96 <a href="/wiki/Kibibyte" title="Kibibyte">KB</a>), featuring interleaving of odd/even addresses</li>/n<li>8<a href="/wiki/Kibi-" class="mw-redirect" title="Kibi-">K</a> words of read-only memory (referred to as the fixed store). This contained the supervisor and extracode routines.</li>/n<li>96K words of <a href="/wiki/Drum_memory" title="Drum memory">drum store</a> (eqv. to 576 KB), split across four drums but integrated with the core store using <a href="/wiki/Virtual_memory" title="Virtual memory">virtual memory</a> (at that time referred to as 'one-level store') and <a href="/wiki/Paging" title="Paging">paging</a> techniques</li>/n<li>128 high-speed <a href="/wiki/Index_register" title="Index register">index registers</a> (B-lines) that could be used for address modification in the mostly double-modified instructions. The register address space also included special registers such as the extracode operand address and the exponent of the <a href="/wiki/Floating-point" class="mw-redirect" title="Floating-point">floating-point</a> <a href="/wiki/Accumulator_(computing)" title="Accumulator (computing)">accumulator</a>. Three of the 128 registers were <a href="/wiki/Program_counter" title="Program counter">program counter</a> registers: 125 was supervisor (interrupt) control, 126 was extracode control, and 127 was user control. Register 0 always held value 0.</li>/n<li>Capability for the addition of (for the time) sophisticated new <a href="/wiki/Peripheral" title="Peripheral">peripherals</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_tape" title="Magnetic tape">magnetic tape</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Direct_memory_access" title="Direct memory access">direct memory access (DMA)</a> facilities</li>/n<li>Peripheral control through V-store addresses (<a href="/wiki/Memory-mapped_I/O" title="Memory-mapped I/O">memory-mapped I/O</a>), <a href="/wiki/Interrupts" class="mw-redirect" title="Interrupts">interrupts</a> and extracode routines, by reading and writing special wired-in store addresses.</li>/n<li>An associative memory (<a href="/wiki/Content-addressable_memory" title="Content-addressable memory">content-addressable memory</a>) to determine whether the desired virtual memory location was in core store</li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Instruction_pipeline" class="mw-redirect" title="Instruction pipeline">Instruction pipelining</a></li>/n</ul>/n<p>Atlas did not use a synchronous clocking mechanism—it was an <a href="/wiki/Asynchronous_Processor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asynchronous Processor">asynchronous Processor</a>—so performance measurements were not easy but as an example:</p>/n<ul>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Fixed-point_arithmetic" title="Fixed-point arithmetic">Fixed-point</a> <a href="/wiki/Processor_register" title="Processor register">register</a> add&#160;– 1.59 <a href="/wiki/Microsecond" title="Microsecond">microseconds</a></li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Floating-point" class="mw-redirect" title="Floating-point">Floating-point</a> add, no modification&#160;– 1.61 microseconds</li>/n<li>Floating-point add, double modify&#160;– 2.61 microseconds</li>/n<li>Floating-point multiply, double modify&#160;– 4.97 microseconds</li>/n</ul>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extracode">Extracode</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlas_(computer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Extracode">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>One feature of the Atlas was "Extracode", a technique that allowed complex instructions to be implemented in software.</p>/n<p>The uppermost ten <a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bits</a> of a 48-bit Atlas machine instruction denoted which operation should be performed. If the <a href="/wiki/Most_significant_bit" title="Most significant bit">most significant bit</a> was set to zero, this was an ordinary machine instruction executed directly by the hardware. If the uppermost bit was set to one, this was an Extracode and was implemented as a special kind of <a href="/wiki/Subroutine" title="Subroutine">subroutine</a> jump to a location in the fixed store (<a href="/wiki/Read-only_memory" title="Read-only memory">ROM</a>), its <a href="/wiki/Memory_address" title="Memory address">address</a> being determined by the other nine bits. About 250&#160;extracodes were implemented, of the 512 possible.</p>/n<p>Extracode mode had its own <a href="/wiki/Program_counter" title="Program counter">program address counter</a>, and easy access to the instruction operands.</p>/n<p>Extracodes were what would be called <a href="/wiki/Interrupt" title="Interrupt">software interrupt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Trap_(computing)" title="Trap (computing)">trap</a> today. They were used to call mathematical <a href="/wiki/Procedure_(computer_science)" class="mw-redirect" title="Procedure (computer science)">procedures</a> which would have been too inefficient to implement in <a href="/wiki/Computer_hardware" title="Computer hardware">hardware</a>, for example <a href="/wiki/Sine" title="Sine">sine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logarithm" title="Logarithm">logarithm</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Square_root" title="Square root">square root</a>. But about half of the codes were designated as Supervisor functions, which invoked <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a> procedures. Typical examples would be "Print the specified character on the specified stream" or "Read a block of 512 words from logical tape N". Extracodes were the only means by which a <a href="/wiki/Computer_program" title="Computer program">program</a> could communicate with the Supervisor. Other UK machines of the era, such as the <a href="/wiki/Ferranti_Orion" title="Ferranti Orion">Ferranti Orion</a>, had similar mechanisms for calling on the services of their operating systems.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Software">Software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlas_(computer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Atlas pioneered many software concepts still in common use today, including the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Supervisor" title="Atlas Supervisor">Atlas Supervisor</a>, "considered by many to be the first recognisable modern operating system".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<p>One of the first <a href="/wiki/High_level_language" class="mw-redirect" title="High level language">high level languages</a> available on Atlas was named <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Autocode" title="Atlas Autocode">Atlas Autocode</a>, which was contemporary to Algol60 and created specifically to address what Tony Brooker perceived to be some defects in Algol60. The Atlas did however support <a href="/wiki/Algol_60" class="mw-redirect" title="Algol 60">Algol 60</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Fortran" title="Fortran">Fortran</a> and <a href="/wiki/COBOL" title="COBOL">COBOL</a>, and ABL (Atlas machine code). Being a university computer it was patronised by a large number of the student population, who had access to a protected <a href="/wiki/Machine_code" title="Machine code">machine code</a> development environment.</p>/n<p>It also had a programming language called SPG = System Program Generator. At run time an SPG program could compile more program for itself. It could define and use <a href="/wiki/Macro_(computer_science)" title="Macro (computer science)">macros</a>. Its variables were in &lt;angle brackets&gt; and it had a text parser, giving SPG program text a resemblance to <a href="/wiki/Backus%E2%80%93Naur_form" title="Backus–Naur form">Backus–Naur form</a>.</p>/n<h3><span id="Hardware/Software_Integration"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardware.2FSoftware_Integration">Hardware/Software Integration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlas_(computer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hardware/Software Integration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>From the outset, Atlas was conceived as a supercomputer that would include a comprehensive operating system. The hardware included specific features that facilitated the work of the operating system. For example, the extracode routines and the interrupt routines each had dedicated storage, registers and program counters; a <a href="/wiki/Context_switch" title="Context switch">context switch</a> from user mode to extracode mode or executive mode, or from extracode mode to executive mode, was therefore very fast.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
