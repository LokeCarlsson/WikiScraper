Modern magnetic tape is most commonly packaged in cartridges and <a href="/wiki/Compact_Cassette" title="Compact Cassette">cassettes</a>. The device that performs writing or reading of data is a <a href="/wiki/Tape_drive" title="Tape drive">tape drive</a>. <a href="/wiki/Autoloader_(data_storage_device)" class="mw-redirect" title="Autoloader (data storage device)">Autoloaders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tape_library" title="Tape library">tape libraries</a> automate cartridge handling. For example, a common cassette-based format is <a href="/wiki/Linear_Tape-Open" title="Linear Tape-Open">Linear Tape-Open</a>, which comes in a variety of densities and is manufactured by several companies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. id="Open_reels">Open reels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" href="/wiki/File:Largetape.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Largetape.jpg/220px-Largetape.jpg" width="220" height="211" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1590" data-file-height="1524" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Largetape.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/n10.5-inch diameter reel of 9 track tape.</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>Initially, magnetic tape for data storage was wound on 10.5-inch (27&#160;cm) <a href="/wiki/Reel" title="Reel">reels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This de facto standard for large computer systems persisted through the late 1980s. Tape cartridges and cassettes were available as early as the mid-1970s and were frequently used with small computer systems. With the introduction of the IBM 3480 cartridge in 1984, large computer systems started to move away from open reel tapes and towards cartridges.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UNIVAC">UNIVAC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: UNIVAC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Magnetic tape was first used to record computer data in 1951 on the <a href="/wiki/J._Presper_Eckert" title="J. Presper Eckert">Eckert</a>-<a href="/wiki/John_Mauchly" title="John Mauchly">Mauchly</a> <a href="/wiki/UNIVAC_I" title="UNIVAC I">UNIVAC I</a>. The <a href="/wiki/UNISERVO" title="UNISERVO">UNISERVO</a> drive recording medium was a thin metal strip of 0.5-inch (12.7&#160;mm) wide <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>-plated <a href="/wiki/Phosphor_bronze" title="Phosphor bronze">phosphor bronze</a>. Recording density was 128 characters per inch (198 micrometre/character) on eight tracks at a linear speed of 100&#160;in/s (2.54&#160;m/s), yielding a data rate of 12,800 characters per second. Of the eight tracks, six were data, one was a <a href="/wiki/Parity_bit" title="Parity bit">parity track</a>, and one was a clock, or timing track. Making allowance for the empty space between tape blocks, the actual transfer rate was around 7,200 characters per second. A small reel of mylar tape provided separation from the metal tape and the read/write head.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IBM_formats">IBM formats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: IBM formats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p><a href="/wiki/IBM_700/7000_series" title="IBM 700/7000 series">IBM computers from the 1950s</a> used ferrous-oxide coated tape similar to that used in audio recording. IBM's technology soon became the de facto industry standard. Magnetic tape dimensions were 0.5-inch (12.7&#160;mm) wide and wound on removable reels up to 10.5 inches (267&#160;mm) in diameter. Different tape lengths were available with 1,200 feet (370&#160;m) and 2,400 feet (730&#160;m) on mil and one half thickness being somewhat standard.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> During the 1980s, longer tape lengths such as 3,600 feet (1,100&#160;m) became available using a much thinner <a href="/wiki/PET_film_(biaxially_oriented)" class="mw-redirect" title="PET film (biaxially oriented)">PET film</a>. Most tape drives could support a maximum reel size of 10.5 inches (267&#160;mm). <a href="/wiki/Control_Data_Corporation" title="Control Data Corporation">CDC</a> used IBM compatible 1/2 inch magnetic tapes, but also offered a 1 inch wide variant, with 14 tracks (12 data tracks corresponding to the 12 bit word of <a href="/wiki/CDC_6000_series" title="CDC 6000 series">CDC 6000 series</a> peripheral processors, plus two parity bits) in the CDC 626 drive.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p>A so-called <i>mini-reel</i> was common for smaller data sets, such as for software distribution. These were 7-inch (18&#160;cm) reels, often with no fixed length—the tape was sized to fit the amount of data recorded on it as a cost-saving measure.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p>Early IBM tape drives, such as the <a href="/wiki/IBM_727" title="IBM 727">IBM 727</a> and <a href="/wiki/IBM_729" title="IBM 729">IBM 729</a>, were mechanically sophisticated floor-standing drives that used <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_column_(tape_drive)" class="mw-redirect" title="Vacuum column (tape drive)">vacuum columns</a> to buffer long u-shaped loops of tape. Between active control of powerful reel motors and vacuum control of these u-shaped tape loops, fast start and stop of the tape at the tape-to-head interface could be achieved: 1.5&#160;ms from stopped tape to full speed of 112.5 inches per second (2.86&#160;m/s).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The fast acceleration is possible because the tape <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> in the vacuum columns is small; the length of tape buffered in the columns provides time to spin the high <a href="/wiki/Inertia" title="Inertia">inertia</a> reels. When active, the two tape reels thus fed tape into or pulled tape out of the vacuum columns, intermittently spinning in rapid, unsynchronized bursts resulting in visually striking action. Stock shots of such vacuum-column tape drives in motion were widely used to represent "the computer" in movies and television.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p>Early half-inch tape had seven parallel tracks of data along the length of the tape, allowing six-bit characters plus one bit of <a href="/wiki/Parity_bit" title="Parity bit">parity</a> written across the tape. This was known as <a href="/wiki/IBM_7_track" title="IBM 7 track">seven-track tape</a>. With the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/IBM_System/360" title="IBM System/360">IBM System/360</a> mainframe, <a href="/wiki/Nine-track_tape" class="mw-redirect" title="Nine-track tape">nine-track tapes</a> were introduced to support the new 8-bit characters that it used.</p>/n<p>Recording density increased over time. Common seven-track densities started at 200 six-bit characters per inch (CPI), then 556, and finally 800. Nine-track tapes had densities of 800 (using <a href="/wiki/NRZI" class="mw-redirect" title="NRZI">NRZI</a>), then 1600 (using <a href="/wiki/Phase_encoding" class="mw-redirect" title="Phase encoding">PE</a>), and finally 6250 (using <a href="/wiki/Group-coded_recording" class="mw-redirect" title="Group-coded recording">GCR</a>). This translates into about 5 megabytes to 140 megabytes per standard length (2400<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>ft) reel of tape. The end of a file was designated by a special recorded pattern called a <a href="/wiki/Tape_mark" class="mw-redirect" title="Tape mark">tape mark</a>, and end of the recorded data on a tape by two successive tape marks. The physical beginning and end of usable tape was indicated by reflective adhesive strips of aluminum foil placed on the back side.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p>At least partly due to the success of the S/360, and the resultant standardization on 8-bit character codes and byte addressing, nine-track tapes were very widely used throughout the computer industry during the 1970s and 1980s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bytes_per_inch">Bytes per inch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bytes per inch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>/n<p>The term BPI can mean bits per inch of a single track,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>, or when referring to nine-track tape, to <a href="/wiki/Byte" title="Byte">bytes</a> per inch.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Since a byte plus its parity bit occupies all nine tracks, the numeric values of the two measurements are the same.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DEC_format">DEC format</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: DEC format">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p><a href="/wiki/LINCtape" class="mw-redirect" title="LINCtape">LINCtape</a>, and its derivative, <a href="/wiki/DECtape" title="DECtape">DECtape</a>, were variations on this "round tape". They were essentially a personal storage medium. The tape was 0.75 inches (19&#160;mm) wide and featured a fixed formatting track which, unlike standard tape, made it feasible to read and rewrite blocks repeatedly in place. LINCtapes and DECtapes had similar capacity and data transfer rate to the <a href="/wiki/Diskette" class="mw-redirect" title="Diskette">diskettes</a> that displaced them, but their "seek times" were on the order of thirty seconds to a minute.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cartridges_and_cassettes">Cartridges and cassettes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cartridges and cassettes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Quarter-Inch_Cartridges.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Quarter-Inch_Cartridges.jpg/220px-Quarter-Inch_Cartridges.jpg" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Quarter-Inch_Cartridges.jpg/330px-Quarter-Inch_Cartridges.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Quarter-Inch_Cartridges.jpg/440px-Quarter-Inch_Cartridges.jpg 2x" data-file-width="970" data-file-height="661" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Quarter-Inch_Cartridges.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nQuarter-inch cartridges.</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>In the context of magnetic tape, the term <i>cassette</i> usually refers to an enclosure that holds two reels with a single span of magnetic tape. The term <i>cartridge</i> is more generic, but frequently means a single reel of tape in a plastic enclosure.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p>The type of packaging is a large determinant of the load and unload times as well as the length of tape that can be held. A tape drive that uses a single reel cartridge has a takeup reel in the drive while cassettes have the take up reel in the cassette. A tape drive (or "transport" or "deck") uses precisely controlled motors to wind the tape from one reel to the other, passing a read/write head as it does.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:3590Tape.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/3590Tape.JPG/220px-3590Tape.JPG" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/3590Tape.JPG/330px-3590Tape.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/3590Tape.JPG/440px-3590Tape.JPG 2x" data-file-width="784" data-file-height="593" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:3590Tape.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/n<a href="/wiki/IBM_3590" title="IBM 3590">IBM 3590</a> data cartridge can hold up to 10<a href="/wiki/GiB" class="mw-redirect" title="GiB">GiB</a> uncompressed</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>A different type of tape cartridge has a continuous loop of tape wound on a special reel that allows tape to be withdrawn from the center of the reel and then wrapped up around the edge. This type is similar to a cassette in that there is no take-up reel inside the tape drive.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p>The IBM 7340 Hypertape drive, introduced in 1961 used a cassette with a 1 inch (2.5&#160;cm) wide tape capable of holding 2 million six-bit characters per cassette.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p>In the 1970s and 1980s, audio <a href="/wiki/Compact_Cassette#Data_recording" title="Compact Cassette">Compact Cassettes</a> were frequently used as an inexpensive data storage system for <a href="/wiki/Home_computers" class="mw-redirect" title="Home computers">home computers</a>. Compact cassettes were logically, as well as physically, sequential; they had to be rewound and read from the start to load data. Early cartridges were available before personal computers had affordable disk drives, and could be used as <a href="/wiki/Random_access" title="Random access">random access</a> devices, automatically winding and positioning the tape, albeit with access times of many seconds.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p>As of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> most magnetic tape systems used reels fixed inside a cartridge to protect the tape and facilitate handling. Cartridge formats include <a href="/wiki/Digital_Data_Storage" title="Digital Data Storage">DDS/DAT</a>, <a href="/wiki/Digital_Linear_Tape" title="Digital Linear Tape">DLT</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linear_Tape-Open" title="Linear Tape-Open">LTO</a> with capacities in the tens to thousands of gigabytes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_details">Technical details</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Technical details">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tape_width">Tape width</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tape width">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Medium width is the primary classification criterion for tape technologies. Half inch has historically been the most common width of tape for high capacity data storage.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Many other sizes exist and most were developed to either have smaller packaging or higher capacity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording_method">Recording method</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Recording method">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Linear_tape_drive.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d6/Linear_tape_drive.png/220px-Linear_tape_drive.png" width="220" height="99" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d6/Linear_tape_drive.png/330px-Linear_tape_drive.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d6/Linear_tape_drive.png/440px-Linear_tape_drive.png 2x" data-file-width="758" data-file-height="342" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Linear_tape_drive.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nLinear.</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>Recording method is also an important way to classify tape technologies, generally falling into two categories:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Linear">Linear</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Linear">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Linear_serpentine_tape_drive.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Linear_serpentine_tape_drive.png/220px-Linear_serpentine_tape_drive.png" width="220" height="94" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Linear_serpentine_tape_drive.png/330px-Linear_serpentine_tape_drive.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Linear_serpentine_tape_drive.png/440px-Linear_serpentine_tape_drive.png 2x" data-file-width="803" data-file-height="342" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Linear_serpentine_tape_drive.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nLinear serpentine.</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>The <b>linear</b> method arranges data in long parallel tracks that span the length of the tape. Multiple tape heads simultaneously write parallel tape tracks on a single medium. This method was used in early tape drives. It is the simplest recording method, but has the lowest data density.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p>A variation on linear technology is <b>linear serpentine</b> recording, which uses more tracks than tape heads. Each head still writes one track at a time. After making a pass over the whole length of the tape, all heads shift slightly and make another pass in the reverse direction, writing another set of tracks. This procedure is repeated until all tracks have been read or written. By using the linear serpentine method, the tape medium can have many more tracks than read/write heads. Compared to simple linear recording, using the same tape length and the same number of heads, the data storage capacity is substantially higher.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Scanning">Scanning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Scanning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Helical_tape_drive.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Helical_tape_drive.png/220px-Helical_tape_drive.png" width="220" height="100" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Helical_tape_drive.png/330px-Helical_tape_drive.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Helical_tape_drive.png/440px-Helical_tape_drive.png 2x" data-file-width="871" data-file-height="395" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Helical_tape_drive.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nHelical</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>Scanning recording methods write short dense tracks across the width of the tape medium, not along the length. Tape heads are placed on a drum or disk which rapidly rotates while the relatively slowly moving tape passes it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p>An early method used to get a higher data rate than the prevailing linear method was <b>transverse scan</b>. In this method a spinning disk, with the tape heads embedded in the outer edge, is placed perpendicular to the path of the tape. This method is used in <a href="/wiki/Ampex" title="Ampex">Ampex</a>'s DCRsi instrumentation data recorders and the old <a href="/wiki/Ampex" title="Ampex">Ampex</a> <a href="/wiki/Quadruplex_videotape" title="Quadruplex videotape">quadruplex videotape</a> system. Another early method was <b>arcuate scan</b>. In this method, the heads are on the face of a spinning disk which is laid flat against the tape. The path of the tape heads makes an arc.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Helical_scan" title="Helical scan">Helical scan</a></b> recording writes short dense tracks in a <i>diagonal</i> manner. This method is used by virtually all current <a href="/wiki/Videotape" title="Videotape">videotape</a> systems and several data tape formats.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Block_layout">Block layout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Block layout">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation">/n<tr>/n<td class="mbox-image">/n<div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div>/n</td>/n<td class="mbox-text">/n<div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>. <span class="hide-when-compact">Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. However, since the rate at which data is written or read to the tape drive is not deterministic, a tape drive usually has to cope with a difference between the rate at which data goes on and off the tape and the rate at which data is supplied or demanded by its host.</p>/n<p>Various methods have been used alone and in combination to cope with this difference. The tape drive can be stopped, backed up, and restarted (known as <a href="/wiki/Tape_drive#Problems" title="Tape drive">shoe-shining</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (December 2016)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Magnetic_tape_data_storage#Dubious" title="Talk:Magnetic tape data storage">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> because of increased wear of both medium and head). A large memory buffer can be used to queue the data. The host can assist this process by choosing appropriate block sizes to send to the tape drive. There is a complex tradeoff between block size, the size of the data buffer in the record/playback deck, the percentage of tape lost on inter-block gaps, and read/write throughput.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p>Finally modern tape drives offer a speed matching feature, where the drive can dynamically decrease the physical tape speed by as much as 50% to avoid shoe-shining.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p>The size of the inter-block gap is constant, while the size of the data block is based on the number of bytes in the block. Thus a given length tape reel can hold much less data when written with smaller block sizes than with larger.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequential_access_to_data">Sequential access to data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sequential access to data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Tape is characterized by <a href="/wiki/Sequential_access" title="Sequential access">sequential access</a> to data. While tape can provide fast sequential data transfers, it takes tens of seconds to load a cassette and position the tape head to an arbitrary place. In contrast, hard disk technology can perform the equivalent action in tens of milliseconds (3 orders of magnitude faster) and can be thought of as offering <a href="/wiki/Random_access" title="Random access">random access</a> to data.</p>/n<p>Logical filesystems require data and metadata to be stored on the data storage medium. Storing metadata in one place and data in another requires lots of slow repositioning activity on most tape systems. As a result, most tape systems use a very trivial filesystem in which files are addressed by number not by filename. <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">Metadata</a> such as file name or modification time is typically not stored at all. <a href="/wiki/Tape_label" title="Tape label">Tape labels</a> store such metadata, and they are used for interchanging data between systems. <a href="/wiki/File_archiver" title="File archiver">File archiver</a> and <a href="/wiki/Backup" title="Backup">backup</a> tools have been created to pack multiple files along with the related metadata into a single 'tape file'. Serpentine tape drives (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Quarter-inch_cartridge" title="Quarter-inch cartridge">QIC</a>) can improve access time by switching to the appropriate track; tape partitions were used for directory information.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Linear_Tape_File_System" title="Linear Tape File System">Linear Tape File System</a> is a method of storing file metadata on a separate part of the tape. This makes it possible to <a href="/wiki/Copy_and_paste" class="mw-redirect" title="Copy and paste">copy and paste</a> files or directories to a tape as if it were just like another disk, but does not change the fundamental sequential access nature of tape.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Access_time">Access time</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Access time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Tape has quite a long latency for random accesses since the deck must wind an average of one-third the tape length to move from one arbitrary data block to another. Most tape systems attempt to alleviate the intrinsic long latency, either using indexing, where a separate lookup table (<i>tape directory</i>) is maintained which gives the physical tape location for a given data block number (a must for serpentine drives), or by marking blocks with a <a href="/wiki/Tape_mark" class="mw-redirect" title="Tape mark">tape mark</a> that can be detected while winding the tape at high speed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_compression">Data compression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Data compression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Most tape drives now include some kind of <a href="/wiki/Lossless_data_compression" class="mw-redirect" title="Lossless data compression">lossless data compression</a>. There are several algorithms which provide similar results: LZ (most), IDRC (Exabyte), ALDC (IBM, QIC) and DLZ1 (DLT). Embedded in tape drive hardware, these compress a relatively small buffer of data at a time, so cannot achieve extremely high compression even of highly redundant data. A ratio of 2:1 is typical, with some vendors claiming 2.6:1 or 3:1. The ratio actually obtained with real data is often less than the stated figure; the compression ratio cannot be relied upon when specifying the capacity of equipment, e.g., a drive claiming a compressed capacity of 500GB may not be adequate to back up 500GB of real data. Data that is already stored efficiently may not allow <i>any</i> significant compression; a sparse database may offer much larger factors. Software compression can achieve much better results with sparse data, but uses the host computer's processor, and can slow the backup if it is unable to compress as fast as the data is written.</p>/n<p>The compression algorithms used in low-end products are not the most effective known today, and better results can usually be obtained by turning off hardware compression AND using software compression (and encryption if desired) instead.</p>/n<p>Plain text, raw images, and database files (<a href="/wiki/Text_file" title="Text file">TXT</a>, <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a>, <a href="/wiki/BMP_file_format" title="BMP file format">BMP</a>, <a href="/wiki/DBASE" class="mw-redirect" title="DBASE">DBF</a>, etc.) typically compress much better than other types of data stored on computer systems. In contrast, encrypted data and pre-compressed data (<a href="/wiki/Pretty_Good_Privacy" title="Pretty Good Privacy">PGP</a>, <a href="/wiki/ZIP_(file_format)" class="mw-redirect" title="ZIP (file format)">ZIP</a>, <a href="/wiki/JPEG" title="JPEG">JPEG</a>, <a href="/wiki/MPEG" class="mw-redirect" title="MPEG">MPEG</a>, <a href="/wiki/MP3" title="MP3">MP3</a>, etc.) would normally <i>increase</i> in size,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> if data compression was applied. In some cases this data expansion could be as much as 15%.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Encryption">Encryption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Encryption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Standards exist to <a href="/wiki/Encryption" title="Encryption">encrypt</a> tapes.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Encryption is used so that even if a tape is stolen, the thieves cannot use the data on the tape. Key management is crucial to maintain security. Encryption is more efficient if done after compression, as encrypted data cannot be compressed effectively. Some enterprise tape drives can quickly encrypt data. Symmetric streaming encryption algorithms can also provide high performance.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cartridge_memory_and_self-identification">Cartridge memory and self-identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Cartridge memory and self-identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Some tape cartridges, notably <a href="/wiki/Linear_Tape-Open#Cartridge_memory" title="Linear Tape-Open">LTO cartridges</a>, have small, associated data storage chips built into the cartridges that record metadata about the tape, such as the type of encoding, size of the storage, dates and other information. It's also common for tape cartridges to have bar codes on their labels in order to assist an automated tape library.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Viability">Viability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_tape_data_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Viability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Medium and large-sized data centers deploy both tape and disk formats.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>/n<p>The costs of disk storage have decreased faster than that of tapes. Until about the end of the twentieth century, prices and capacities allowed backing up a desktop hard drive to tape more cheaply and more compactly than backing up to an additional external or removable drive. Drive prices offer capacities of hundreds to thousands of megabytes on relatively inexpensive machines. Backing up to an external USB drive became cheaper, and the drive more compact, than tape for a non-networked machine used by a business or serious user.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>/n<p>As a basic comparison, <a href="/wiki/Mainframe_computer" title="Mainframe computer">mainframe</a>-class tape drives, such as Oracle's Sun StorageTek T10000B, are priced<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (November 2011)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> at approximately <a href="/wiki/US$" class="mw-redirect" title="US$">US$</a>37,000 each, excluding tape libraries. (IBM's TS1130 is also representative of this storage class.) At any single moment in time each T10000C tape drive can read or write (or both read and write)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to one tape cartridge which can contain up to 5TB of uncompressed data. Real-world sequential data transfer speeds are high (sustained 240MB/second for the T10000C and 160MB/second for the TS1130) compared to disk. However, PC-class hard disks are priced below $200 for 3TB.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> One mainframe-class hard disk still has a much lower price than one mainframe-class tape drive, so the economics might favor disk.</p>/n<p>However, the key difference is that tape drives can exchange their magnetic media (the cartridges) frequently, while the magnetic media installed inside each hard disk is fixed and cannot be swapped. (The drives themselves could be moved if installed in swappable caddies at extra cost, with extra cost hot-swappable infrastructure.) Mainframe-class tape drives are almost always installed in robotic <a href="/wiki/Tape_libraries" class="mw-redirect" title="Tape libraries">tape libraries</a> which are often quite large and can hold thousands of cartridges. The StorageTek SL8500 library is one representative example. The <i>smallest</i> SL8500 library holds up to 1,448 tape cartridges, for 1.4 Petabytes of online uncompressed storage. An equivalent amount of PC-class hard disk storage would be priced at $100,000 or less for the drives. The tape library would likely deliver a higher sustained sequential write speed, the media would be more rugged (for off-site storage), the media would meet or exceed long-term archival storage requirements (for reliable retrieval decades into the future), and the data center power and cooling requirements would be considerably lower.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The economics of this comparison are more complicated than a single-spindle versus tape drive comparison.</p>/n<p>Whether tape's characteristics versus disk are useful or not will depend on the particular data center and its data storage requirements. What has tended to happen in recent years is that the amount of data has grown exponentially<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="exponentially is a precise statement; Moore's law probably fits better? Don't have reference (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, with both disk (especially) and tape participating in the growth. In the early twenty-first century <a href="/wiki/Solid_state_storage" class="mw-redirect" title="Solid state storage">solid state storage</a> encroached on disk's previous near-monopoly in random access non-volatile data storage, while disk pushed into tape's territory to some extent, particularly in situations where sequential data access is only a relatively small part of a particular data center's storage requirements.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="High_density_magnetic_media">High density magnetic media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
