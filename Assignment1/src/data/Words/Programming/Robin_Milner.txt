Robin Gorell Milner</b> (13 January 1934 – 20 March 2010), known as <b>Robin Milner</b> or <b>A. J. R. G. Milner</b>, was a British <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Turing_Award" title="Turing Award">Turing Award</a> winner.<sup id="cite_ref-times_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">7</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>/n</ul>/n</div>/n<p></p>/n<h2><span id="Life,_education_and_career"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Life.2C_education_and_career">Life, education and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robin_Milner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life, education and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Milner was born in <a href="/wiki/Yealmpton" title="Yealmpton">Yealmpton</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> into a military family. He was awarded a scholarship to <a href="/wiki/Eton_College" title="Eton College">Eton College</a> in 1947, and subsequently served in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Engineers" title="Royal Engineers">Royal Engineers</a>, attaining the rank of Second Lieutenant. He then enrolled at <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="King's College, Cambridge">King's College, Cambridge</a>, graduating in 1957. Milner first worked as a schoolteacher then as a <a href="/wiki/Programmer" title="Programmer">programmer</a> at <a href="/wiki/Ferranti" title="Ferranti">Ferranti</a>, before entering academia at <a href="/wiki/City_University,_London" class="mw-redirect" title="City University, London">City University, London</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Swansea_University" title="Swansea University">Swansea University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>, and from 1973 at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a>, where he was a co-founder of the <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_for_Foundations_of_Computer_Science" title="Laboratory for Foundations of Computer Science">Laboratory for Foundations of Computer Science</a> (LFCS). He returned to <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge</a> as the head of the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_Computer_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="Cambridge University Computer Laboratory">Computer Laboratory</a> in 1995 from which he eventually stepped down, although he was still at the laboratory. From 2009, Milner was a SICSA Advanced Research Fellow and held (part-time) the Chair of Computer Science at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a>.</p>/n<p>Milner died of a <a href="/wiki/Heart_attack" class="mw-redirect" title="Heart attack">heart attack</a> on 20 March 2010 in Cambridge.<sup id="cite_ref-times_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> His wife, Lucy, died shortly before him.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributions">Contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robin_Milner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Milner is generally regarded as having made three major contributions to <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>. He developed <a href="/wiki/Logic_for_Computable_Functions" title="Logic for Computable Functions">LCF</a>, one of the first tools for <a href="/wiki/Automated_theorem_proving" title="Automated theorem proving">automated theorem proving</a>. The language he developed for LCF, <a href="/wiki/ML_programming_language" class="mw-redirect" title="ML programming language">ML</a>, was the first language with polymorphic <a href="/wiki/Type_inference" title="Type inference">type inference</a> and type-safe <a href="/wiki/Exception_handling" title="Exception handling">exception handling</a>. In a very different area, Milner also developed a theoretical framework for analyzing <a href="/wiki/Concurrent_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Concurrent systems">concurrent systems</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Calculus_of_communicating_systems" title="Calculus of communicating systems">calculus of communicating systems</a> (CCS), and its successor, the <a href="/wiki/Pi-calculus" class="mw-redirect" title="Pi-calculus">pi-calculus</a>. At the time of his death, he was working on <a href="/wiki/Bigraphs" class="mw-redirect" title="Bigraphs">bigraphs</a>, a formalism for <a href="/wiki/Ubiquitous_computing" title="Ubiquitous computing">ubiquitous computing</a> subsuming CCS and the pi-calculus.<sup id="cite_ref-bigraphs_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bigraphs-11">[11]</a></sup> He is also credited for rediscovering the <a href="/wiki/Hindley%E2%80%93Milner_type_system" title="Hindley–Milner type system">Hindley–Milner type system</a>.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors_and_awards">Honors and awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robin_Milner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Honors and awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>He was made a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> and a Distinguished Fellow of the British Computer Society in 1988. Milner received the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">ACM</a> <a href="/wiki/Turing_Award" title="Turing Award">Turing Award</a> in 1991. In 1994 he was inducted as a <a href="/wiki/Fellow" title="Fellow">Fellow</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">ACM</a>. In 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Edinburgh" title="Royal Society of Edinburgh">Royal Society of Edinburgh</a> awarded Milner with a Royal Medal for his "bringing about public benefits on a global scale". In 2008, he was elected a Foreign Associate of the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Engineering" title="National Academy of Engineering">National Academy of Engineering</a> for "fundamental contributions to computer science, including the development of LCF, ML, CCS, and the pi-calculus." <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber"
