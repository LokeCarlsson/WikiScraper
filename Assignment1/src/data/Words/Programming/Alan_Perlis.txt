style="display:none">/n<td colspan="2"></td>/n</tr>/n</table>/n<p><b>Alan Jay Perlis</b> (April 1, 1922– February 7, 1990) was an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a> and professor at <a href="/wiki/Purdue_University" title="Purdue University">Purdue University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>. He is best known for his pioneering work in <a href="/wiki/Programming_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Programming languages">programming languages</a> and was the first recipient of the <a href="/wiki/Turing_Award" title="Turing Award">Turing Award</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>/n<p></p>/n<div id="toc" class="toc">/n<div class="toctitle">/n<h2>Contents</h2>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Biography"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Biography</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Publications"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Publications</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>/n</ul>/n</div>/n<p></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alan_Perlis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Perlis was born to a <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> family in <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</a>. In 1943, he received his <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">bachelor's degree</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Institute_of_Technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Carnegie Institute of Technology">Carnegie Institute of Technology</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a>). During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, he served in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Army" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a>, where he became interested in mathematics. He then earned both a <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">master's degree</a> (1949) and a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a> (1950) in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> at <a href="/wiki/MIT" class="mw-redirect" title="MIT">MIT</a>. His doctoral dissertation was titled "On <a href="/wiki/Integral_equation" title="Integral equation">Integral Equations</a>, Their Solution by Iteration and <a href="/wiki/Analytic_continuation" title="Analytic continuation">Analytic Continuation</a>".</p>/n<p>In 1952, he participated in <a href="/wiki/Project_Whirlwind" class="mw-redirect" title="Project Whirlwind">Project Whirlwind</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He joined the faculty at <a href="/wiki/Purdue_University" title="Purdue University">Purdue University</a> and then moved to the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Institute_of_Technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Carnegie Institute of Technology">Carnegie Institute of Technology</a> in 1956. He was chair of mathematics and then the first head of the Computer Science Department. He was elected president of the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">Association for Computing Machinery</a> in 1962.</p>/n<p>He was awarded the Turing Award in 1966, according to the citation, <i>for his influence in the area of advanced programming techniques and <a href="/wiki/Compiler" title="Compiler">compiler</a> construction</i>. This is a reference to the work he had done as a member of the team that developed the <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_programming_language" class="mw-redirect" title="ALGOL programming language">ALGOL programming language</a>.</p>/n<p>In 1971, Perlis moved to <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> to become the chair of Computer Science and hold the Eugene Higgins chair. Perlis was elected to the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Engineering" title="National Academy of Engineering">National Academy of Engineering</a> in 1977.</p>/n<p>In 1982, he wrote an article, <i><a href="/wiki/Epigrams_on_Programming" title="Epigrams on Programming">Epigrams on Programming</a></i>, for <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">ACM</a>'s SIGPLAN journal, describing in one-sentence distillations many of the things he had learned about programming over his career. The <a href="/wiki/Epigram" title="Epigram">epigrams</a> have been widely quoted.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He remained at Yale until his death in 1990.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publications">Publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alan_Perlis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Publications, a selection:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>/n<ul>/n<li>1965. <i>An introductory course in computer programming</i>.
