style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:EDSAC_(19).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/EDSAC_%2819%29.jpg/350px-EDSAC_%2819%29.jpg" width="350" href="/wiki/File:EDSAC_(19).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nEDSAC</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>The <b>electronic delay storage automatic calculator</b> (<b>EDSAC</b>) was an early British computer.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Inspired by <a href="/wiki/John_von_Neumann" title="John von Neumann">John von Neumann</a>'s seminal <i><a href="/wiki/First_Draft_of_a_Report_on_the_EDVAC" title="First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC">First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC</a></i>, the machine was constructed by <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Wilkes" title="Maurice Wilkes">Maurice Wilkes</a> and his team at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge_Mathematical_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Cambridge Mathematical Laboratory">University of Cambridge Mathematical Laboratory</a> in England. EDSAC was the second electronic digital <a href="/wiki/Von_Neumann_architecture" title="Von Neumann architecture">stored-program</a> computer to go into regular service.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Later the project was supported by <a href="/wiki/J._Lyons_and_Co." title="J. Lyons and Co.">J. Lyons &amp; Co. Ltd.</a>, a British firm, who were rewarded with the first commercially applied computer, <a href="/wiki/LEO_(computer)" title="LEO (computer)">LEO I</a>, based on the EDSAC design. Work on EDSAC started during 1947,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and it ran its first programs on 6 May 1949, when it calculated a table of squares<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and a list of <a href="/wiki/Prime_number" title="Prime number">prime numbers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> EDSAC 1 was finally shut down on 11 July 1958, having been superseded by <a href="/wiki/EDSAC_2" title="EDSAC 2">EDSAC 2</a>, which remained in use until 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<p></p>/n<div id="toc" class="toc">/n<div class="toctitle">/n<h2>Contents</h2>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li class="toclevel-1 It used <a href="/wiki/Delay_line_memory" title="Delay line memory">mercury delay lines</a> for memory, and <a href="/wiki/Derating" title="Derating">derated</a> <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tubes</a> for logic. Cycle time was 1.5 ms for all ordinary instructions, 6 ms for multiplication. Input was via five-hole <a href="/wiki/Punched_tape" title="Punched tape">punched tape</a> and output was via a <a href="/wiki/Teleprinter" title="Teleprinter">teleprinter</a>.</p>/n<p>Initially registers were limited to an <a href="/wiki/Accumulator_(computing)" title="Accumulator (computing)">accumulator</a> and a multiplier register. In 1953, <a href="/wiki/David_Wheeler_(computer_scientist)" class="mw-redirect" title="David Wheeler (computer scientist)">David Wheeler</a>, returning from a stay at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana-Champaign" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign">University of Illinois</a>, designed an <a href="/wiki/Index_register" title="Index register">index register</a> as an extension to the original EDSAC hardware.</p>/n<p>A magnetic tape drive was added in 1952 but never worked sufficiently well to be of real use.<sup id="cite_ref-autogeneratedcl_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogeneratedcl-7">[7]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Until 1952, the available main memory (instructions and data) was only 512 18-bit words, and there was no backing store.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The delay lines (or "tanks") were arranged in two batteries providing 512 words each. The second battery came into operation in 1952.<sup id="cite_ref-autogeneratedcl_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogeneratedcl-7">[7]</a></sup></p>/n<p>The full 1024-word delay line store was not available until 1955 or early 1956,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> limiting programs to about 800 words until then.</p>/n<p>John Lindley (diploma student 1958–1959) mentioned "the incredible difficulty we had ever to produce a single correct piece of paper tape with the crude and unreliable home-made punching, printing and verifying gear available in the late 50s".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Memory_and_instructions">Memory and instructions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_delay_storage_automatic_calculator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Memory and instructions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:EDSAC_(12).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/EDSAC_%2812%29.jpg/220px-EDSAC_%2812%29.jpg" width="220" height="177" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/EDSAC_%2812%29.jpg/330px-EDSAC_%2812%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/EDSAC_%2812%29.jpg/440px-EDSAC_%2812%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1878" data-file-height="1511" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:EDSAC_(12).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/n<a href="/wiki/Maurice_Wilkes" title="Maurice Wilkes">Maurice Wilkes</a> and Bill Renwick in front of the complete EDSAC</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>The EDSAC's main memory consisted of 1024 locations, though only 512 locations were initially installed. Each contained 18 bits, but the topmost bit was always unavailable due to timing problems, so only 17 bits were used. An instruction consisted of a five-bit instruction code, one spare bit, a ten bit operand (usually a memory address), and a length bit to control whether the instruction used a 17-bit or a 35-bit operand (two consecutive words, <a href="/wiki/Endianness" title="Endianness">little-endian</a>). All instruction codes were by design represented by one mnemonic letter, so that the <i>Add</i> instruction, for example, used the EDSAC character code for the letter A.</p>/n<p>Internally, the EDSAC used <a href="/wiki/Two%27s_complement" title="Two's complement">two's complement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Binary_number" title="Binary number">binary</a> numbers. Numbers were either 17 bits (one word) or 35 bits (two words) long. Unusually, the <a href="/wiki/Multiplication_ALU" class="mw-redirect" title="Multiplication ALU">multiplier</a> was designed to treat numbers as <a href="/wiki/Fixed-point_arithmetic" title="Fixed-point arithmetic">fixed-point</a> fractions in the range −1 ≤ <i>x</i> &lt; 1, i.e. the binary point was immediately to the right of the sign. The <a href="/wiki/Accumulator_(computing)" title="Accumulator (computing)">accumulator</a> could hold 71 bits, including the sign, allowing two long (35-bit) numbers to be multiplied without losing any precision.</p>/n<p>The instructions available were:</p>/n<ul>/n<li>Add</li>/n<li>Subtract</li>/n<li>Multiply-and-add</li>/n<li>AND-and-add (called "Collate")</li>/n<li>Shift Left</li>/n<li>Arithmetic shift Right</li>/n<li>Load multiplier register</li>/n<li>Store (and optionally clear) accumulator</li>/n<li>Conditional Goto</li>/n<li>Read Input tape</li>/n<li>Print character</li>/n<li>Round accumulator</li>/n<li>No-op</li>/n<li>Stop.</li>/n</ul>/n<p>There was no division instruction (but various division subroutines were supplied) and no way to directly load a number into the accumulator (a "sTore and zero accumulator" instruction followed by an "Add" instruction were necessary for this). There was no unconditional jump instruction, nor was there a procedure call instruction&#160;- it had not yet been invented.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="System_software">System software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_delay_storage_automatic_calculator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: System software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>The <i>initial orders</i> were hard-wired on a set of <a href="/wiki/Stepping_switch" title="Stepping switch">uniselector switches</a> and loaded into the low words of memory at startup. By May 1949, the initial orders provided a primitive relocating <a href="/wiki/Assembly_language_assembler" class="mw-redirect" title="Assembly language assembler">assembler</a> taking advantage of the mnemonic design described above, all in 31 words. This was the world's first assembler, and arguably the start of the global software industry. There is a simulation of EDSAC available and a full description of the initial orders and first programs.</p>/n<p>The machine was used by other members of the University to solve real problems, and many early techniques were developed that are now included in operating systems. Users prepared their programs by punching them (in assembler) onto a paper tape. They soon became good at being able to hold the paper tape up to the light and read back the codes. When a program was ready it was hung on a length of line strung up near the paper tape reader. The machine operators, who were present during the day, selected the next tape from the line and loaded it into EDSAC. This is of course well known today as job queues. If it printed something then the tape and the printout were returned to the user, otherwise they were informed at which memory location it had stopped. Debuggers were some time away, but a CRT screen could be set to display the contents of a particular piece of memory. This was used to see if a number was converging, for example. A loudspeaker was connected to the accumulator's sign bit; experienced users knew healthy and unhealthy sounds of programs, particularly programs 'hung' in a loop. After office hours certain "Authorised Users" were allowed to run the machine for themselves, which went on late into the night until a valve blew – which usually happened according to one such user.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Programming_technique">Programming technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_delay_storage_automatic_calculator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Programming technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:EDSAC_(23).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/EDSAC_%2823%29.jpg/220px-EDSAC_%2823%29.jpg" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/EDSAC_%2823%29.jpg/330px-EDSAC_%2823%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/EDSAC_%2823%29.jpg/440px-EDSAC_%2823%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="838" data-file-height="633" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:EDSAC_(23).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nEDSAC monitoring desk</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>The early programmers had to make use of techniques frowned upon today&#160;— especially altering the code. As there was no index register until much later, the only way of accessing an array was to alter which memory location a particular instruction was referencing.</p>/n<p><a href="/wiki/David_Wheeler_(computer_scientist)" class="mw-redirect" title="David Wheeler (computer scientist)">David Wheeler</a>, who earned the world's first Computer Science PhD working on the project, is credited with inventing the concept of a subroutine. Users wrote programs that called a routine by jumping to the start of the subroutine with the return address (i.e. the location-plus-one of the jump itself) in the accumulator (a Wheeler jump). By convention the subroutine expected this and the first thing it did was to modify its concluding jump instruction to that return address. Multiple and nested subroutines could be called so long as the user knew the length of each one in order to calculate the location to jump to; <a href="/wiki/Recursion_(computer_science)" title="Recursion (computer science)">recursive calls</a> were forbidden. The user then copied the code for the subroutine from a master tape onto their own tape following the end of their own program.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_software">Application software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_delay_storage_automatic_calculator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Application software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>The subroutine concept led to the availability of a substantial subroutine library. By 1951, 87 subroutines in the following categories were available for general use: <a href="/wiki/Floating_point" class="mw-redirect" title="Floating point">floating point arithmetic</a>; arithmetic operations on <a href="/wiki/Complex_number" title="Complex number">complex numbers</a>; checking; division; <a href="/wiki/Exponentiation" title="Exponentiation">exponentiation</a>; routines relating to functions; <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equations</a>; special functions; <a href="/wiki/Power_series" title="Power series">power series</a>; <a href="/wiki/Logarithm" title="Logarithm">logarithms</a>; miscellaneous; print and layout; <a href="/wiki/Numerical_integration" title="Numerical integration">quadrature</a>; read (input); <i>n</i>th root; <a href="/wiki/Trigonometric_functions" title="Trigonometric functions">trigonometric functions</a>; counting operations (simulating <a href="/wiki/Do_while_loop" title="Do while loop">repeat until loops</a>, <a href="/wiki/While_loop" title="While loop">while loops</a> and <a href="/wiki/For_loop" title="For loop">for loops</a>); <a href="/wiki/Probability_vector" title="Probability vector">vectors</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics)" title="Matrix (mathematics)">matrices</a>.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications_of_EDSAC">Applications of EDSAC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_delay_storage_automatic_calculator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Applications of EDSAC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<ul>/n<li>In 1950, M. V. Wilkes and Wheeler used EDSAC to solve a differential equation relating to gene frequencies in a paper by <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Fisher" title="Ronald Fisher">Ronald Fisher</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This represents the first use of a computer for a problem in the field of biology.</li>/n<li>In 1951, Miller and Wheeler used the machine to discover a 79-digit prime<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> – the <a href="/wiki/Largest_known_prime" class="mw-redirect" title="Largest known prime">largest known</a> at the time.</li>/n<li>In 1952, <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Douglas" title="Sandy Douglas">Sandy Douglas</a> developed <i><a href="/wiki/OXO" title="OXO">OXO</a></i>, a version of <a href="/wiki/Tic-tac-toe" title="Tic-tac-toe">noughts and crosses</a> (tic-tac-toe) for the EDSAC, with graphical output to a VCR97 6" <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">cathode ray tube</a>. This may well have been the world's <a href="/wiki/First_video_game" class="mw-redirect" title="First video game">first video game</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>/n<li>In the early 1960s <a href="/wiki/Peter_Swinnerton-Dyer" title="Peter Swinnerton-Dyer">Peter Swinnerton-Dyer</a> used the EDSAC computer to calculate the number of points modulo <i>p</i> (denoted by <i>N<sub>p</sub></i>) for a large number of primes <i>p</i> on elliptic curves whose rank was known. Based on these numerical results <a href="#CITEREFBirchSwinnerton-Dyer1965">Birch &amp; Swinnerton-Dyer (1965)</a> conjectured that <i>N<sub>p</sub></i> for a curve <i>E</i> with rank <i>r</i> obeys an asymptotic law, the <a href="/wiki/Birch_and_Swinnerton-Dyer_conjecture" title="Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture">Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture</a>, considered one of the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Prize_Problems" title="Millennium Prize Problems">top unsolved problems in mathematics</a> as of 2016.</li>/n</ul>/n<blockquote class="templatequote">/n<p>The “brain” [computer] may one day come down to our level [of the common people] and help with our income-tax and book-keeping calculations. But this is speculation and there is no sign of it so far.</p>/n<div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>British newspaper <i>The Star</i> in a June 1949 news article about the EDSAC computer, long before the era of the personal computers.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></cite></div>/n</blockquote>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_developments">Further developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_delay_storage_automatic_calculator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Further developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>EDSAC's successor, <a href="/wiki/EDSAC_2" title="EDSAC 2">EDSAC 2</a>, was commissioned in 1958.</p>/n<p>In 1961, an EDSAC 2 version of <a href="/wiki/Autocode" title="Autocode">Autocode</a>, an <a href="/wiki/ALGOL" title="ALGOL">ALGOL</a>-like high-level programming language for scientists and engineers, was developed by <a href="/wiki/David_Hartley_(computer_scientist)" title="David Hartley (computer scientist)">David Hartley</a>.</p>/n<p>In the mid-1960s, a successor to the EDSAC 2 was planned, but the move was instead made to the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(computer)" title="Titan (computer)">Titan</a>, a prototype Atlas 2 developed from the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Computer" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlas Computer">Atlas Computer</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_University_of_Manchester" title="Victoria University of Manchester">University of Manchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ferranti" title="Ferranti">Ferranti</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plessey" title="Plessey">Plessey</a>.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="EDSAC_Replica_Project">EDSAC Replica Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_delay_storage_automatic_calculator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: EDSAC Replica Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>On 13 January 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Computer_Conservation_Society" title="Computer Conservation Society">Computer Conservation Society</a> announced that it planned to build a working replica of EDSAC, at the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Computing" class="mw-redirect" title="National Museum of Computing">National Museum of Computing</a> (TNMoC) in <a href="/wiki/Bletchley_Park" title="Bletchley Park">Bletchley Park</a> supervised by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Herbert" title="Andrew Herbert">Andrew Herbert</a>, who studied under Maurice Wilkes.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The first parts of the recreation were switched on in November 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The ongoing project is open to visitors of the museum.
