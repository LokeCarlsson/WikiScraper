(<b>Universal Automatic Computer I</b>) was the first commercial computer produced in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It was designed principally by <a href="/wiki/J._Presper_Eckert" title="J. Presper Eckert">J. Presper Eckert</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Mauchly" title="John Mauchly">John Mauchly</a>, the inventors of the <a href="/wiki/ENIAC" title="ENIAC">ENIAC</a>. Design work was started by their company, <a href="/wiki/Eckert%E2%80%93Mauchly_Computer_Corporation" title="Eckert–Mauchly Computer Corporation">Eckert–Mauchly Computer Corporation</a> (EMCC), and was completed after the company had been acquired by <a href="/wiki/Remington_Rand" title="Remington Rand">Remington Rand</a> (which later became part of <a href="/wiki/Sperry_Corporation" title="Sperry Corporation">Sperry</a>, now <a href="/wiki/Unisys" title="Unisys">Unisys</a>). In the years before successor models of the UNIVAC I appeared, the machine was simply known as "the <b>UNIVAC</b>".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>/n<p>The first Univac was accepted by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a> on March 31, 1951, and was dedicated on June 14 that year.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The fifth machine (built for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Atomic_Energy_Commission" title="United States Atomic Energy Commission">U.S. Atomic Energy Commission</a>) was used by <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> to predict the result of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1952" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. presidential election, 1952">1952 presidential election</a>. With a sample of just 1% of the voting population it famously predicted an <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Eisenhower</a> landslide while the conventional wisdom favored Stevenson.<sup id="cite_ref-Lukoff_1979_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lukoff_1979-5">[5]</a></sup></p>/n<p></p>/n<div id="toc" class="toc">/n<div class="toctitle">/n<h2>Contents</h2>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a As such the UNIVAC competed directly against <a href="/wiki/Punched_card" title="Punched card">punch-card</a> machines, though the UNIVAC originally could neither read nor punch cards. That shortcoming hindered sales to companies concerned about the high cost of manually converting large quantities of existing data stored on cards. This was corrected by adding offline card processing equipment, the <a href="/wiki/UNIVAC_Card_to_Tape_converter" title="UNIVAC Card to Tape converter">UNIVAC Card to Tape converter</a> and the <a href="/wiki/UNIVAC_Tape_to_Card_converter" title="UNIVAC Tape to Card converter">UNIVAC Tape to Card converter</a>, to transfer data between cards and UNIVAC magnetic tapes.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, the early market share of the UNIVAC I was lower than the Remington Rand Company wished.</p>/n<p>To promote sales, the company joined with CBS to have UNIVAC I predict the result of the 1952 Presidential election. UNIVAC I predicted Eisenhower would have a landslide victory over <a href="/wiki/Adlai_Stevenson_II" title="Adlai Stevenson II">Adlai Stevenson</a> whom the pollsters favored. The CBS crew was so certain that UNIVAC was wrong that they pretended it was not working. As the election continued and it became clear it was correct all along, the announcer admitted their sleight of hand and the machine became famous. The result was a greater public awareness of computing technology,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and from then on computerized predictions were a must-have part of election night broadcasts.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Installations">Installations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UNIVAC_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Installations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>The first contracts were with government agencies such as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">Census Bureau</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a>, and the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Army_Map_Service" title="Army Map Service">Army Map Service</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Contracts were also signed by the <a href="/wiki/ACNielsen" class="mw-redirect" title="ACNielsen">ACNielsen Company</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Prudential_Financial" title="Prudential Financial">Prudential Insurance Company</a>. Following the sale of Eckert–Mauchly Computer Corporation to Remington Rand, due to the cost overruns on the project, Remington Rand convinced Nielsen and Prudential to cancel their contracts.</p>/n<p>The first sale, to the Census Bureau, was marked with a formal ceremony on March 31, 1951, at the Eckert–Mauchly Division's factory at 3747 Ridge Avenue, Philadelphia. The machine was not actually shipped until the following December, because, as the sole fully set-up model, it was needed for demonstration purposes, and the company was apprehensive about the difficulties of dismantling, transporting, and reassembling the delicate machine.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> As a result, the first installation was with the second computer, delivered to the Pentagon in June 1952.</p>/n<p><b>UNIVAC installations, 1951–1954</b><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>/n<table class="wikitable">/n<tr>/n<th>Date</th>/n<th>Customer</th>/n<th>Comments</th>/n</tr>/n<tr>/n<td>1951</td>/n<td><a href="/wiki/U.S._Census_Bureau" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Census Bureau">U.S. Census Bureau</a>, Suitland, MD</td>/n<td>Not shipped until 1952</td>/n</tr>/n<tr>/n<td>1952</td>/n<td><a href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a></td>/n<td><a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">Pentagon</a>, Arlington, VA</td>/n</tr>/n<tr>/n<td>1952</td>/n<td><a href="/wiki/National_Geospatial-Intelligence_Agency" title="National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency">U.S. Army Map Service</a></td>/n<td>Washington, DC. However Sperry Rand donated UNIVAC I systems to <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> (1956), the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a> (1957), and <a href="/wiki/Case_Institute_of_Technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Case Institute of Technology">Case Institute of Technology</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleveland, Ohio">Cleveland, Ohio</a> (1957); the UNIVAC I at Case was still operable in 1965 but had been supplanted by a UNIVAC 1107.</p>/n<p>A few UNIVAC I systems stayed in service long after they were made obsolete by advancing technology. Sperry Rand itself used two systems in <a href="/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York" title="Buffalo, New York">Buffalo, New York</a> until 1968. The Central Complex alone (i.e. the processor and memory unit) was 4.3 m by 2.4 m by 2.6 m high. The complete system occupied more than 35.5 m² (382&#160;ft²) of floor space.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_memory_details">Main memory details</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UNIVAC_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Main memory details">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>The main memory consisted of 1000 <a href="/wiki/Word_(data_type)" class="mw-redirect" title="Word (data type)">words</a> of 12 characters. When representing numbers, they were written as 11 <a href="/wiki/Decimal" title="Decimal">decimal</a> digits plus <a href="/wiki/Signed_number_representations" title="Signed number representations">sign</a>. The 1000 words of memory consisted of 100 channels of 10-word <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> <a href="/wiki/Delay_line_memory" title="Delay line memory">delay line</a> registers. The <a href="/wiki/Input/output" title="Input/output">input/output</a> buffers were 60 words each, consisting of 12 channels of 10-word mercury delay line <a href="/wiki/Register_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Register (computing)">registers</a>. There are six channels of 10-word mercury delay line registers as spares. With modified circuitry, seven more channels control the temperature of the seven mercury tanks, and one more channel is used for the 10 word "Y" register. The total of 126 mercury channels is contained in the seven mercury tanks mounted on the backs of sections MT, MV, MX, NT, NV, NX, and GV. Each mercury tank is divided into 18 mercury channels.</p>/n<p>Each 10-word mercury delay line channel is made up of three sections:</p>/n<ol>/n<li>A channel in a column of mercury, with receiving and transmitting <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> piezo-electric <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystals</a> mounted at opposite ends.</li>/n<li>An intermediate frequency chassis, connected to the receiving crystal, containing amplifiers, detector, and compensating delay, mounted on the shell of the mercury tank.</li>/n<li>A recirculation chassis, containing cathode follower, pulse former and retimer, modulator, which drives the transmitting crystal, and input, clear, and memory-switch gates, mounted in the sections adjacent to the mercury tanks.</li>/n</ol>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instructions_and_data">Instructions and data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UNIVAC_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Instructions and data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p><a href="/wiki/Instruction_set" class="mw-redirect" title="Instruction set">Instructions</a> were six <a href="/wiki/Alphanumeric" title="Alphanumeric">alphanumeric</a> characters, packed two instructions per word. The addition time was 525 <a href="/wiki/Microseconds" class="mw-redirect" title="Microseconds">microseconds</a> and the multiplication time was 2150 microseconds. A non-standard modification called "Overdrive" did exist, that allowed for three four-character instructions per word under some circumstances. (Ingerman's simulator for the UNIVAC, referenced below, also makes this modification available.)</p>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:UNIVAC_I_Interior.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/UNIVAC_I_Interior.jpg/220px-UNIVAC_I_Interior.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/UNIVAC_I_Interior.jpg/330px-UNIVAC_I_Interior.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/UNIVAC_I_Interior.jpg/440px-UNIVAC_I_Interior.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3183" data-file-height="2384" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:UNIVAC_I_Interior.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nInternal view of UNIVAC I</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>Digits were represented internally using <a href="/wiki/Excess-3" title="Excess-3">excess-3</a> ("XS3") <a href="/wiki/Binary_coded_decimal" class="mw-redirect" title="Binary coded decimal">binary coded decimal (BCD)</a> arithmetic with six bits per digit using the same value as the digits of the alphanumeric character set (and one <a href="/wiki/Parity_bit" title="Parity bit">parity bit</a> per digit for <a href="/wiki/Redundancy_check" class="mw-redirect" title="Redundancy check">error checking</a>), allowing 11-digit <a href="/wiki/Signed_number_representations#Sign-and-magnitude" title="Signed number representations">signed magnitude</a> numbers. But with the exception of one or two machine instructions, UNIVAC was considered by programmers to be a decimal machine, not a binary machine, and the binary representation of the characters was irrelevant. If a non-digit character was encountered in a position during an arithmetic operation the machine passed it unchanged to the output, and any carry into the non-digit was lost. (Note, however, that a peculiarity of UNIVAC I's addition/subtraction circuitry was that the "ignore", space, and minus characters were occasionally treated as numeric, with values of –3, –2, and –1 respectively, and the apostrophe, ampersand, and left parenthesis were occasionally treated as numeric, with values 10, 11, and 12.)</p>/n<h3><span id="Input/output"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Input.2Foutput">Input/output</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UNIVAC_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Input/output">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Besides the operator's console, the only I/O devices connected to the UNIVAC I were up to 10 <a href="/wiki/UNISERVO" title="UNISERVO">UNISERVO</a> tape drives, a <a href="/wiki/Remington_Standard" class="mw-redirect" title="Remington Standard">Remington Standard</a> <a href="/wiki/Electric_typewriter" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric typewriter">electric typewriter</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Tektronix" title="Tektronix">Tektronix</a> <a href="/wiki/Oscilloscope" title="Oscilloscope">oscilloscope</a>. The UNISERVO was the first commercial computer tape drive commercially sold. It used data density 128 bits per inch (with real transfer rate 7,200 characters per second) on magnetically plated phosphor bronze tapes. The UNISERVO could also read and write UNITYPER created tapes at 20 bits per inch. The <a href="/wiki/UNITYPER" title="UNITYPER">UNITYPER</a> was an offline typewriter to tape device, used by programmers and for minor data editing. Backward and forward tape read and write operations were possible on the UNIVAC and were fully overlapped with instruction execution, permitting high system throughput in typical sort/merge data processing applications. Large volumes of data could be submitted as input via magnetic tapes created on offline card to tape system and made as output via a separate offline tape to printer system. The operators console had three columns of decimal coded switches that allowed any of the 1000 memory locations to be displayed on the oscilloscope. Since the mercury delay line memory stored bits in a serial format, a programmer or operator could monitor any memory location continuously and with sufficient patience, decode its contents as displayed on the scope. The on-line typewriter was typically used for announcing program breakpoints, checkpoints, and for memory dumps.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UNIVAC_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>A typical UNIVAC I installation had several ancillary devices. There were, typically: a printer that read a magnetic tape and printed output on continuous-form paper; a card-to-tape converter, that read <a href="/wiki/Punched_card" title="Punched card">punched cards</a> and recorded their images on magnetic tape; and a tape-to-card converter, that read a magnetic tape and produced punched cards. There was no UNIVAC provided operating system. Operators loaded on a UNISERVO a program tape which could be loaded automatically by processor logic. The appropriate source and output data tapes would be mounted and the program started. Results tapes then went to the offline printer or typically for data processing into short term storage to be updated with the next set of data produced on the offline card to tape unit. The mercury delay line memory tank temperature was very closely controlled as the speed of sound in mercury varies with temperature. In the event of a power failure, many hours could elapse before the temperature stabilized.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reliability">Reliability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UNIVAC_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reliability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Eckert and Mauchly were uncertain about the reliability of digital logic circuits and little was known at the time. The UNIVAC I was designed with parallel computation circuits and result comparison. In practice, only failing components yielded comparison faults as their circuit designs were very reliable. Tricks were used to manage the reliability of tubes. Prior to use in the machine, large lots of the predominant tube type 25L6 were burned in and carefully tested. Often half of a production lot would be thrown away. Technicians installed a tested and burned in tube in an easily diagnosed location such as the memory recirculate amplifiers. Then when aged further, this "golden" tube was sent to stock to be used in a difficult to diagnose logic position. It took about 30 minutes to turn on the computer as all filament power supplies were stepped up to operating value over that time, to reduce in-rush current and thermal stress on the tubes. As a result, uptimes (MTBF) of many days to weeks were obtained on the processor. The UNISERVO did not have vacuum columns, rather had springs and strings to buffer tape from the reels to the capstan. These were a frequent source of failures.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
