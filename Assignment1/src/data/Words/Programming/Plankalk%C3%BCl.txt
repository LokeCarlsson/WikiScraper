href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></td>/n</tr>/n</table>/n<p><b>Plankalkül</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈplaːnkalkyːl]</a></span>, "Plan Calculus") is a <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming language</a> designed for engineering purposes by <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Zuse" title="Konrad Zuse">Konrad Zuse</a> between 1942 and 1945. It was the first <a href="/wiki/High-level_programming_language" title="High-level programming language">high-level</a> (<a href="/wiki/Von_Neumann_programming_languages" title="Von Neumann programming languages">non-von Neumann</a>) <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming language</a> to be designed for a computer.</p>/n<p>"<i>Kalkül</i>" means <a href="/wiki/Formal_system" title="Formal system">formal system</a> – the <a href="/wiki/Hilbert-style_deduction_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Hilbert-style deduction system">Hilbert-style deduction system</a> is for example originally called "<i>Hilbert-Kalkül</i>", so Plankalkül means "formal system for planning".</p>/n<p></p>/n<div id="toc" class="toc">/n<div class="toctitle">/n<h2>Contents</h2>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#History"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">History</span></a></li>/n<li href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>/n</ul>/n</div>/n<p></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plankalk%C3%BCl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Notes survive with scribblings about such a plan calculation dating back to May 1939 and in 1942 Zuse began writing a <a href="/wiki/Computer_chess" title="Computer chess">computer chess</a> program in Plankalkül.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1944 Zuse met with the German logician and philosopher <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Scholz" title="Heinrich Scholz">Heinrich Scholz</a> and they discussed Zuse's Plankalkül. In March 1945 Scholz personally expressed his deep appreciation for Zuse's utilization of the logical calculus.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Plankalkül was not published at that time owing to a combination of factors such as conditions in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and postwar Germany and his efforts to commercialise the <a href="/wiki/Z3_(computer)" title="Z3 (computer)">Z3</a> computer and its successors. By 1946, Zuse had written a book on the subject<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> but this remained unpublished.</p>/n<p>Only in 1948 Zuse published a paper about the Plankalkül in the "Archiv der Mathematik" but still did not attract much feedback – for a long time to come programming a computer would only be thought of as programming with <a href="/wiki/Machine_code" title="Machine code">machine code</a>. In the same year Zuse introduced his programming language at the Annual Meeting of the <a href="/wiki/GAMM" class="mw-redirect" title="GAMM">GAMM</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>/n<p>The Plankalkül was eventually more comprehensively published in 1972 and the first compiler for it was implemented in 1975 in a dissertation by Joachim Hohmann.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Other independent implementations followed in 1998 and then in 2000 by the <a href="/wiki/Free_University_of_Berlin" title="Free University of Berlin">Free University of Berlin</a>.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plankalk%C3%BCl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Plankalkül has drawn comparisons to <a href="/wiki/APL_programming_language" class="mw-redirect" title="APL programming language">APL</a> and <a href="/wiki/Relational_algebra" title="Relational algebra">relational algebra</a>. It includes assignment statements, <a href="/wiki/Subroutine" title="Subroutine">subroutines</a>, conditional statements, iteration, <a href="/wiki/Floating_point" class="mw-redirect" title="Floating point">floating point</a> arithmetic, arrays, hierarchical record structures, assertions, exception handling, and other advanced features such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goal-directed_execution&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Goal-directed execution (page does not exist)">goal-directed execution</a>. The Plankalkül provides a data structure called <i>generalized <a href="/wiki/Graph_(abstract_data_type)" title="Graph (abstract data type)">graph</a></i> (<i>verallgemeinerter Graph</i>), which can be used to represent geometrical structures.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Plankalkül shared an idiosyncratic notation using multiple lines with <a href="/wiki/Gottlob_Frege" title="Gottlob Frege">Frege</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Begriffsschrift" title="Begriffsschrift">Begriffsschrift</a></i> of 1879 (dealing with <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_logic" title="Mathematical logic">mathematical logic</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2009)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p>Some features of the Plankalkül:<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>/n<ul>/n<li>only local variables</li>/n<li>functions do not support recursion</li>/n<li>only supports call by value</li>/n<li>composite types are arrays and tuples</li>/n<li>contains conditional expressions</li>/n<li>contains a for loop and a while loop</li>/n<li>no goto</li>/n</ul>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plankalk%C3%BCl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Zuse called a single program a <i>Rechenplan</i> (i.e. <i>computation plan</i>), and in 1944 he already envisioned a device that should read and then automatically translate a mathematical formulation of a program into machine readable punched film stock – a device which he called <i>Planfertigungsgerät</i> (i.e. <i>plan construction device</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Example">Example</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plankalk%C3%BCl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Example">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>The original notation was two dimensional. For a later implementation in the 1990s, a linear notation was developed.</p>/n<p>The following example shows a program (in a linear transcription), which calculates the maximum of three variables by calling the function <code>max3</code>:</p>/n<pre>/nP1 max3 (V0[:8.0],V1[:8.0],V2[:8.0]) → R0[:8.0]/nmax(V0[:8.0],V1[:8.0]) → Z1[:8.0]/nmax(Z1[:8.0],V2[:8.0]) → R0[:8.0]/nEND/nP2 max (V0[:8.0],V1[:8.0]) → R0[:8.0]/nV0[:8.0] → Z1[:8.0]/n(Z1[:8.0] &lt; V1[:8.0]) → V1[:8.0] → Z1[:8.0]/nZ1[:8.0] → R0[:8.0]/nEND/n</pre>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quotations">Quotations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plankalk%C3%BCl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Quotations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>In a lecture in 1957 Zuse mentioned his hope that the Plankalkül "after some time as a <a href="/wiki/Sleeping_Beauty" title="Sleeping Beauty">Sleeping Beauty</a>, will yet come to life."</p>/n<p><a href="/wiki/Heinz_Rutishauser" title="Heinz Rutishauser">Heinz Rutishauser</a>, one of the founders of <a href="/wiki/ALGOL" title="ALGOL">ALGOL</a>:</p>/n<blockquote class="templatequote">/n<p>The very first attempt to devise an algorithmic language was undertaken in 1948 by K. Zuse. His notation was quite general, but the proposal never attained the consideration it deserved.</p>/n</blockquote>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plankalk%C3%BCl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<ul>/n<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_programming_languages" title="History of programming languages">History of programming languages</a></li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_programming_languages" title="Timeline of programming languages">Timeline of programming languages</a></li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_programming_languages" title="List of programming languages">List of programming languages</a></li>/n</ul>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plankalk%C3%BCl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">/n<div class="mw-references-wrap">/n<ol class="references">/n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation book">Rojas, Raúl; Hashagen, Ulf (2002). <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=nDWPW9uwZPAC&amp;pg=PA292&amp;dq=algol-68+konrad+zuse"><i>The First Computers: History and Architectures</i></a>.
