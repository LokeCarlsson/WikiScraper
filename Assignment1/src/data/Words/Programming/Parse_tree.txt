class="mw-body-content">/n									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>/n								<div id="contentSub"></div>/n												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">/n					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>/n				</div>/n				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>parse tree</b> or <b>parsing tree</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or <b>derivation tree</b> or <b>concrete syntax tree</b> is an ordered, rooted <a href="/wiki/Tree_(data_structure)" title="Tree (data structure)">tree</a> that represents the <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntactic</a> structure of a <a href="/wiki/String_(computer_science)" title="String (computer science)">string</a> according to some <a href="/wiki/Context-free_grammar" title="Context-free grammar">context-free grammar</a>. The term <i>parse tree</i> itself is used primarily in <a href="/wiki/Computational_linguistics" title="Computational linguistics">computational linguistics</a>; in theoretical syntax, the term <i>syntax tree</i> is more common.</p>/n<p>Parse trees concretely<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> reflect the syntax of the input language, making them distinct from the <a href="/wiki/Abstract_syntax_tree" title="Abstract syntax tree">abstract syntax trees</a> used in computer programming. Unlike Reed-Kellogg <a href="/wiki/Sentence_diagram" title="Sentence diagram">sentence diagrams</a> used for teaching grammar, parse trees do not use distinct symbol shapes for different types of <a href="/wiki/Constituent_(linguistics)" title="Constituent (linguistics)">constituents</a>.</p>/n<p>Parse trees are usually constructed based on either the constituency relation of constituency grammars (<a href="/wiki/Phrase_structure_grammar" title="Phrase structure grammar">phrase structure grammars</a>) or the dependency relation of <a href="/wiki/Dependency_grammar" title="Dependency grammar">dependency grammars</a>. Parse trees may be generated for <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence (linguistics)">sentences</a> in <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">natural languages</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_processing" title="Natural language processing">natural language processing</a>), as well as during <a href="/wiki/Compiler" title="Compiler">processing</a> of computer languages, such as <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a>.</p>/n<p>A related concept is that of <b>phrase marker</b> or <b>P-marker</b>, as used in <a href="/wiki/Transformational_generative_grammar" class="mw-redirect" title="Transformational generative grammar">transformational generative grammar</a>. A phrase marker is a linguistic expression marked as to its phrase structure. This may be presented in the form of a tree, or as a bracketed expression. Phrase markers are generated by applying <a href="/wiki/Phrase_structure_rules" title="Phrase structure rules">phrase structure rules</a>, and themselves are subject to further transformational rules.</p>/n<p></p>/n<div id="toc" class="toc">/n<div class="toctitle">/n<h2>Contents</h2>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Constituency-based_parse_trees"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Constituency-based parse trees</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Dependency-based_parse_trees"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Dependency-based parse trees</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#Phrase_markers"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">Phrase markers</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#Notes"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">Notes</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-7"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">7</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>/n</ul>/n</div>/n<p></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Constituency-based_parse_trees">Constituency-based parse trees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parse_tree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Constituency-based parse trees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>The constituency-based parse trees of constituency grammars (= <a href="/wiki/Phrase_structure_grammar" title="Phrase structure grammar">phrase structure grammars</a>) distinguish between terminal and non-terminal nodes. The <a href="/wiki/Interior_node" class="mw-redirect" title="Interior node">interior nodes</a> are labeled by <a href="/wiki/Nonterminal" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonterminal">non-terminal</a> categories of the grammar, while the <a href="/wiki/Leaf_node" class="mw-redirect" title="Leaf node">leaf nodes</a> are labeled by <a href="/wiki/Terminal_symbol" class="mw-redirect" title="Terminal symbol">terminal</a> categories. The image below represents a constituency-based parse tree; it shows the syntactic structure of the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> sentence <i>John hit the ball</i>:</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>/n<dl>/n<dd>/n<dl>/n<dd><a href="/wiki/File:Parse_tree_1.jpg" class="image" title="Parse tree PSG"><img alt="Parse tree PSG" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/54/Parse_tree_1.jpg" width="338" height="169" data-file-width="338" data-file-height="169" /></a></dd>/n</dl>/n</dd>/n</dl>/n</dd>/n</dl>/n<p>The parse tree is the entire structure, starting from S and ending in each of the leaf nodes (<i>John</i>, <i>hit</i>, <i>the</i>, <i>ball</i>). The following abbreviations are used in the tree:</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>/n<dl>/n<dd>/n<ul>/n<li>S for <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence (linguistics)">sentence</a>, the top-level structure in this example</li>/n</ul>/n</dd>/n</dl>/n</dd>/n</dl>/n<dl>/n<dd>/n<dl>/n<dd>/n<ul>/n<li>NP for <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">noun phrase</a>. The first (leftmost) NP, a single noun "John", serves as the <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">subject</a> of the sentence. The second one is the <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">object</a> of the sentence.</li>/n</ul>/n</dd>/n</dl>/n</dd>/n</dl>/n<dl>/n<dd>/n<dl>/n<dd>/n<ul>/n<li>VP for <a href="/wiki/Verb_phrase" title="Verb phrase">verb phrase</a>, which serves as the <a href="/wiki/Predicate_(grammar)" title="Predicate (grammar)">predicate</a></li>/n</ul>/n</dd>/n</dl>/n</dd>/n</dl>/n<dl>/n<dd>/n<dl>/n<dd>/n<ul>/n<li>V for <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verb</a>. In this case, it's a <a href="/wiki/Transitive_verb" title="Transitive verb">transitive verb</a> <i>hit</i>.</li>/n</ul>/n</dd>/n</dl>/n</dd>/n</dl>/n<dl>/n<dd>/n<dl>/n<dd>/n<ul>/n<li>D for <a href="/wiki/Determiner_(class)" class="mw-redirect" title="Determiner (class)">determiner</a>, in this instance the <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">definite article</a> "the"</li>/n</ul>/n</dd>/n</dl>/n</dd>/n</dl>/n<dl>/n<dd>/n<dl>/n<dd>/n<ul>/n<li>N for <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">noun</a></li>/n</ul>/n</dd>/n</dl>/n</dd>/n</dl>/n<p>Each node in the tree is either a <i>root</i> node, a <i>branch</i> node, or a <i>leaf</i> node.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A root node is a node that doesn't have any branches on top of it. Within a sentence, there is only ever one root node. A branch node is a mother node that connects to two or more daughter nodes. A leaf node, however, is a terminal node that does not dominate other nodes in the tree. S is the root node, NP and VP are branch nodes, and <i>John</i> (N), <i>hit</i> (V), <i>the</i> (D), and <i>ball</i> (N) are all leaf nodes. The leaves are the lexical tokens of the sentence.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> A mother node is one that has at least one other node linked by a branch under it. In the example, S is a parent of both N and VP. A daughter node is one that has at least one node directly above it to which it is linked by a branch of a tree. From the example, <i>hit</i> is a daughter node of V. The terms <i>parent</i> and <i>child</i> are also sometimes used for this relationship.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dependency-based_parse_trees">Dependency-based parse trees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parse_tree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dependency-based parse trees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>The dependency-based parse trees of <a href="/wiki/Dependency_grammar" title="Dependency grammar">dependency grammars</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> see all nodes as terminal, which means they do not acknowledge the distinction between terminal and non-terminal categories. They are simpler on average than constituency-based parse trees because they contain fewer nodes. The dependency-based parse tree for the example sentence above is as follows:</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>/n<dl>/n<dd>/n<dl>/n<dd><a href="/wiki/File:Parse2.jpg" class="image" title="Parse tree DG"><img alt="Parse tree DG" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8c/Parse2.jpg" width="344" height="133" data-file-width="344" data-file-height="133" /></a></dd>/n</dl>/n</dd>/n</dl>/n</dd>/n</dl>/n<p>This parse tree lacks the phrasal categories (S, VP, and NP) seen in the constituency-based counterpart above. Like the constituency-based tree, <a href="/wiki/Constituent_(linguistics)" title="Constituent (linguistics)">constituent</a> structure is acknowledged. Any complete sub-tree of the tree is a constituent. Thus this dependency-based parse tree acknowledges the subject noun <i>John</i> and the object noun phrase <i>the ball</i> as constituents just like the constituency-based parse tree does.</p>/n<p>The constituency vs. dependency distinction is far-reaching. Whether the additional syntactic structure associated with constituency-based parse trees is necessary or beneficial is a matter of debate.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phrase_markers">Phrase markers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parse_tree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Phrase markers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Phrase markers, or P-markers, were introduced in early <a href="/wiki/Transformational_generative_grammar" class="mw-redirect" title="Transformational generative grammar">transformational generative grammar</a>, as developed by <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a> and others. A phrase marker representing the <a href="/wiki/Deep_structure" class="mw-redirect" title="Deep structure">deep structure</a> of a sentence is generated by applying <a href="/wiki/Phrase_structure_rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Phrase structure rule">phrase structure rules</a>; this may then undergo further transformations.
