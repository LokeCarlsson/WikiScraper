class="mw-jump">/n					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>/n				</div>/n				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Michael Fredric Sipser</b> (born September 17, 1954) is a <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_computer_scientist" class="mw-redirect" title="Theoretical computer scientist">theoretical computer scientist</a> who has made early contributions to <a href="/wiki/Computational_complexity_theory" title="Computational complexity theory">computational complexity theory</a>. He is a <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">professor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Applied_Mathematics" class="mw-redirect" title="Applied Mathematics">Applied Mathematics</a> and Dean of Science at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>.</p>/n<p></p>/n<div id="toc" class="toc">/n<div class="toctitle">/n<h2>Contents</h2>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Biography"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Biography</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Scientific_career"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Scientific career</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#Notable_books"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">Notable books</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#Personal_life"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">Personal life</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>/n</ul>/n</div>/n<p></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Sipser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Sipser was born and raised in Brooklyn, New York and moved to Oswego, New York when he was 12 years old. He earned his BA in mathematics from <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> in 1974 and his PhD in engineering from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California at Berkeley</a> in 1980 under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Blum" title="Manuel Blum">Manuel Blum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MITNEWS06052014_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MITNEWS06052014-1">[1]</a></sup></p>/n<p>He joined MIT's <a href="/wiki/MIT_Computer_Science_and_Artificial_Intelligence_Laboratory" title="MIT Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Laboratory">Laboratory for Computer Science</a> as a research associate in 1979 and became an MIT professor the following year. From 2004 until 2014, he served as head of the MIT Mathematics department. He was appointed Dean of the <a href="/wiki/School_of_Science,_Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" class="mw-redirect" title="School of Science, Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT School of Science</a> in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He is a fellow of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2015 he was elected as a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Mathematical_Society" title="American Mathematical Society">American Mathematical Society</a> "for contributions to complexity theory and for leadership and service to the mathematical community."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_career">Scientific career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Sipser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scientific career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Sipser specializes in <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computational_complexity_theory" title="Computational complexity theory">complexity theory</a>, specifically efficient error correcting codes, interactive proof systems, randomness, quantum computation, and establishing the inherent computational difficulty of problems. He introduced the method of probabilistic restriction for proving super-polynomial lower bounds on <a href="/wiki/Circuit_complexity" title="Circuit complexity">circuit complexity</a> in a paper joint with Merrick Furst and <a href="/wiki/James_B._Saxe" title="James B. Saxe">James B. Saxe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Their result was later improved to be an exponential lower bound by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Yao" title="Andrew Yao">Andrew Yao</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johan_H%C3%A5stad" title="Johan Håstad">Johan Håstad</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<p>In an early <a href="/wiki/Derandomization" class="mw-redirect" title="Derandomization">derandomization</a> theorem, Sipser showed that <a href="/wiki/BPP_(complexity)" title="BPP (complexity)">BPP</a> is contained in the <a href="/wiki/Polynomial_hierarchy" title="Polynomial hierarchy">polynomial hierarchy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> subsequently improved by Peter Gács and Clemens Lautemann to form what is now known as the <a href="/wiki/Sipser-Gacs-Lautemann_theorem" class="mw-redirect" title="Sipser-Gacs-Lautemann theorem">Sipser-Gàcs-Lautemann theorem</a>. Sipser also established a connection between <a href="/wiki/Expander_graph" title="Expander graph">expander graphs</a> and derandomization.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He and his PhD student <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Spielman" title="Daniel Spielman">Daniel Spielman</a> introduced <a href="/wiki/Expander_code" title="Expander code">expander codes</a>, an application of expander graphs.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> With fellow graduate student David Lichtenstein, Sipser proved that <a href="/wiki/Go_(game)" title="Go (game)">Go</a> is <a href="/wiki/PSPACE" title="PSPACE">PSPACE</a> hard.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>/n<p>In quantum computation theory, he introduced the <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_quantum_computation" title="Adiabatic quantum computation">adiabatic algorithm</a> jointly with <a href="/wiki/Edward_Farhi" title="Edward Farhi">Edward Farhi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Goldstone" title="Jeffrey Goldstone">Jeffrey Goldstone</a>, and Samuel Gutmann.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Sipser has long been interested in the <a href="/wiki/P_versus_NP_problem" title="P versus NP problem">P versus NP problem</a>. In 1975, he wagered an ounce of gold with <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Adleman" title="Leonard Adleman">Leonard Adleman</a> that the problem would be solved with a proof that P≠NP by the end of the 20th century. Sipser sent Adleman an <a href="/wiki/American_gold_eagle" class="mw-redirect" title="American gold eagle">American gold eagle coin</a> in 2000 because the problem remained (and remains) unsolved.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_books">Notable books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Sipser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notable books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Sipser is the author of <i><a href="/wiki/Introduction_to_the_Theory_of_Computation" title="Introduction to the Theory of Computation">Introduction to the Theory of Computation</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> a textbook for <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_computer_science" title="Theoretical computer science">theoretical computer science</a>.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Sipser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Sipser lives in Cambridge, Massachusetts with his wife, Ina, and has two children: a daughter, Rachel, who graduated from New York University, and a younger son, Aaron, who is an undergraduate at MIT.<sup id="cite_ref-MITNEWS06052014_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MITNEWS06052014-1">[1]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span
