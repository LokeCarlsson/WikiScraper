<b>software bug</b> is an error, flaw, <a href="/wiki/Failure" title="Failure">failure</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fault_(technology)" title="Fault (technology)">fault</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Computer_program" title="Computer program">computer program</a> or <a href="/wiki/Software_system" title="Software system">system</a> that causes it to produce an incorrect or unexpected result, or to behave in unintended ways. The process of fixing bugs is termed "<a href="/wiki/Debugging" title="Debugging">debugging</a>" and often uses formal techniques or tools to pinpoint bugs, and since the 1950s, some computer systems have been designed to also deter, detect or auto-correct various computer bugs during operations.</p>/n<p>Most bugs arise from mistakes and errors made in either a program's <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a> or its <a href="/wiki/Software_architecture" title="Software architecture">design</a>, or in components and <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a> used by such programs. A few are caused by <a href="/wiki/Compiler" title="Compiler">compilers</a> producing incorrect code. A program that contains a large number of bugs, and/or bugs that seriously interfere with its functionality, is said to be <i>buggy</i> (defective). Bugs can trigger errors that may have <a href="/wiki/Ripple_effect" title="Ripple effect">ripple effects</a>. Bugs may have subtle effects or cause the program to <a href="/wiki/Crash_(computing)" title="Crash (computing)">crash</a> or <a href="/wiki/Freeze_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Freeze (computing)">freeze</a> the computer. Other bugs qualify as <a href="/wiki/Security_bugs" class="mw-redirect" title="Security bugs">security bugs</a> and might, for example, enable a <a href="/wiki/Black_hat_hacking" class="mw-redirect" title="Black hat hacking">malicious user</a> to bypass <a href="/wiki/Access_control" title="Access control">access controls</a> in order to <a href="/wiki/Privilege_escalation" title="Privilege escalation">obtain unauthorized privileges</a>.</p>/n<p>Some software bugs have been linked to disasters. Bugs in code that controls the <a href="/wiki/Therac-25" title="Therac-25">Therac-25</a> <a href="/wiki/Radiation_therapy" title="Radiation therapy">radiation therapy</a> machine were directly responsible for patient deaths in the 1980s. In 1996, the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a>'s US$1&#160;billion <a href="/wiki/Ariane_5_Flight_501" class="mw-redirect" title="Ariane 5 Flight 501">prototype</a> <a href="/wiki/Ariane_5" title="Ariane 5">Ariane 5</a> rocket had to be destroyed less than a minute after launch due to a bug in the on-board guidance computer program. In June 1994, a Royal Air Force <a href="/wiki/CH-47_Chinook" class="mw-redirect" title="CH-47 Chinook">Chinook</a> helicopter <a href="/wiki/1994_Scotland_RAF_Chinook_crash" title="1994 Scotland RAF Chinook crash">crashed</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Mull_of_Kintyre" title="Mull of Kintyre">Mull of Kintyre</a>, killing 29. This was initially dismissed as pilot error, but an investigation by <i><a href="/wiki/Computer_Weekly" title="Computer Weekly">Computer Weekly</a></i> convinced a <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> inquiry that it may have been caused by a software bug in the aircraft's <a href="/wiki/FADEC" title="FADEC">engine-control computer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>/n<p>In 2002, a study commissioned by the US <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Commerce" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Commerce">Department of Commerce</a>'s <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> concluded that "software bugs, or errors, are so prevalent and so detrimental that they cost the US economy an estimated $59&#160;billion annually, or about 0.6 percent of the gross domestic product".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>/n<p></p>/n<div id="toc" class="toc">/n<div class="toctitle">/n<h2>Contents</h2>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>The term "bug" to describe defects has been a part of engineering jargon since the 1870s and predates electronic computers and computer software; it may have originally been used in hardware engineering to describe mechanical malfunctions. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a> wrote the following words in a letter to an associate in 1878:</p>/n<blockquote class="templatequote">/n<p>It has been just so in all of my inventions. The first step is an intuition, and comes with a burst, then difficulties arise—this thing gives out and [it is] then that "Bugs"—as such little faults and difficulties are called—show themselves and months of intense watching, study and labor are requisite before commercial success or failure is certainly reached.<sup id="cite_ref-Hughes1989_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes1989-3">[3]</a></sup></p>/n</blockquote>/n<p>The Middle English word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bugge#Noun" class="extiw" title="wikt:bugge">bugge</a></i> is the basis for the terms "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bugbear#Noun" class="extiw" title="wikt:bugbear">bugbear</a>" and "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bug-a-boo#Noun" class="extiw" title="wikt:bug-a-boo">bugaboo</a>" as terms used for a monster.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Baffle_Ball" title="Baffle Ball">Baffle Ball</a>, the first mechanical <a href="/wiki/Pinball" title="Pinball">pinball</a> game, was advertised as being "free of bugs" in 1931.<sup id="cite_ref-Baffle_Ball_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baffle_Ball-5">[5]</a></sup> Problems with military gear during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> were referred to as bugs (or <a href="/wiki/Glitch" title="Glitch">glitches</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-life1942062925_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-life1942062925-6">[6]</a></sup> In a book published in 1942, <a href="/wiki/Louise_Dickinson_Rich" title="Louise Dickinson Rich">Louise Dickinson Rich</a>, speaking of a powered <a href="/wiki/Ice_cutting" title="Ice cutting">ice cutting</a> machine, said, "Ice sawing was suspended until the creator could be brought in to take the bugs out of his darling."<sup id="cite_ref-oclc_405243_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oclc_405243-7">[7]</a></sup></p>/n<p><a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a> used the term "bug" to relate to issues with a robot in his short story "Catch That Rabbit", published in 1944, and included in his well-known collection of short stories <i><a href="/wiki/I,_Robot" title="I, Robot">I, Robot</a></i>. From page 1 of "Catch That Rabbit": "U.S. Robots had to get the bugs out of the multiple robot, and there were plenty of bugs, and there are always at least half a dozen bugs left for the field-testing."</p>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:H96566k.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/H96566k.jpg/250px-H96566k.jpg" width="250" height="197" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/H96566k.jpg/375px-H96566k.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/H96566k.jpg/500px-H96566k.jpg 2x" data-file-width="740" data-file-height="583" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:H96566k.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nA page from the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Mark_II" title="Harvard Mark II">Harvard Mark II</a> electromechanical computer's log, featuring a dead moth that was removed from the device</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>The term "bug" was used in an account by computer pioneer <a href="/wiki/Grace_Hopper" title="Grace Hopper">Grace Hopper</a>, who publicized the cause of a malfunction in an early electromechanical computer.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A typical version of the story is:</p>/n<blockquote class="templatequote">/n<p>In 1946, when Hopper was released from active duty, she joined the Harvard Faculty at the Computation Laboratory where she continued her work on the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Mark_II" title="Harvard Mark II">Mark II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Mark_III" title="Harvard Mark III">Mark III</a>. Operators traced an error in the Mark II to a <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moth</a> trapped in a relay, coining the term <i>bug</i>. This bug was carefully removed and taped to the log book. Stemming from the first bug, today we call errors or glitches in a program a <i>bug</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>/n</blockquote>/n<p>Hopper did not find the bug, as she readily acknowledged. The date in the log book was September 9, 1947.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-si-bug_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-si-bug-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The operators who found it, including William "Bill" Burke, later of the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Surface_Warfare_Center_Dahlgren_Division" title="Naval Surface Warfare Center Dahlgren Division">Naval Weapons Laboratory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dahlgren,_Virginia" title="Dahlgren, Virginia">Dahlgren, Virginia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> were familiar with the engineering term and amusedly kept the insect with the notation "First actual case of bug being found." Hopper loved to recount the story.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This log book, complete with attached moth, is part of the collection of the Smithsonian <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_American_History" title="National Museum of American History">National Museum of American History</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-si-bug_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-si-bug-11">[11]</a></sup></p>/n<p>The related term "<a href="/wiki/Debug" class="mw-redirect" title="Debug">debug</a>" also appears to predate its usage in computing: the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s etymology of the word contains an attestation from 1945, in the context of aircraft engines.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>The concept that software might contain errors dates back to <a href="/wiki/Ada_Byron%27s_notes_on_the_analytical_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Ada Byron's notes on the analytical engine">Ada Lovelace's 1843 notes on the analytical engine</a>, in which she speaks of the possibility of program "cards" for <a href="/wiki/Charles_Babbage" title="Charles Babbage">Charles Babbage</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Analytical_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Analytical engine">analytical engine</a> being erroneous:</p>/n<blockquote class="templatequote">/n<p>...&#160;an analysing process must equally have been performed in order to furnish the Analytical Engine with the necessary <i>operative</i> data; and that herein may also lie a possible source of error. Granted that the actual mechanism is unerring in its processes, the <i>cards</i> may give it wrong orders.</p>/n</blockquote>/n<h3><span id="&quot;Bugs_in_the_System&quot;_report"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Bugs_in_the_System.22_report">"Bugs in the System" report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: &quot;Bugs in the System&quot; report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>The Open Technology Institute, run by the group, New America,<sup id="cite_ref-:1_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">[16]</a></sup> released a report "Bugs in the System" in August 2016 stating that U.S. policymakers should make reforms to help researchers identify and address software bugs. The report "highlights the need for reform in the field of software vulnerability discovery and disclosure."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup> One of the report’s authors said that Congress has not done enough to address cyber software vulnerability, even though Congress has passed a number of bills to combat the larger issue of cyber security.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Government researchers, companies, and cyber security experts are the people who typically discover software flaws. The report calls for reforming computer crime and copyright laws.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup></p>/n<blockquote>/n<p>"The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, the Digital Millennium Copyright Act and the Electronic Communications Privacy Act criminalize and create civil penalties for actions that security researchers routinely engage in while conducting legitimate security research, the report said."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup></p>/n</blockquote>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>There is ongoing debate over the use of the term "bug" to describe software errors. One argument is that the word "bug" is divorced from a sense that a human being caused the problem, and instead implies that the defect arose on its own, leading to a push to abandon the term "bug" in favor of terms such as "defect", with limited success.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Since the 1970s <a href="/wiki/Gary_Kildall" title="Gary Kildall">Gary Kildall</a> somewhat humorously suggested to use the term "blunder".<sup id="cite_ref-Shustek_2016_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shustek_2016-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kildall_1993_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kildall_1993-20">[20]</a></sup></p>/n<p>In software engineering, <i>mistake metamorphism</i> (from Greek <i>meta</i> = "change", <i>morph</i> = "form") refers to the evolution of a defect in the final stage of software deployment. Transformation of a "mistake" committed by an analyst in the early stages of the software development lifecycle, which leads to a "defect" in the final stage of the cycle has been called 'mistake metamorphism'.<sup id="cite_ref-metamorph_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metamorph-21">[21]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Different stages of a "mistake" in the entire cycle may be described as "mistakes", "anomalies", "faults", "failures", "errors", "exceptions", "crashes", "bugs", "defects", "incidents", or "side effects".<sup id="cite_ref-metamorph_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metamorph-21">[21]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">Prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>The software industry has put much effort into reducing bug counts.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> These include:</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Typographical_errors">Typographical errors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Typographical errors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Bugs usually appear when the programmer makes a <a href="/wiki/Logic_error" title="Logic error">logic error</a>. Various innovations in <a href="/wiki/Programming_style" title="Programming style">programming style</a> and <a href="/wiki/Defensive_programming" title="Defensive programming">defensive programming</a> are designed to make these bugs less likely, or easier to spot. Some typos, especially of symbols or logical/<a href="/wiki/Operator_(mathematics)" title="Operator (mathematics)">mathematical operators</a>, allow the program to operate incorrectly, while others such as a missing symbol or misspelled name may prevent the program from operating. Compiled languages can reveal some typos when the source code is compiled.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_methodologies">Development methodologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Development methodologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Several schemes assist managing programmer activity so that fewer bugs are produced. <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">Software engineering</a> (which addresses software design issues as well) applies many techniques to prevent defects. For example, formal <a href="/wiki/Program_specification" class="mw-redirect" title="Program specification">program specifications</a> state the exact behavior of programs so that design bugs may be eliminated. Unfortunately, formal specifications are impractical for anything but the shortest programs, because of problems of <a href="/wiki/Combinatorial_explosion" title="Combinatorial explosion">combinatorial explosion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nondeterministic_algorithm" title="Nondeterministic algorithm">indeterminacy</a>.</p>/n<p><a href="/wiki/Unit_testing" title="Unit testing">Unit testing</a> involves writing a test for every function (unit) that a program is to perform.</p>/n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Test-driven_development" title="Test-driven development">test-driven development</a> unit tests are written before the code and the code is not considered complete until all tests complete successfully.</p>/n<p><a href="/wiki/Agile_software_development" title="Agile software development">Agile software development</a> involves frequent software releases with relatively small changes. Defects are revealed by user feedback.</p>/n<p>Open source development allows anyone to examine source code. A school of thought popularized by <a href="/wiki/Eric_S._Raymond" title="Eric S. Raymond">Eric S. Raymond</a> as <a href="/wiki/Linus%27s_Law" title="Linus's Law">Linus's Law</a> says that popular <a href="/wiki/Open-source_software" title="Open-source software">open-source software</a> has more chance of having few or no bugs than other software, because "given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This assertion has been disputed, however: computer security specialist <a href="/wiki/Elias_Levy" title="Elias Levy">Elias Levy</a> wrote that "it is easy to hide vulnerabilities in complex, little understood and undocumented source code," because, "even if people are reviewing the code, that doesn't mean they're qualified to do so."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> An example of this actually happening, accidentally, was the <a href="/wiki/Debian#2008_OpenSSL_vulnerability" title="Debian">2008 OpenSSL vulnerability in Debian</a>.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Programming_language_support">Programming language support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Programming language support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p><a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">Programming languages</a> include features to help prevent bugs, such as static <a href="/wiki/Type_system" title="Type system">type systems</a>, restricted <a href="/wiki/Namespace" title="Namespace">namespaces</a> and <a href="/wiki/Modular_programming" title="Modular programming">modular programming</a>. For example, when a programmer writes (pseudocode) <code>LET REAL_VALUE PI = "THREE AND A BIT"</code>, although this may be syntactically correct, the code fails a <a href="/wiki/Type_checking" class="mw-redirect" title="Type checking">type check</a>. Compiled languages catch this without having to run the program. Interpreted languages catch such errors at runtime. Some languages deliberate exclude features that easily lead to bugs, at the expense of slower performance: the general principle being that, it is almost always better to write simpler, slower code than inscrutable code that runs slightly faster, especially considering that <a href="/wiki/Maintenance_cost" class="mw-redirect" title="Maintenance cost">maintenance cost</a> is substantial. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Java_(programming_language)" title="Java (programming language)">Java programming language</a> does not support <a href="/wiki/Pointer_(computer_programming)" title="Pointer (computer programming)">pointer</a> arithmetic; implementations of some languages such as <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(programming_language)" title="Pascal (programming language)">Pascal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scripting_language" title="Scripting language">scripting languages</a> often have runtime <a href="/wiki/Bounds_checking" title="Bounds checking">bounds checking</a> of arrays, at least in a debugging build.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Code_analysis">Code analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Code analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Tools for <a href="/wiki/Static_code_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Static code analysis">code analysis</a> help developers by inspecting the program text beyond the compiler's capabilities to spot potential problems. Although in general the problem of finding all programming errors given a specification is not solvable (see <a href="/wiki/Halting_problem" title="Halting problem">halting problem</a>), these tools exploit the fact that human programmers tend to make certain kinds of simple mistakes often when writing software.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instrumentation">Instrumentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Instrumentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Tools to monitor the performance of the software as it is running, either specifically to find problems such as <a href="/wiki/Bottleneck_(engineering)" title="Bottleneck (engineering)">bottlenecks</a> or to give assurance as to correct working, may be embedded in the code explicitly (perhaps as simple as a statement saying <code>PRINT "I AM HERE"</code>), or provided as tools. It is often a surprise to find where most of the time is taken by a piece of code, and this removal of assumptions might cause the code to be rewritten.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing">Testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p><a href="/wiki/Software_tester" class="mw-redirect" title="Software tester">Software testers</a> are people whose primary task is to find bugs, or write code to support testing. On some projects, more resources may be spent on testing than in developing the program.</p>/n<p>Measurements during testing can provide an estimate of the number of likely bugs remaining; this becomes more reliable the longer a product is tested and developed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Debugging">Debugging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Debugging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Classpath_bugs.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Classpath_bugs.png/350px-Classpath_bugs.png" width="350" height="291" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Classpath_bugs.png/525px-Classpath_bugs.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Classpath_bugs.png/700px-Classpath_bugs.png 2x" data-file-width="722" data-file-height="600" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Classpath_bugs.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nThe typical bug history (<a href="/wiki/GNU_Classpath" title="GNU Classpath">GNU Classpath</a> project data). A new bug submitted by the user is <i>unconfirmed.</i> Once it has been reproduced by a developer, it is a <i>confirmed</i> bug. The confirmed bugs are later <i>fixed</i>. Bugs belonging to other categories (unreproducible, will not be fixed, etc.) are usually in the minority</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Debugging" title="Debugging">Debugging</a></div>/n<p>Finding and fixing bugs, or <i>debugging</i>, is a major part of <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">computer programming</a>. <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Wilkes" title="Maurice Wilkes">Maurice Wilkes</a>, an early computing pioneer, described his realization in the late 1940s that much of the rest of his life would be spent finding mistakes in his own programs.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Usually, the most difficult part of debugging is finding the bug. Once it is found, correcting it is usually relatively easy. Programs known as <a href="/wiki/Debugger" title="Debugger">debuggers</a> help programmers locate bugs by executing code line by line, watching variable values, and other features to observe program behavior. Without a debugger, code may be added so that messages or values may be written to a console or to a window or log file to trace program execution or show values.</p>/n<p>However, even with the aid of a debugger, locating bugs is something of an art. It is not uncommon for a bug in one section of a program to cause failures in a completely different section,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> thus making it especially difficult to track (for example, an error in a graphics <a href="/wiki/Rendering_(computer_graphics)" title="Rendering (computer graphics)">rendering</a> routine causing a file <a href="/wiki/Input/output" title="Input/output">I/O</a> routine to fail), in an apparently unrelated part of the system.</p>/n<p>Sometimes, a bug is not an isolated flaw, but represents an error of thinking or planning on the part of the programmer. Such <i><a href="/wiki/Logic_error" title="Logic error">logic errors</a></i> require a section of the program to be overhauled or rewritten. As a part of <a href="/wiki/Code_review" title="Code review">code review</a>, stepping through the code and imagining or transcribing the execution process may often find errors without ever reproducing the bug as such.</p>/n<p>More typically, the first step in locating a bug is to reproduce it reliably. Once the bug is reproducible, the programmer may use a debugger or other tool while reproducing the error to find the point at which the program went astray.</p>/n<p>Some bugs are revealed by inputs that may be difficult for the programmer to re-create. One cause of the <a href="/wiki/Therac-25" title="Therac-25">Therac-25</a> radiation machine deaths was a bug (specifically, a <a href="/wiki/Race_condition" title="Race condition">race condition</a>) that occurred only when the machine operator very rapidly entered a treatment plan; it took days of practice to become able to do this, so the bug did not manifest in testing or when the manufacturer attempted to duplicate it. Other bugs may disappear when the program is run with a debugger; these <a href="/wiki/Heisenbug" title="Heisenbug"><i>heisenbugs</i></a> (humorously named after the <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty_principle" title="Uncertainty principle">Heisenberg uncertainty principle</a>).</p>/n<p>Since the 1990s, particularly following the <a href="/wiki/Ariane_5_Flight_501" class="mw-redirect" title="Ariane 5 Flight 501">Ariane 5 Flight 501</a> disaster, interest in automated aids to debugging rose, such as <a href="/wiki/Static_code_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Static code analysis">static code analysis</a> by <a href="/wiki/Abstract_interpretation" title="Abstract interpretation">abstract interpretation</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p>Some classes of bugs have nothing to do with the code. Faulty documentation or hardware may lead to problems in system use, even though the code matches the documentation. In some cases, changes to the code eliminate the problem even though the code then no longer matches the documentation. <a href="/wiki/Embedded_system" title="Embedded system">Embedded systems</a> frequently <a href="/wiki/Workaround" title="Workaround">work around</a> hardware bugs, since to make a new version of a <a href="/wiki/Read-only_memory" title="Read-only memory">ROM</a> is much cheaper than remanufacturing the hardware, especially if they are <a href="/wiki/Commodity_items" class="mw-redirect" title="Commodity items">commodity items</a>.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bug_management">Bug management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Bug management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Bug management includes the process of documenting, categorizing, assigning, reproducing, correcting and releasing the corrected code. Proposed changes to software&#160;– bugs as well as enhancement requests and even entire releases&#160;– are commonly tracked and managed using <a href="/wiki/Bug_tracking_system" title="Bug tracking system">bug tracking systems</a> or <a href="/wiki/Issue_tracking_system" title="Issue tracking system">issue tracking systems</a>. The items added may be called defects, tickets, issues, or, following the <a href="/wiki/Agile_development" class="mw-redirect" title="Agile development">agile development</a> paradigm, stories and epics. Categories may be objective, subjective or a combination, such as <a href="/wiki/Version_number" class="mw-redirect" title="Version number">version number</a>, area of the software, severity and priority, as well as what type of issue it is, such as a feature request or a bug.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Severity">Severity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Severity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p><i>Severity</i> is the impact the bug has on system operation. This impact may be data loss, financial, loss of goodwill and wasted effort. Severity levels are not standardized. Impacts differ across industry. A crash in a video game has a totally different impact than a crash in a web browser, or real time monitoring system. For example, bug severity levels might be "crash or hang", "no workaround" (meaning there is no way the customer can accomplish a given task), "has workaround" (meaning the user can still accomplish the task), "visual defect" (for example, a missing image or displaced button or form element), or "documentation error". Some software publishers use more qualified severities such as "critical", "high", "low", "blocker" or "trivial".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The severity of a bug may be a separate category to its priority for fixing, and the two may be quantified and managed separately.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Priority">Priority</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Priority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p><i>Priority</i> controls where a bug falls on the list of planned changes. The priority is decided by each software producer. Priorities are sometimes numerical and sometimes named, such as "critical," "high," "low" or "deferred"; note that these may be similar or even identical to severity ratings when looking at different software producers. For example, priority 1 bugs may always be fixed for the next release, while "5" bugs may never be fixed. Industry practice employs an inverted scale, so that highest priority are low numbers (0 and 1), while larger numbers indicate lower priority.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_releases">Software releases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Software releases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>It is common practice to release software with known, low-priority bugs. Most big software projects maintain two lists of "known bugs" – those known to the software team, and those to be told to users.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The second list informs users about bugs that are not fixed in a specific release and <a href="/wiki/Workaround" title="Workaround">workarounds</a> may be offered. Releases are of different kinds. Bugs of sufficiently high priority may warrant a special release of part of the code containing only modules with those fixes. These are known as <i><a href="/wiki/Patch_(computing)" title="Patch (computing)">patches</a></i>. Most releases include a mixture of behavior changes and multiple bug fixes. Releases that emphasize bug fixes are known as <i>maintenance</i> releases. Releases that emphasize feature additions/changes are known as major releases and often have names to distinguish the new features from the old.</p>/n<p>Reasons that a software publisher opts not to patch or even fix a particular bug include:</p>/n<ul>/n<li>A deadline must be met and resources are insufficient to fix all bugs by the deadline.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>/n<li>The bug is already fixed in an upcoming release, and it is not of high priority.</li>/n<li>The changes required to fix the bug are too costly or affect too many other components, requiring a major testing activity.</li>/n<li>It may be suspected, or known, that some users are relying on the existing buggy behavior; a proposed fix may introduce a <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/breaking_change" class="extiw" title="wikt:breaking change">breaking change</a>.</li>/n<li>The problem is in an area that will be obsolete with an upcoming release; fixing it is unnecessary.</li>/n<li>It's "not a bug". A misunderstanding has arisen between expected and perceived behavior, when such misunderstanding is not due to confusion arising from design flaws, or faulty documentation.</li>/n</ul>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style" role="presentation">/n<tr>/n<td class="mbox-image">/n<div style="width:52px"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="48" data-file-height="48" /></div>/n</td>/n<td class="mbox-text">/n<div class="mbox-text-span">This section contains <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Embedded_lists" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Embedded lists">embedded lists</a> that <b>may be poorly defined, <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">unverified</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOT#IINFO" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOT">indiscriminate</a></b>. <span class="hide-when-compact">Please help to <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit">clean it up</a> to meet Wikipedia's quality standards. Bugs arise from oversights or misunderstandings made by a software team during specification, design, coding, data entry or documentation. For example, a relatively simple program to alphabetize a list of words, the design might fail to consider what should happen when a word contains a <a href="/wiki/Hyphen" title="Hyphen">hyphen</a>. Or when converting an abstract design into code, the coder might inadvertently create an <a href="/wiki/Off-by-one_error" title="Off-by-one error">off-by-one error</a> and fail to sort the last word in a list. Errors may be as simple as a typing error: a "&lt;" where a "&gt;" was intended.</p>/n<p>Another category of bug is called a <i><a href="/wiki/Race_condition" title="Race condition">race condition</a></i> that may occur when programs have multiple components executing at the same time. These bugs may be difficult to detect or anticipate, since they may not occur during every execution of a program.</p>/n<p>Conceptual errors are a developer's misunderstanding of what the software must do. The resulting may perform according to the developer's understanding, but not what is really needed. In applications such as <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">manned space travel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Automotive_safety" class="mw-redirect" title="Automotive safety">automotive safety</a>, since software flaws have the potential to cause human injury or even death, such software will have far more scrutiny and quality control than, for example, an online shopping website. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but this was not felt to be feasible for projects in the business world.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Well-known_bugs">Well-known bugs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_bug&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Well-known bugs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_software_bugs" title="List of software bugs">List of software bugs</a></div>/n<p>A number of software bugs have become well-known, usually due to their severity: examples include various space and military aircraft crashes.
