For the extended play and long play by Australian Crawl, see <a href="/wiki/Semantics_(album)" title="Semantics (album)">Semantics (album)</a>.</div>/n<p><b>Semantics</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient&#160;Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%83%CE%B7%CE%BC%CE%B1%CE%BD%CF%84%CE%B9%CE%BA%CF%8C%CF%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:σημαντικός">σημαντικός</a></span> <i>sēmantikos</i>, "significant")<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is the <a href="/wiki/Linguistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Linguistic">linguistic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philosophical" class="mw-redirect" title="Philosophical">philosophical</a> study of <a href="/wiki/Meaning_(linguistics)" title="Meaning (linguistics)">meaning</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>, programming languages, formal logics, and <a href="/wiki/Semiotics" title="Semiotics">semiotics</a>. It is concerned with the relationship between <i>signifiers</i>—like <a href="/wiki/Word" title="Word">words</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phrase" title="Phrase">phrases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sign" title="Sign">signs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbols</a>—and what they stand for, their <a href="/wiki/Denotation" title="Denotation">denotation</a>.</p>/n<p>In <a href="/wiki/International_scientific_vocabulary" title="International scientific vocabulary">international scientific vocabulary</a> semantics is also called <i><a href="/wiki/Semasiology" title="Semasiology">semasiology</a></i>. The word <i>semantics</i> was first used by <a href="/wiki/Michel_Br%C3%A9al" title="Michel Bréal">Michel Bréal</a>, a French philologist.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It denotes a range of ideas—from the popular to the highly technical. It is often used in ordinary language for denoting a problem of understanding that comes down to word selection or <a href="/wiki/Connotation" title="Connotation">connotation</a>. This problem of understanding has been the subject of many formal enquiries, over a long period of time, especially in the field of <a href="/wiki/Formal_semantics_(linguistics)" title="Formal semantics (linguistics)">formal semantics</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, it is the study of the interpretation of signs or symbols used in <a href="/wiki/Agent_(grammar)" title="Agent (grammar)">agents</a> or <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">communities</a> within particular circumstances and contexts.<sup id="cite_ref-Neurath1955_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neurath1955-4">[4]</a></sup> Within this view, sounds, facial expressions, body language, and <a href="/wiki/Proxemics" title="Proxemics">proxemics</a> have semantic (meaningful) content, and each comprises several branches of study. In written language, things like paragraph structure and punctuation bear semantic content; other forms of language bear other semantic content.<sup id="cite_ref-Neurath1955_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neurath1955-4">[4]</a></sup></p>/n<p>The formal study of semantics intersects with many other fields of inquiry, including <a href="/wiki/Lexicology" title="Lexicology">lexicology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">pragmatics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> and others. Independently, semantics is also a well-defined field in its own right, often with synthetic properties.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_language" title="Philosophy of language">philosophy of language</a>, semantics and <a href="/wiki/Reference" title="Reference">reference</a> are closely connected. Further related fields include <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">philology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Semiotics" title="Semiotics">semiotics</a>. The formal study of semantics can therefore be manifold and complex.</p>/n<p>Semantics contrasts with <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a>, the study of the combinatorics of units of a language (without reference to their meaning), and <a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">pragmatics</a>, the study of the relationships between the symbols of a language, their meaning, and the users of the language.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Semantics as a field of study also has significant ties to various representational theories of meaning including truth theories of meaning, coherence theories of meaning, and correspondence theories of meaning. Each of these is related to the general philosophical study of reality and the representation of meaning. In 1960s psychosemantic studies became popular after <a href="/wiki/Charles_E._Osgood" title="Charles E. Osgood">Osgood</a>'s massive cross-cultural studies using his <a href="/wiki/Semantic_differential" title="Semantic differential">semantic differential</a> (SD) method that used thousands of nouns and adjective bipolar scales. A specific form of the SD, Projective Semantics method<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> uses only most common and neutral nouns that correspond to the 7 groups (factors) of adjective-scales most consistently found in cross-cultural studies (Evaluation, Potency, Activity as found by Osgood, and Reality, Organization, Complexity, Limitation as found in other studies). In this method, seven groups of bipolar adjective scales corresponded to seven types of nouns so the method was thought to have the object-scale symmetry (OSS) between the scales and nouns for evaluation using these scales. For example, the nouns corresponding to the listed 7 factors would be: Beauty, Power, Motion, Life, Work, Chaos, Law. Beauty was expected to be assessed unequivocally as “very good” on adjectives of Evaluation-related scales, Life as “very real” on Reality-related scales, etc. However, deviations in this symmetric and very basic matrix might show underlying biases of two types: scales-related bias and objects-related bias. This OSS design meant to increase the sensitivity of the SD method to any semantic biases in responses of people within the same culture and educational background.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>/n<p></p>/n<div id="toc" class="toc">/n<div href="/w/index.php?title=Semantics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Linguistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, <b>semantics</b> is the subfield that is devoted to the study of meaning, as inherent at the levels of words, phrases, sentences, and larger units of <a href="/wiki/Discourse" title="Discourse">discourse</a> (termed <i>texts</i>, or <i>narratives</i>). The study of semantics is also closely linked to the subjects of representation, reference and denotation. The basic study of semantics is oriented to the examination of the meaning of <a href="/wiki/Sign_(semiotics)" title="Sign (semiotics)">signs</a>, and the study of relations between different linguistic units and <a href="/wiki/Compound_(linguistics)" title="Compound (linguistics)">compounds</a>: <a href="/wiki/Homonym" title="Homonym">homonymy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">synonymy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Antonym">antonymy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypernym" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypernym">hypernymy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyponym" class="mw-redirect" title="Hyponym">hyponymy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meronymy" title="Meronymy">meronymy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metonymy" title="Metonymy">metonymy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holonymy" title="Holonymy">holonymy</a>, paronyms. A key concern is how meaning attaches to larger chunks of text, possibly as a result of the composition from smaller units of meaning. Traditionally, semantics has included the study of <i><a href="/wiki/Word_sense" title="Word sense">sense</a></i> and denotative <i><a href="/wiki/Reference" title="Reference">reference</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Truth_condition" title="Truth condition">truth conditions</a>, argument structure, <a href="/wiki/Thematic_relation" title="Thematic relation">thematic roles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Discourse_analysis" title="Discourse analysis">discourse analysis</a>, and the linkage of all of these to syntax.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Montague_grammar">Montague grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Montague grammar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>In the late 1960s, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Montague" title="Richard Montague">Richard Montague</a> proposed a system for defining semantic entries in the lexicon in terms of the <a href="/wiki/Lambda_calculus" title="Lambda calculus">lambda calculus</a>. In these terms, the syntactic <a href="/wiki/Parsing" title="Parsing">parse</a> of the sentence <i>John ate every bagel</i> would consist of a subject (<i>John</i>) and a predicate (<i>ate every bagel</i>); Montague demonstrated that the meaning of the sentence altogether could be decomposed into the meanings of its parts and in relatively few rules of combination. The logical predicate thus obtained would be elaborated further, e.g. using truth theory models, which ultimately relate meanings to a set of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Tarski" title="Alfred Tarski">Tarskian</a> universals, which may lie outside the logic. The notion of such meaning atoms or primitives is basic to the <a href="/wiki/Language_of_thought" class="mw-redirect" title="Language of thought">language of thought</a> hypothesis from the 1970s.</p>/n<p>Despite its elegance, <a href="/wiki/Montague_grammar" title="Montague grammar">Montague grammar</a> was limited by the context-dependent variability in word sense, and led to several attempts at incorporating context, such as:</p>/n<ul>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Situation_semantics" title="Situation semantics">Situation semantics</a> (1980s): <a href="/wiki/Truth-value" class="mw-redirect" title="Truth-value">truth-values</a> are incomplete, they get assigned based on context</li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Generative_lexicon" title="Generative lexicon">Generative lexicon</a> (1990s): categories (types) are incomplete, and get assigned based on context</li>/n</ul>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamic_turn_in_semantics">Dynamic turn in template">e</abbr></a></li>/n</ul>/n</div>/n</td>/n</tr>/n</table>/n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Chomskyan</a> linguistics there was no mechanism for the learning of semantic relations, and the <a href="/wiki/Psychological_nativism" title="Psychological nativism">nativist</a> view considered all semantic notions as inborn. Thus, even novel concepts were proposed to have been dormant in some sense. This view was also thought unable to address many issues such as <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphor</a> or associative meanings, and <a href="/wiki/Semantic_change" title="Semantic change">semantic change</a>, where meanings within a linguistic community change over time, and <a href="/wiki/Qualia" title="Qualia">qualia</a> or subjective experience. Another issue not addressed by the nativist model was how perceptual cues are combined in thought, e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Mental_rotation" title="Mental rotation">mental rotation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>/n<p>This view of semantics, as an innate finite meaning inherent in a <a href="/wiki/Lexical_unit" class="mw-redirect" title="Lexical unit">lexical unit</a> that can be composed to generate meanings for larger chunks of discourse, is now being fiercely debated in the emerging domain of <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_linguistics" title="Cognitive linguistics">cognitive linguistics</a><sup id="cite_ref-Langacker:1999_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Langacker:1999-11">[11]</a></sup> and also in the non-<a href="/wiki/Jerry_Fodor" title="Jerry Fodor">Fodorian</a> camp in <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_language" title="Philosophy of language">philosophy of language</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Peregrin:2003_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peregrin:2003-12">[12]</a></sup> The challenge is motivated by:</p>/n<ul>/n<li>factors internal to language, such as the problem of resolving <a href="/wiki/Indexical" class="mw-redirect" title="Indexical">indexical</a> or <a href="/wiki/Anaphora_(linguistics)" title="Anaphora (linguistics)">anaphora</a> (e.g. <i>this x</i>, <i>him</i>, <i>last week</i>). In these situations <i>context</i> serves as the input, but the interpreted utterance also modifies the context, so it is also the output. Thus, the interpretation is necessarily dynamic and the meaning of sentences is viewed as contexts changing potentials instead of <a href="/wiki/Proposition" title="Proposition">propositions</a>.</li>/n<li>factors external to language, i.e. language is not a set of labels stuck on things, but "a toolbox, the importance of whose elements lie in the way they function rather than their attachments to things."<sup id="cite_ref-Peregrin:2003_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peregrin:2003-12">[12]</a></sup> This view reflects the position of the later <a href="/wiki/Wittgenstein" class="mw-redirect" title="Wittgenstein">Wittgenstein</a> and his famous <i>game</i> example, and is related to the positions of <a href="/wiki/Willard_Van_Orman_Quine" title="Willard Van Orman Quine">Quine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donald_Davidson_(philosopher)" title="Donald Davidson (philosopher)">Davidson</a>, and others.</li>/n</ul>/n<p>A concrete example of the latter phenomenon is semantic <a href="/wiki/Underspecification" title="Underspecification">underspecification</a>&#160;– meanings are not complete without some elements of context. To take an example of one word, <i>red</i>, its meaning in a phrase such as <i>red book</i> is similar to many other usages, and can be viewed as compositional.<sup id="cite_ref-Gardenfors:2000_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardenfors:2000-13">[13]</a></sup> However, the colours implied in phrases such as <i>red wine</i> (very dark), and <i>red hair</i> (coppery), or <i>red soil</i>, or <i>red skin</i> are very different. Indeed, these colours by themselves would not be called <i>red</i> by native speakers. These instances are contrastive, so <i>red wine</i> is so called only in comparison with the other kind of wine (which also is not <i>white</i> for the same reasons). This view goes back to <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_de_Saussure" title="Ferdinand de Saussure">de Saussure</a>:</p>/n<blockquote class="templatequote">/n<p>Each of a set of synonyms like <i>redouter</i> ('to dread'), <i>craindre</i> ('to fear'), <i>avoir peur</i> ('to be afraid') has its particular value only because they stand in contrast with one another. No word has a value that can be identified independently of what else is in its vicinity.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>/n</blockquote>/n<p>and may go back to earlier <a href="/wiki/Vy%C4%81kara%E1%B9%87a" title="Vyākaraṇa">Indian</a> views on language, especially the <a href="/wiki/Nyaya" title="Nyaya">Nyaya</a> view of words as indicators and not carriers of meaning.<sup id="cite_ref-Matilal_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Matilal-15">[15]</a></sup></p>/n<p>An attempt to defend a system based on propositional meaning for semantic underspecification can be found in the <a href="/wiki/Generative_lexicon" title="Generative lexicon">generative lexicon</a> model of <a href="/wiki/James_Pustejovsky" title="James Pustejovsky">James Pustejovsky</a>, who extends contextual operations (based on type shifting) into the lexicon. Thus meanings are generated "on the fly" (as you go), based on finite context.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prototype_theory">Prototype theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Prototype theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Another set of concepts related to fuzziness in semantics is based on <a href="/wiki/Prototype_theory" title="Prototype theory">prototypes</a>. The work of <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Rosch" title="Eleanor Rosch">Eleanor Rosch</a> in the 1970s led to a view that natural categories are not characterizable in terms of necessary and sufficient conditions, but are graded (fuzzy at their boundaries) and inconsistent as to the status of their constituent members. One may compare it with <a href="/wiki/Jung" class="mw-redirect" title="Jung">Jung</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Archetype" title="Archetype">archetype</a>, though the concept of <a href="/wiki/Archetype" title="Archetype">archetype</a> sticks to static concept. Some <a href="/wiki/Post-structuralists" class="mw-redirect" title="Post-structuralists">post-structuralists</a> are against the fixed or static meaning of the <a href="/wiki/Words" class="mw-redirect" title="Words">words</a>. <a href="/wiki/Derrida" class="mw-redirect" title="Derrida">Derrida</a>, following <a href="/wiki/Nietzsche" class="mw-redirect" title="Nietzsche">Nietzsche</a>, talked about slippages in fixed meanings.</p>/n<p>Systems of categories are not objectively <i>out there</i> in the world but are rooted in people's experience. These categories evolve as <a href="/wiki/Learning_theory_(education)" title="Learning theory (education)">learned</a> concepts of the world&#160;– meaning is not an objective truth, but a subjective construct, learned from experience, and language arises out of the "grounding of our conceptual systems in shared <a href="/wiki/Embodied_philosophy" class="mw-redirect" title="Embodied philosophy">embodiment</a> and bodily experience".<sup id="cite_ref-LakoffJohnson_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LakoffJohnson-16">[16]</a></sup> A corollary of this is that the conceptual categories (i.e. the lexicon) will not be identical for different cultures, or indeed, for every individual in the same culture. This leads to another debate (see the <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_relativity" title="Linguistic relativity">Sapir–Whorf hypothesis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Eskimo_words_for_snow" title="Eskimo words for snow">Eskimo words for snow</a>).</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories_in_semantics">Theories in semantics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Theories in semantics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formal_semantics">Formal semantics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Formal semantics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Formal_semantics_(linguistics)" title="Formal semantics (linguistics)">Formal semantics (linguistics)</a></div>/n<p>Originates from Montague's work (see above). A highly formalized theory of natural language semantics in which expressions are assigned denotations (meanings) such as individuals, truth values, or functions from one of these to another. The truth of a sentence, and more interestingly, its logical relation to other sentences, is then evaluated relative to a model.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Truth-conditional_semantics">Truth-conditional semantics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Truth-conditional semantics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Truth-conditional_semantics" title="Truth-conditional semantics">Truth-conditional semantics</a></div>/n<p>Pioneered by the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Donald_Davidson_(philosopher)" title="Donald Davidson (philosopher)">Donald Davidson</a>, another formalized theory, which aims to associate each natural language sentence with a meta-language description of the conditions under which it is true, for example: 'Snow is white' is true if and only if snow is white. The challenge is to arrive at the truth conditions for any sentences from fixed meanings assigned to the individual words and fixed rules for how to combine them. In practice, truth-conditional semantics is similar to model-theoretic semantics; conceptually, however, they differ in that truth-conditional semantics seeks to connect language with statements about the real world (in the form of meta-language statements), rather than with abstract models.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conceptual_semantics">Conceptual semantics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Conceptual semantics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_semantics" title="Conceptual semantics">Conceptual semantics</a></div>/n<p>This theory is an effort to explain properties of argument structure. The assumption behind this theory is that syntactic properties of phrases reflect the meanings of the words that head them.<sup id="cite_ref-Levin.2C_Beth_1991_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levin.2C_Beth_1991-17">[17]</a></sup> With this theory, linguists can better deal with the fact that subtle differences in word meaning correlate with other differences in the syntactic structure that the word appears in.<sup id="cite_ref-Levin.2C_Beth_1991_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levin.2C_Beth_1991-17">[17]</a></sup> The way this is gone about is by looking at the internal structure of words.<sup id="cite_ref-Jackendoff.2C_Ray_1990_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jackendoff.2C_Ray_1990-18">[18]</a></sup> These small parts that make up the internal structure of words are termed <i>semantic primitives</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jackendoff.2C_Ray_1990_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jackendoff.2C_Ray_1990-18">[18]</a></sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lexical_semantics">Lexical semantics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Lexical semantics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lexical_semantics" title="Lexical semantics">Lexical semantics</a></div>/n<p>A linguistic theory that investigates word meaning. This theory understands that the meaning of a word is fully reflected by its context. Here, the meaning of a word is constituted by its contextual relations.<sup id="cite_ref-Cruse.2C_D._1986_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cruse.2C_D._1986-19">[19]</a></sup> Therefore, a distinction between degrees of participation as well as modes of participation are made.<sup id="cite_ref-Cruse.2C_D._1986_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cruse.2C_D._1986-19">[19]</a></sup> In order to accomplish this distinction any part of a sentence that bears a meaning and combines with the meanings of other constituents is labeled as a semantic constituent. Semantic constituents that cannot be broken down into more elementary constituents are labeled minimal semantic constituents.<sup id="cite_ref-Cruse.2C_D._1986_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cruse.2C_D._1986-19">[19]</a></sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computational_semantics">Computational semantics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Computational semantics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Computational_semantics" title="Computational semantics">Computational semantics</a></div>/n<p>Computational semantics is focused on the processing of linguistic meaning. In order to do this concrete algorithms and architectures are described. Within this framework the algorithms and architectures are also analyzed in terms of decidability, time/space complexity, data structures that they require and communication protocols.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_science">Computer science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Computer science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Semantics_(computer_science)" title="Semantics (computer science)">Semantics (computer science)</a></div>/n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, the term <i>semantics</i> refers to the meaning of language constructs, as opposed to their form (<a href="/wiki/Syntax_(logic)" title="Syntax (logic)">syntax</a>). According to Euzenat, semantics "provides the rules for interpreting the syntax which do not provide the meaning directly but constrains the possible interpretations of what is declared."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In ontology engineering, the term <i>semantics</i> refers to the meaning of concepts, properties, and relationships that formally represent real-world entities, events, and scenes in a logical underpinning, such as a <a href="/wiki/Description_logic" title="Description logic">description logic</a>, and typically implemented in the <a href="/wiki/Web_Ontology_Language" title="Web Ontology Language">Web Ontology Language</a>. The meaning of <a href="/wiki/Description_logic" title="Description logic">description logic</a> concepts and roles is defined by their model-theoretic semantics, which are based on interpretations.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The concepts, properties, and relationships defined in <a href="/wiki/Web_Ontology_Language" title="Web Ontology Language">OWL</a> ontologies can be deployed directly in the web site markup as <a href="/wiki/RDFa" title="RDFa">RDFa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microdata_(HTML)" title="Microdata (HTML)">HTML5 Microdata</a>, or <a href="/wiki/JSON-LD" title="JSON-LD">JSON-LD</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Graph_databases" class="mw-redirect" title="Graph databases">graph databases</a> as <a href="/wiki/Semantic_triple" title="Semantic triple">RDF triples</a> or <a href="/wiki/Named_graph" title="Named graph">quads</a>, and dereferenced in <a href="/wiki/Linked_Open_Data" class="mw-redirect" title="Linked Open Data">LOD</a> datasets.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Programming_languages">Programming languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Programming languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>The semantics of <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a> and other languages is an important issue and area of study in computer science. On the <a href="/wiki/Semantic_Web" title="Semantic Web">Semantic Web</a>, terms such as <i><a href="/wiki/Semantic_network" title="Semantic network">semantic network</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Semantic_data_model" title="Semantic data model">semantic data model</a></i> are used to describe particular types of data model characterized by the use of <a href="/wiki/Directed_graph" title="Directed graph">directed graphs</a> in which the vertices denote concepts or entities in the world and their properties, and the arcs denote relationships between them. These can formally be described as <a href="/wiki/Description_logic" title="Description logic">description logic</a> concepts and roles, which correspond to <a href="/wiki/Web_Ontology_Language" title="Web Ontology Language">OWL</a> classes and properties.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychology">Psychology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semantics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Psychology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Semantic_memory" title="Semantic memory">semantic memory</a></i> is memory for meaning&#160;– in other words, the aspect of memory that preserves only the <i>gist</i>, the general significance, of remembered experience&#160;– while <a href="/wiki/Episodic_memory" title="Episodic memory">episodic memory</a> is memory for the ephemeral details&#160;– the individual features, or the unique particulars of experience. The term 'episodic memory' was introduced by Tulving and Schacter in the context of 'declarative memory' which involved simple association of factual or objective information concerning its object. Word meaning is measured by the company they keep, i.e. the relationships among words themselves in a <a href="/wiki/Semantic_network" title="Semantic network">semantic network</a>. The memories may be transferred intergenerationally or isolated in one generation due to a cultural disruption. Different generations may have different experiences at similar points in their own time-lines. This may then create a vertically heterogeneous semantic net for certain words in an otherwise homogeneous culture.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In a network created by people analyzing their understanding of the word (such as <a href="/wiki/Wordnet" class="mw-redirect" title="Wordnet">Wordnet</a>) the links and decomposition structures of the network are few in number and kind, and include <i>part of</i>, <i>kind of</i>, and similar links. In automated <a href="/wiki/Ontologies" class="mw-redirect" title="Ontologies">ontologies</a> the links are computed vectors without explicit meaning. Various automated technologies are being developed to compute the meaning of words: <a href="/wiki/Latent_semantic_indexing" class="mw-redirect" title="Latent semantic indexing">latent semantic indexing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Support_vector_machines" class="mw-redirect" title="Support vector machines">support vector machines</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_processing" title="Natural language processing">natural language processing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neural_networks" class="mw-redirect" title="Neural networks">neural networks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Predicate_calculus" class="mw-redirect" title="Predicate calculus">predicate calculus</a> techniques.</p>/n<p><a href="/wiki/Ideasthesia" title="Ideasthesia">Ideasthesia</a> is a psychological phenomenon in which activation of concepts evokes sensory experiences. For example, in synesthesia, activation of a concept of a letter (e.g., that of the letter <i>A</i>) evokes sensory-like experiences (e.g., of red color).</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
