style="display:none">/n<td colspan="2"></td>/n</tr>/n</table>/n<p><b>David Fielding Hartley</b> <a href="/wiki/FBCS" class="mw-redirect" title="FBCS">FBCS</a> (born 14 September 1937)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fellow" title="Fellow">Fellow</a> of <a href="/wiki/Clare_College,_Cambridge" title="Clare College, Cambridge">Clare College, Cambridge</a>. He was Director of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge_Computing_Service" title="University of Cambridge Computing Service">University of Cambridge Computing Service</a> from 1970–1994, Chief Executive of United Kingdom <a href="/wiki/JANET" title="JANET">Joint Academic Network</a> (JANET) 1994–1997, and Executive Director of <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Crystallographic_Data_Centre" title="Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre">Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre</a> (CCDC) 1997–2002. He is now much involved with the <a href="/wiki/The_National_Museum_of_Computing" title="The National Museum of Computing">National Museum of Computing</a>.</p>/n<p>He was involved in the development of the programming language <a href="/wiki/CPL_(programming_language)" title="CPL (programming language)">CPL</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> whose influence can be traced on to <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C</a>, and <a href="/wiki/C%2B%2B" title="C++">C++</a>. He was president of the <a href="/wiki/British_Computer_Society" title="British Computer Society">British Computer Society</a> from 1999 to 2000 and chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Computer_Conservation_Society" title="Computer Conservation Society">Computer Conservation Society</a> from 2007 to 2011.</p>/n<p></p>/n<div id="toc" class="toc">/n<div class="toctitle">/n<h2>Contents</h2>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Student_years"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Student years</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Software_development"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Software development</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#Computing_service"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">Computing service</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#Public_service"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">Public service</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#Citations"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">Citations</span></a></li>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#Sources"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">Sources</span></a></li>/n</ul>/n</div>/n<p></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_years">Student years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Hartley_(computer_scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Student years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Dr. Hartley became an undergraduate at <a href="/wiki/Clare_College,_Cambridge" title="Clare College, Cambridge">Clare College</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> in 1956. He read Mathematics for the first two years and studied Numerical Analysis and Automatic Computing in his third year, graduating BA in 1959. He then became a research student of <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">Computer science</a> developing the first programming language "<a href="/wiki/Autocode" title="Autocode">Autocode</a>", and its compiler for the <a href="/wiki/EDSAC_2" title="EDSAC 2">EDSAC 2</a> computer, for which he was awarded a PhD in 1963. His thesis was entitled "Automatic Programming for Digital Computers".</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_development">Software development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Hartley_(computer_scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Software development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>As a member of the staff of the university's <a href="/wiki/Computer_Laboratory,_University_of_Cambridge" title="Computer Laboratory, University of Cambridge">Mathematical Laboratory</a> he was joint author, with <a href="/wiki/David_W._Barron" title="David W. Barron">David Barron</a>, John Buxton, Eric Nixon, and <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Strachey" title="Christopher Strachey">Christopher Strachey</a>, of the early high-level programming language <a href="/wiki/CPL_(programming_language)" title="CPL (programming language)">CPL</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> which was subsequently developed into <a href="/wiki/BCPL" title="BCPL">BCPL</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which in turn influenced <a href="/wiki/B_(programming_language)" title="B (programming language)">B</a> and <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>/n<p>From 1962 to 1967 he was a major contributor to the development of the Cambridge Multiple Access System<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> that was developed for the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(computer)" title="Titan (computer)">Titan</a>, the prototype <a href="/wiki/Atlas_(computer)" title="Atlas (computer)">Atlas</a> 2 computer built by <a href="/wiki/Ferranti" title="Ferranti">Ferranti</a> for the university. This was the first <a href="/wiki/Time-sharing" title="Time-sharing">time-sharing</a> system developed outside the United States, and it influenced the later development of <a href="/wiki/UNIX" class="mw-redirect" title="UNIX">UNIX</a>.</p>/n<p>Hartley was successively Junior <a href="/wiki/Research_Fellow" class="mw-redirect" title="Research Fellow">Research Fellow</a> at <a href="/wiki/Churchill_College,_Cambridge" title="Churchill College, Cambridge">Churchill College, Cambridge</a>, Fellow of <a href="/wiki/Darwin_College,_Cambridge" title="Darwin College, Cambridge">Darwin College</a> and then <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge#Teaching" title="University of Cambridge">University Lecturer</a>. He also did some pioneering work in <a href="/wiki/Videotape" title="Videotape">video-tape</a> recorded lecturers. In 1986 he was elected a Fellow of Clare College where he is currently secretary of the Alumni Association.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Computing_service">Computing service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Hartley_(computer_scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Computing service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Between 1970 and 1994 Dr. Hartley was director of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge_Computing_Service" title="University of Cambridge Computing Service">University of Cambridge Computing Service</a>.</p>/n<p>The service had been founded as the Mathematical Laboratory under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/John_Lennard-Jones" title="John Lennard-Jones">John Lennard-Jones</a> in 1937, although it did not become properly established until after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> when <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Wilkes" title="Maurice Wilkes">Maurice Wilkes</a> became Director. Upon its foundation, it was intended <i>"to provide a computing service for general use, and to be a centre for the development of computational techniques in the University"</i><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and Wilkes continued this strong service ethos. He learnt about electronic computation, reading <a href="/wiki/John_von_Neumann" title="John von Neumann">John von Neumann</a>'s <a href="/wiki/First_Draft_of_a_Report_on_the_EDVAC" title="First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC">First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC</a><sup id="cite_ref-eniac_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eniac-9">[9]</a></sup> and attending the final two weeks of the <a href="/wiki/Moore_School_Lectures" title="Moore School Lectures">Moore School Lectures</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/EDSAC" class="mw-redirect" title="EDSAC">EDSAC</a> was the result, and Wilkes also supervised Hartley's PhD.</p>/n<p>When Dr. Hartley became Director, the Mathematical Laboratory was renamed the Computer Laboratory, with separate departments for Teaching and Research, and the Computing Service. One of Dr. Hartley' most notable achievements as Director between 1987 and 1992, was to conceive and design the Granta Backbone Network, a <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">fibre-optic network</a> that joined up all of Cambridge's university and college sites.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_service">Public service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Hartley_(computer_scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Public service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>From 1972 to 1974 Dr Hartley was chairman of the UK Inter-University Committee on Computing. He was a member of the Computer Board for Universities and Research Councils – which allocated government funds to purchase the large and expensive computers that the institutions needed – from 1979 to 1983 where he had special responsibility for network development.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> From 1981 to 1986 he was a member of the Prime Minister's Information Technology Advisory Panel. Having become a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/British_Computer_Society" title="British Computer Society">British Computer Society</a> (FBCS) in 1968, Dr. Hartley served on its Council in 1970–73, 1977–1980 and 1988 to 1990, was a Vice-President from 1987 to 1990, Deputy President in 1998–99 and President in 1999–2000.</p>/n<p>Since ceasing to be Director of the University Computing Service, Dr. Hartley has served both private sector and public sector bodies, but the latter have predominated.</p>/n<p>From 1994 to 1997 he was Chief Executive of the United Kingdom Education and Research Networking Association (UKERNA) whose objectives were to take responsibility for the UK academic community's networking programme, and to further opportunities with other communities, including industry. It developed <a href="/wiki/JANET" title="JANET">JANET</a>, the UK's joint academic network, which provides computer network and related collaborative services to UK education and research, including further- and higher-education organisations and the <a href="/wiki/UK_Research_Councils" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Research Councils">UK Research Councils</a>.</p>/n<p>From 1997 to 2002 Dr. Hartley was Executive Director of the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Crystallographic_Data_Centre" title="Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre">Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> which maintains the largest searchable database of experimentally-determined small molecule crystal structures. It performs analyses on these data and facilitates others' use.</p>/n<p>Dr Hartley maintains strong ties with the University of Cambridge where he continues to be a Fellow of Clare College and an Honorary Member of the Computer Laboratory. He has recently become involved in the history of computing, serving for four years as Chairman of the Computer Conservation Society, a special-interest group of the British Computer Society.
