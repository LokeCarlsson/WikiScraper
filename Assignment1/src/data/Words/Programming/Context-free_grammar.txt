Different context-free grammars can generate the same context-free language. It is important to distinguish the properties of the language (intrinsic properties) from the properties of a particular grammar (extrinsic properties). The <a href="#Language_equality">language equality</a> question (do two given context-free grammars generate the same language?) is <a href="/wiki/Decidability_(logic)" title="Decidability (logic)">undecidable</a>.</p>/n<p>Context-free grammars arise in <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a> where they are used to describe the structure of sentences and words in a <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">natural language</a>, and they were in fact invented by the linguist <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a> for this purpose, but have not really lived up to their original expectation. By contrast, in <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, as the use of recursively-defined concepts increased, they were used more and more. class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>/n</ul>/n</div>/n<p></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Since the time of <a href="/wiki/P%C4%81%E1%B9%87ini" title="Pāṇini">Pāṇini</a>, at least, linguists have described the <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammars</a> of languages in terms of their block structure, and described how sentences are <a href="/wiki/Recursion" title="Recursion">recursively</a> built up from smaller phrases, and eventually individual words or word elements. An essential property of these block structures is that logical units never overlap. For example, the sentence:</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>John, whose blue car was in the garage, walked to the grocery store.</dd>/n</dl>/n<p>can be logically parenthesized as follows:</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>(John, ((whose blue car) (was (in the garage))), (walked (to (the grocery store)))).</dd>/n</dl>/n<p>A context-free grammar provides a simple and mathematically precise mechanism for describing the methods by which phrases in some natural language are built from smaller blocks, capturing the "block structure" of sentences in a natural way. Its simplicity makes the formalism amenable to rigorous mathematical study. Important features of natural language syntax such as <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">agreement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reference" title="Reference">reference</a> are not part of the context-free grammar, but the basic recursive structure of sentences, the way in which clauses nest inside other clauses, and the way in which lists of adjectives and adverbs are swallowed by nouns and verbs, is described exactly.</p>/n<p>The formalism of context-free grammars was developed in the mid-1950s by <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-hu106_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hu106-3">[3]</a></sup> and also their <a href="/wiki/Chomsky_hierarchy" title="Chomsky hierarchy">classification as a special type</a> of <a href="/wiki/Formal_grammar" title="Formal grammar">formal grammar</a> (which he called <a href="/wiki/Phrase-structure_grammar" class="mw-redirect" title="Phrase-structure grammar">phrase-structure grammars</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-chomsky1956_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chomsky1956-4">[4]</a></sup> What Chomsky called a phrase structure grammar is also known now as a constituency grammar, whereby constituency grammars stand in contrast to <a href="/wiki/Dependency_grammar" title="Dependency grammar">dependency grammars</a>. In Chomsky's <a href="/wiki/Generative_grammar" title="Generative grammar">generative grammar</a> framework, the syntax of natural language was described by context-free rules combined with transformation rules.</p>/n<p>Block structure was introduced into computer <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Algol_(programming_language)" class="mw-redirect" title="Algol (programming language)">Algol</a> project (1957–1960), which, as a consequence, also featured a context-free grammar to describe the resulting Algol syntax. This became a standard feature of computer languages, and the notation for grammars used in concrete descriptions of computer languages came to be known as <a href="/wiki/Backus%E2%80%93Naur_form" title="Backus–Naur form">Backus–Naur form</a>, after two members of the Algol language design committee.<sup id="cite_ref-hu106_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hu106-3">[3]</a></sup> The "block structure" aspect that context-free grammars capture is so fundamental to grammar that the terms syntax and grammar are often identified with context-free grammar rules, especially in computer science. Formal constraints not captured by the grammar are then considered to be part of the "semantics" of the language.</p>/n<p>Context-free grammars are simple enough to allow the construction of efficient <a href="/wiki/List_of_algorithms#Parsing" title="List of algorithms">parsing algorithms</a> that, for a given string, determine whether and how it can be generated from the grammar. An <a href="/wiki/Earley_parser" title="Earley parser">Earley parser</a> is an example of such an algorithm, while the widely used <a href="/wiki/LR_parser" title="LR parser">LR</a> and <a href="/wiki/LL_parser" title="LL parser">LL parsers</a> are simpler algorithms that deal only with more restrictive subsets of context-free grammars.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formal_definitions">Formal definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formal definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>A context-free grammar <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">G</span> is defined by the 4-<a href="/wiki/Tuple" title="Tuple">tuple</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>/n<p><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" >/n  <semantics>/n    <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">/n      <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">/n        <mi>G</mi>/n        <mo>=</mo>/n        <mo stretchy="false">(</mo>/n        <mi>V</mi>/n        <mo>,</mo>/n        <mi mathvariant="normal">&#x03A3;<!-- Σ --></mi>/n        <mo>,</mo>/n        <mi>R</mi>/n        <mo>,</mo>/n        <mi>S</mi>/n        <mo stretchy="false">)</mo>/n      </mstyle>/n    </mrow>/n    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle G=(V,\Sigma ,R,S)}</annotation>/n  </semantics>/n</math></span><img src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2543ae859dd620f858299855868ae6c2ed066323" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" aria-hidden="true" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; The simplest example:</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>S → aSb</dd>/n<dd>S → ab</dd>/n</dl>/n<p>This grammar generates the language <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" >/n  <semantics>/n    <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">/n      <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">/n        <mo fence="false" stretchy="false">{</mo>/n        <msup>/n          <mi>a</mi>/n          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">/n            <mi>n</mi>/n          </mrow>/n        </msup>/n        <msup>/n          <mi>b</mi>/n          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">/n            <mi>n</mi>/n          </mrow>/n        </msup>/n        <mo>:</mo>/n        <mi>n</mi>/n        <mo>&#x2265;<!-- ≥ --></mo>/n        <mn>1</mn>/n        <mo fence="false" stretchy="false">}</mo>/n      </mstyle>/n    </mrow>/n    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle \{a^{n}b^{n}:n\geq 1\}}</annotation>/n  </semantics>/n</math></span><img src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5dae75c188a2c5c897ef0751e4a7af45d452f73a" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" aria-hidden="true" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:14.68ex; height:2.843ex;" alt="\{a^{n}b^{n}:n\geq 1\}" /></span>, which is not <a href="/wiki/Regular_language" title="Regular language">regular</a> (according to the <a href="/wiki/Pumping_lemma_for_regular_languages" title="Pumping lemma for regular languages">pumping lemma for regular languages</a>).</p>/n<p>The special character ε stands for the empty string. By changing the above grammar to</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>S → aSb | ε</dd>/n</dl>/n<p>we obtain a grammar generating the language <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" >/n  <semantics>/n    <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">/n      <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">/n        <mo fence="false" stretchy="false">{</mo>/n        <msup>/n          <mi>a</mi>/n          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">/n            <mi>n</mi>/n          </mrow>/n        </msup>/n        <msup>/n          <mi>b</mi>/n          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">/n            <mi>n</mi>/n          </mrow>/n        </msup>/n        <mo>:</mo>/n        <mi>n</mi>/n        <mo>&#x2265;<!-- ≥ --></mo>/n        <mn>0</mn>/n        <mo fence="false" stretchy="false">}</mo>/n      </mstyle>/n    </mrow>/n    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle \{a^{n}b^{n}:n\geq 0\}}</annotation>/n  </semantics>/n</math></span><img src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60bf05fe2dd369bcaa4a3f9e6572418062dc702f" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" aria-hidden="true" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:14.68ex; height:2.843ex;" alt="\{a^{n}b^{n}:n\geq 0\}" /></span> instead. This differs only in that it contains the empty string while the original grammar did not.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Algebraic_expressions">Algebraic expressions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Algebraic expressions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Here is a context-free grammar for syntactically correct <a href="/wiki/Infix_notation" title="Infix notation">infix</a> algebraic expressions in the variables x, y and z:</p>/n<ol>/n<li>S → x</li>/n<li>S → y</li>/n<li>S → z</li>/n<li>S → S + S</li>/n<li>S → S - S</li>/n<li>S → S * S</li>/n<li>S → S / S</li>/n<li>S → ( S )</li>/n</ol>/n<p>This grammar can, for example, generate the string</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>( x + y ) * x - z * y / ( x + x )</dd>/n</dl>/n<p>as follows:</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>S (the start symbol)</dd>/n<dd>→ S - S (by rule 5)</dd>/n<dd>→ S * S - S (by rule 6, applied to the leftmost S)</dd>/n<dd>→ S * S - S / S (by rule 7, applied to the rightmost S)</dd>/n<dd>→ ( S ) * S - S / S (by rule 8, applied to the leftmost S)</dd>/n<dd>→ ( S ) * S - S / ( S ) (by rule 8, applied to the rightmost S)</dd>/n<dd>→ ( S + S ) * S - S / ( S ) (etc.)</dd>/n<dd>→ ( S + S ) * S - S * S / ( S )</dd>/n<dd>→ ( S + S ) * S - S * S / ( S + S )</dd>/n<dd>→ ( x + S ) * S - S * S / ( S + S )</dd>/n<dd>→ ( x + y ) * S - S * S / ( S + S )</dd>/n<dd>→ ( x + y ) * x - S * y / ( S + S )</dd>/n<dd>→ ( x + y ) * x - S * y / ( x + S )</dd>/n<dd>→ ( x + y ) * x - z * y / ( x + S )</dd>/n<dd>→ ( x + y ) * x - z * y / ( x + x )</dd>/n</dl>/n<p>Note that many choices were made underway as to which rewrite was going to be performed next. These choices look quite arbitrary. As a matter of fact, they are, in the sense that the string finally generated is always the same. For example, the second and third rewrites</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>→ S * S - S (by rule 6, applied to the leftmost S)</dd>/n<dd>→ S * S - S / S (by rule 7, applied to the rightmost S)</dd>/n</dl>/n<p>could be done in the opposite order:</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>→ S - S / S (by rule 7, applied to the rightmost S)</dd>/n<dd>→ S * S - S / S (by rule 6, applied to the leftmost S)</dd>/n</dl>/n<p>Also, many choices were made on which rule to apply to each selected <code>S</code>. Changing the choices made and not only the order they were made in usually affects which terminal string comes out at the end.</p>/n<p>Let's look at this in more detail. Consider the <a href="/wiki/Parse_tree" title="Parse tree">parse tree</a> of this derivation:</p>/n<pre>/n           S/n           |/n          /|\/n         S - S/n        /     \/n       /|\    /|\/n      S * S  S / S/n     /    |  |    \/n    /|\   x /|\   /|\/n   ( S )   S * S ( S )/n    /      |   |    \   /n   /|\     z   y   /|\/n  S + S           S + S/n  |   |           |   |/n  x   y           x   x/n</pre>/n<p>Starting at the top, step by step, an S in the tree is expanded, until no more unexpanded <code>S</code>es (nonterminals) remain. Picking a different order of expansion will produce a different derivation, but the same parse tree. The parse tree will only change if we pick a different rule to apply at some position in the tree.</p>/n<p>But can a different parse tree still produce the same terminal string, which is <code>( x + y ) * x - z * y / ( x + x )</code> in this case? Yes, for this particular grammar, this is possible. Grammars with this property are called <a href="/wiki/Ambiguous_grammar" title="Ambiguous grammar">ambiguous</a>.</p>/n<p>For example, <code>x + y * z</code> can be produced with these two different parse trees:</p>/n<pre>/n         S               S/n         |               |/n        /|\             /|\/n       S * S           S + S    /n      /     \         /     \/n     /|\     z       x     /|\/n    S + S                 S * S    /n    |   |                 |   |/n    x   y                 y   z/n</pre>/n<p>However, the <i>language</i> described by this grammar is not inherently ambiguous: an alternative, unambiguous grammar can be given for the language, for example:</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>T → x</dd>/n<dd>T → y</dd>/n<dd>T → z</dd>/n<dd>S → S + T</dd>/n<dd>S → S - T</dd>/n<dd>S → S * T</dd>/n<dd>S → S / T</dd>/n<dd>T → ( S )</dd>/n<dd>S → T</dd>/n</dl>/n<p>(once again picking <code>S</code> as the start symbol). This alternative grammar will produce <code>x + y * z</code> with a parse tree similar to the left one above, i.e. implicitly assuming the association <code>(x + y) * z</code>, which is not according to standard operator precedence. More elaborate, unambiguous and context-free grammars can be constructed that produce parse trees that obey all desired <a href="/wiki/Operator-precedence_parser" title="Operator-precedence parser">operator precedence</a> and associativity rules.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_examples">Further examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Further examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Example_1">Example 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Example 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>/n<p>A context-free grammar for the language consisting of all strings over {a,b} containing an unequal number of a's and b's:</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>S → U | V</dd>/n<dd>U → TaU | TaT | UaT</dd>/n<dd>V → TbV | TbT | VbT</dd>/n<dd>T → aTbT | bTaT | ε</dd>/n</dl>/n<p>Here, the nonterminal T can generate all strings with the same number of a's as b's, the nonterminal U generates all strings with more a's than b's and the nonterminal V generates all strings with fewer a's than b's. Omitting the third alternative in the rule for U and V doesn't restrict the grammar's language.</p>/n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Example_2">Example 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Example 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>/n<p>Another example of a non-regular language is <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" >/n  <semantics>/n    <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">/n      <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">/n        <mo fence="false" stretchy="false">{</mo>/n        <msup>/n          <mi>b</mi>/n          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">/n            <mi>n</mi>/n          </mrow>/n        </msup>/n        <msup>/n          <mi>a</mi>/n          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">/n            <mi>m</mi>/n          </mrow>/n        </msup>/n        <msup>/n          <mi>b</mi>/n          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">/n            <mn>2</mn>/n            <mi>n</mi>/n          </mrow>/n        </msup>/n        <mo>:</mo>/n        <mi>n</mi>/n        <mo>&#x2265;<!-- ≥ --></mo>/n        <mn>0</mn>/n        <mo>,</mo>/n        <mi>m</mi>/n        <mo>&#x2265;<!-- ≥ --></mo>/n        <mn>0</mn>/n        <mo fence="false" stretchy="false">}</mo>/n      </mstyle>/n    </mrow>/n    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle \{b^{n}a^{m}b^{2n}:n\geq 0,m\geq 0\}}</annotation>/n  </semantics>/n</math></span><img src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09bff523144419b72c947e8228abd5b1e04ca83a" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" aria-hidden="true" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:25.577ex; height:3.176ex;" alt="\{b^{n}a^{m}b^{2n}:n\geq 0,m\geq 0\}" /></span>. It is context-free as it can be generated by the following context-free grammar:</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>S → bSbb | A</dd>/n<dd>A → aA | ε</dd>/n</dl>/n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_examples">Other examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>/n<p>The <a href="/wiki/First-order_logic#Formation_rules" title="First-order logic">formation rules</a> for the terms and formulas of formal logic fit the definition of context-free grammar, except that the set of symbols may be infinite and there may be more than one start symbol.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Derivations_and_syntax_trees">Derivations and syntax trees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Derivations and syntax trees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>A <i>derivation</i> of a string for a grammar is a sequence of grammar rule applications that transform the start symbol into the string. A derivation proves that the string belongs to the grammar's language.</p>/n<p>A derivation is fully determined by giving, for each step:</p>/n<ul>/n<li>the rule applied in that step</li>/n<li>the occurrence of its left-hand side to which it is applied</li>/n</ul>/n<p>For clarity, the intermediate string is usually given as well.</p>/n<p>For instance, with the grammar:</p>/n<pre>/n (1)  S → S + S/n (2)  S → 1/n (3)  S → a/n</pre>/n<p>the string</p>/n<pre>/n1 + 1 + a/n</pre>/n<p>can be derived with the derivation:</p>/n<pre>/nS/n    → (rule 1 on the first S)/n S+S/n    → (rule 1 on the second S)/n S+S+S/n    → (rule 2 on the second S)/n S+1+S/n    → (rule 3 on the third S)/n S+1+a/n    → (rule 2 on the first S)/n 1+1+a/n</pre>/n<p>Often, a strategy is followed that deterministically determines the next nonterminal to rewrite:</p>/n<ul>/n<li>in a <i>leftmost derivation</i>, it is always the leftmost nonterminal;</li>/n<li>in a <i>rightmost derivation</i>, it is always the rightmost nonterminal.</li>/n</ul>/n<p>Given such a strategy, a derivation is completely determined by the sequence of rules applied. For instance, the leftmost derivation</p>/n<pre>/nS/n    → (rule 1 on the first S)/n S+S/n    → (rule 2 on the first S)/n 1+S/n    → (rule 1 on the first S)/n 1+S+S/n    → (rule 2 on the first S)/n 1+1+S/n    → (rule 3 on the first S)/n 1+1+a/n</pre>/n<p>can be summarized as</p>/n<pre>/nrule 1, rule 2, rule 1, rule 2, rule 3/n</pre>/n<p>The distinction between leftmost derivation and rightmost derivation is important because in most <a href="/wiki/Parsing" title="Parsing">parsers</a> the transformation of the input is defined by giving a piece of code for every grammar rule that is executed whenever the rule is applied. Therefore, it is important to know whether the parser determines a leftmost or a rightmost derivation because this determines the order in which the pieces of code will be executed. See for an example <a href="/wiki/LL_parser" title="LL parser">LL parsers</a> and <a href="/wiki/LR_parser" title="LR parser">LR parsers</a>.</p>/n<p>A derivation also imposes in some sense a hierarchical structure on the string that is derived. For example, if the string "1 + 1 + a" is derived according to the leftmost derivation:</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>S → S + S (1)</dd>/n<dd>&#160;&#160; → 1 + S (2)</dd>/n<dd>&#160;&#160; → 1 + S + S (1)</dd>/n<dd>&#160;&#160; → 1 + 1 + S (2)</dd>/n<dd>&#160;&#160; → 1 + 1 + a (3)</dd>/n</dl>/n<p>the structure of the string would be:</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>{ { 1 }<sub>S</sub> + { { 1 }<sub>S</sub> + { a }<sub>S</sub> }<sub>S</sub> }<sub>S</sub></dd>/n</dl>/n<p>where { ... }<sub>S</sub> indicates a substring recognized as belonging to S. This hierarchy can also be seen as a tree:</p>/n<pre>/n           S/n          /|\/n         / | \/n        /  |  \/n       S  '+'  S/n       |      /|\/n       |     / | \/n      '1'   S '+' S/n            |     |/n           '1'   'a'/n</pre>/n<p>This tree is called a <i><a href="/wiki/Parse_tree" title="Parse tree">parse tree</a></i> or "concrete syntax tree" of the string, by contrast with the <a href="/wiki/Abstract_syntax_tree" title="Abstract syntax tree">abstract syntax tree</a>. In this case the presented leftmost and the rightmost derivations define the same parse tree; however, there is another (rightmost) derivation of the same string</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>S → S + S (1)</dd>/n<dd>&#160;&#160; → S + a (3)</dd>/n<dd>&#160;&#160; → S + S + a (1)</dd>/n<dd>&#160;&#160; → S + 1 + a (2)</dd>/n<dd>&#160;&#160; → 1 + 1 + a (2)</dd>/n</dl>/n<p>and this defines the following parse tree:</p>/n<pre>/n           S /n          /|\/n         / | \/n        /  |  \/n       S  '+'  S/n      /|\      |/n     / | \     |/n    S '+' S   'a'/n    |     |/n   '1'   '1'/n</pre>/n<p>Note however that both parse trees can be obtained by both leftmost and rightmost derivations. For example, the last tree can be obtained with the leftmost derivation as follows:</p>/n<dl>/n<dd>S → S + S (1)</dd>/n<dd>&#160;&#160; → S + S + S (1)</dd>/n<dd>&#160;&#160; → 1 + S + S (2)</dd>/n<dd>&#160;&#160; → 1 + 1 + S (2)</dd>/n<dd>&#160;&#160; → 1 + 1 + a (3)</dd>/n</dl>/n<p>If a string in the language of the grammar has more than one parsing tree, then the grammar is said to be an <i><a href="/wiki/Ambiguous_grammar" title="Ambiguous grammar">ambiguous grammar</a></i>. Such grammars are usually hard to parse because the parser cannot always decide which grammar rule it has to apply. Usually, ambiguity is a feature of the grammar, not the language, and an unambiguous grammar can be found that generates the same context-free language. However, there are certain languages that can only be generated by ambiguous grammars; such languages are called <i><a href="/wiki/Inherently_ambiguous_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Inherently ambiguous language">inherently ambiguous languages</a></i>.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Normal_forms">Normal forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Normal forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Every context-free grammar that does not generate the empty string can be transformed into one in which there is no ε-production (that is, a rule that has the empty string as a product). If a grammar does generate the empty string, it will be necessary to include the rule <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" >/n  <semantics>/n    <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">/n      <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">/n        <mi>S</mi>/n        <mo stretchy="false">&#x2192;<!-- → --></mo>/n        <mi>&#x03F5;<!-- ϵ --></mi>/n      </mstyle>/n    </mrow>/n    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle S\rightarrow \epsilon }</annotation>/n  </semantics>/n</math></span><img src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b118a2c5fb5cbe4c11e07687a2b702019d508431" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" aria-hidden="true" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:6.089ex; height:2.176ex;" alt="S\rightarrow \epsilon " /></span>, but there need be no other ε-rule. Every context-free grammar with no ε-production has an equivalent grammar in <a href="/wiki/Chomsky_normal_form" title="Chomsky normal form">Chomsky normal form</a>, and a grammar in <a href="/wiki/Greibach_normal_form" title="Greibach normal form">Greibach normal form</a>. "Equivalent" here means that the two grammars generate the same language.</p>/n<p>The especially simple form of production rules in Chomsky normal form grammars has both theoretical and practical implications. For instance, given a context-free grammar, one can use the Chomsky normal form to construct a <a href="/wiki/Polynomial-time" class="mw-redirect" title="Polynomial-time">polynomial-time</a> algorithm that decides whether a given string is in the language represented by that grammar or not (the <a href="/wiki/CYK_algorithm" title="CYK algorithm">CYK algorithm</a>).</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Closure_properties">Closure properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Closure properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Context-free languages are <a href="/wiki/Closure_(mathematics)" title="Closure (mathematics)">closed</a> under <a href="/wiki/Set_union" class="mw-redirect" title="Set union">union</a>, <a href="/wiki/String_concatenation#Concatenation_of_sets_of_strings" class="mw-redirect" title="String concatenation">concatenation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kleene_star" title="Kleene star">Kleene star</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/String_substitution" class="mw-redirect" title="String substitution">substitution</a> (in particular <a href="/wiki/String_homomorphism" class="mw-redirect" title="String homomorphism">homomorphism</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/String_homomorphism" class="mw-redirect" title="String homomorphism">inverse homomorphism</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Set_intersection" class="mw-redirect" title="Set intersection">intersection</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Regular_language" href="/wiki/Set_complement" class="mw-redirect" title="Set complement">complementation</a>) and set difference.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decidable_problems">Decidable problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Decidable problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>There are algorithms to decide whether a context-free language is empty and whether it is finite.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Undecidable_problems">Undecidable problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Undecidable problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Some questions that are undecidable for wider classes of grammars become decidable for context-free grammars; e.g. the emptiness problem (whether the grammar generates any terminal strings at all), is undecidable for <a href="/wiki/Context-sensitive_grammar" title="Context-sensitive grammar">context-sensitive grammars</a>, but decidable for context-free grammars.</p>/n<p>However, many problems are <a href="/wiki/Undecidable_problem" title="Undecidable problem">undecidable</a> even for context-free grammars. Examples are:</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Universality">Universality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Universality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Given a CFG, does it generate the language of all strings over the alphabet of terminal symbols used in its rules?<sup id="cite_ref-sipser_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sipser-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hu281_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hu281-19">[19]</a></sup></p>/n<p>A reduction can be demonstrated to this problem from the well-known undecidable problem of determining whether a <a href="/wiki/Turing_machine" title="Turing machine">Turing machine</a> accepts a particular input (the <a href="/wiki/Halting_problem" title="Halting problem">halting problem</a>). The reduction uses the concept of a <i><a href="/wiki/Computation_history" title="Computation history">computation history</a></i>, a string describing an entire computation of a <a href="/wiki/Turing_machine" title="Turing machine">Turing machine</a>. A CFG can be constructed that generates all strings that are not accepting computation histories for a particular Turing machine on a particular input, and thus it will accept all strings only if the machine doesn't accept that input.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_equality">Language equality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Language equality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Given two CFGs, do they generate the same language?<sup id="cite_ref-hu281_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hu281-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eom_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eom-20">[20]</a></sup></p>/n<p>The undecidability of this problem is a direct consequence of the previous: it is impossible to even decide whether a CFG is equivalent to the trivial CFG defining the language of all strings.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_inclusion">Language inclusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Language inclusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Given two CFGs, can the first one generate all strings that the second one can generate?<sup id="cite_ref-hu281_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hu281-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eom_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eom-20">[20]</a></sup></p>/n<p>If this problem was decidable, then language equality could be decided too: two CFGs G1 and G2 generate the same language if L(G1) is a subset of L(G2) and L(G2) is a subset of L(G1).</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Being_in_a_lower_or_higher_level_of_the_Chomsky_hierarchy">Being in a lower or higher level of the Chomsky hierarchy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Being in a lower or higher level of the Chomsky hierarchy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Using <a href="/wiki/Greibach%27s_theorem" title="Greibach's theorem">Greibach's theorem</a>, it can be shown that the two following problems are undecidable:</p>/n<ul>/n<li>Given a <a href="/wiki/Context-sensitive_grammar" title="Context-sensitive grammar">context-sensitive grammar</a>, does it describe a context-free language?</li>/n<li>Given a context-free grammar, does it describe a <a href="/wiki/Regular_language" title="Regular language">regular language</a>?<sup id="cite_ref-hu281_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hu281-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eom_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eom-20">[20]</a></sup></li>/n</ul>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar_ambiguity">Grammar ambiguity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Grammar ambiguity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Given a CFG, is it <a href="/wiki/Ambiguous_grammar" title="Ambiguous grammar">ambiguous</a>?</p>/n<p>The undecidability of this problem follows from the fact that if an algorithm to determine ambiguity existed, the <a href="/wiki/Post_correspondence_problem" title="Post correspondence problem">Post correspondence problem</a> could be decided, which is known to be undecidable.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>An obvious way to extend the context-free grammar formalism is to allow nonterminals to have arguments, the values of which are passed along within the rules. This allows natural language features such as <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">agreement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reference" title="Reference">reference</a>, and programming language analogs such as the correct use and definition of identifiers, to be expressed in a natural way. E.g. we can now easily express that in English sentences, the subject and verb must agree in number. In computer science, examples of this approach include <a href="/wiki/Affix_grammar" title="Affix grammar">affix grammars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Attribute_grammar" title="Attribute grammar">attribute grammars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indexed_grammar" title="Indexed grammar">indexed grammars</a>, and Van Wijngaarden <a href="/wiki/Two-level_grammar" title="Two-level grammar">two-level grammars</a>. Similar extensions exist in linguistics.</p>/n<p>An <b>extended context-free grammar</b> (or <b>regular right part grammar</b>) is one in which the right-hand side of the production rules is allowed to be a <a href="/wiki/Regular_expression" title="Regular expression">regular expression</a> over the grammar's terminals and nonterminals. Extended context-free grammars describe exactly the context-free languages.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Another extension is to allow additional terminal symbols to appear at the left-hand side of rules, constraining their application. This produces the formalism of <a href="/wiki/Context-sensitive_grammar" title="Context-sensitive grammar">context-sensitive grammars</a>.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subclasses">Subclasses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Subclasses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>There are a number of important subclasses of the context-free grammars:</p>/n<ul>/n<li><a href="/wiki/LR_parser" title="LR parser">LR(<i>k</i>)</a> grammars (also known as <a href="/wiki/Deterministic_context-free_grammar" title="Deterministic context-free grammar">deterministic context-free grammars</a>) allow <a href="/wiki/Parsing" title="Parsing">parsing</a> (string recognition) with <a href="/wiki/Deterministic_pushdown_automaton" title="Deterministic pushdown automaton">deterministic pushdown automata</a> (PDA), but they can only describe <a href="/wiki/Deterministic_context-free_language" title="Deterministic context-free language">deterministic context-free languages</a>.</li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/SLR_grammar" title="SLR grammar">Simple LR</a>, <a href="/wiki/LALR_parser" title="LALR parser">Look-Ahead LR</a> grammars are subclasses that allow further simplification of parsing. SLR and LALR are recognized using the same PDA as LR, but with simpler tables, in most cases.</li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/LL_parser" title="LL parser">LL(<i>k</i>) and LL(<i>*</i>)</a> grammars allow parsing by direct construction of a leftmost derivation as described above, and describe even fewer languages.</li>/n<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simple_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Simple grammar (page does not exist)">Simple grammars</a> are a subclass of the LL(1) grammars mostly interesting for its theoretical property that language equality of simple grammars is decidable, while language inclusion is not.</li>/n<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bracketed_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bracketed grammar (page does not exist)">Bracketed grammars</a> have the property that the terminal symbols are divided into left and right bracket pairs that always match up in rules.</li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Linear_grammar" title="Linear grammar">Linear grammars</a> have no rules with more than one nonterminal on the right-hand side.</li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Regular_grammar" title="Regular grammar">Regular grammars</a> are a subclass of the linear grammars and describe the <a href="/wiki/Regular_language" title="Regular language">regular</a> languages, i.e. they correspond to <a href="/wiki/Finite_automaton" class="mw-redirect" title="Finite automaton">finite automata</a> and <a href="/wiki/Regular_expression" title="Regular expression">regular expressions</a>.</li>/n</ul>/n<p>LR parsing extends LL parsing to support a larger range of grammars; in turn, <a href="/wiki/GLR_parser" title="GLR parser">generalized LR parsing</a> extends LR parsing to support arbitrary context-free grammars. On LL grammars and LR grammars, it essentially performs LL parsing and LR parsing, respectively, while on <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nondeterministic_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nondeterministic grammar (page does not exist)">nondeterministic grammars</a>, it is as efficient as can be expected. Although GLR parsing was developed in the 1980s, many new language definitions and <a href="/wiki/Parser_generator" class="mw-redirect" title="Parser generator">parser generators</a> continue to be based on LL, LALR or LR parsing up to the present day.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_applications">Linguistic applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Context-free_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Linguistic applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p><a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Chomsky</a> initially hoped to overcome the limitations of context-free grammars by adding <a href="/wiki/Transformational_grammar" title="Transformational grammar">transformation rules</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-chomsky1956_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chomsky1956-4">[4]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Such rules are another standard device in traditional linguistics; e.g. <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_voice" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammatical voice">passivization</a> in English. Much of <a href="/wiki/Generative_grammar" title="Generative grammar">generative grammar</a> has been devoted to finding ways of refining the descriptive mechanisms of phrase-structure grammar and transformation rules such that exactly the kinds of things can be expressed that natural language actually allows. Allowing arbitrary transformations doesn't meet that goal: they are much too powerful, being <a href="/wiki/Turing_complete" class="mw-redirect" title="Turing complete">Turing complete</a> unless significant restrictions are added (e.g. no transformations that introduce and then rewrite symbols in a context-free fashion).</p>/n<p>Chomsky's general position regarding the non-context-freeness of natural language has held up since then,<sup id="cite_ref-shieber1985_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shieber1985-22">[22]</a></sup> although his specific examples regarding the inadequacy of context-free grammars in terms of their weak generative capacity were later disproved.<sup id="cite_ref-pullum-gazdar1982_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pullum-gazdar1982-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Gazdar" title="Gerald Gazdar">Gerald Gazdar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Pullum" class="mw-redirect" title="Geoffrey Pullum">Geoffrey Pullum</a> have argued that despite a few non-context-free constructions in natural language (such as <a href="/wiki/Cross-serial_dependencies" title="Cross-serial dependencies">cross-serial dependencies</a> in <a href="/wiki/Swiss_German" title="Swiss German">Swiss German</a><sup id="cite_ref-shieber1985_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shieber1985-22">[22]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Reduplication" title="Reduplication">reduplication</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bambara_language" title="Bambara language">Bambara</a><sup id="cite_ref-culy1985_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-culy1985-24">[24]</a></sup>), the vast majority of forms in natural language are indeed context-free.<sup id="cite_ref-pullum-gazdar1982_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pullum-gazdar1982-23">[23]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
