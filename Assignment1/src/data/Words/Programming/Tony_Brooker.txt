University">Essex University</a></td>/n</tr>/n<tr style="display:none">/n<td colspan="2"></td>/n</tr>/n</table>/n<p><b>Ralph Anthony "Tony" Brooker</b> (born 1925, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a><sup id="cite_ref-mile_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mile-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a British academic who was a <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a> known for developing the <a href="/wiki/Mark_1_Autocode" class="mw-redirect" title="Mark 1 Autocode">Mark 1 Autocode</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mile_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mile-1">[1]</a></sup></p>/n<p>He was educated at <a href="/wiki/Emanuel_School" title="Emanuel School">Emanuel School</a> and graduated in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">Mathematics</a> from <a href="/wiki/Imperial_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial College">Imperial College</a> in 1945 and returned there in 1947 as Assistant Lecturer. His first computer project was the construction of a fast multiplier unit from electro-mechanical relays. This was taken over by Sid Michaelson <sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/K._D._Tocher" title="K. D. Tocher">K. D. Tocher</a> and incorporated into ICCE, the Imperial College Computing Engine based on the same technology.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> By then (1949) Tony had moved to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge_Computer_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Cambridge Computer Laboratory">University of Cambridge Computer Laboratory</a> to work for <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Wilkes" title="Maurice Wilkes">Maurice Wilkes</a> on <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a> development for <a href="/wiki/EDSAC" class="mw-redirect" title="EDSAC">EDSAC</a>.</p>/n<p>In October 1951 Tony joined the <a href="/wiki/Computing_Machine_Laboratory" title="Computing Machine Laboratory">Computing Machine Laboratory</a> at <a href="/wiki/Manchester_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchester University">Manchester University</a>, where he took over from <a href="/wiki/Alan_Turing" title="Alan Turing">Alan Turing</a> the task of writing programming manuals and running a user service on the <a href="/wiki/Ferranti_Mark_1" title="Ferranti Mark 1">Ferranti Mark 1</a> computer. It was his experience with the rather tedious Manchester machine-coding conventions that led him to devise what was probably the world's first publicly available <a href="/wiki/High-Level_Language" class="mw-redirect" title="High-Level Language">High-Level Language</a>. This was the <a href="/wiki/Mark_1_Autocode" class="mw-redirect" title="Mark 1 Autocode">Mark 1 Autocode</a> available from March 1954 and therefore about two years ahead of the first <a href="/wiki/Fortran" title="Fortran">Fortran</a> <a href="/wiki/Compiler" title="Compiler">compiler</a>.</p>/n<p>Throughout the 1950s Tony led a group at Manchester working on the theoretical underpinnings of compilers. This culminated in the <a href="/wiki/Compiler-compiler" title="Compiler-compiler">compiler-compiler</a>, a seminal idea first presented at a British Computer Society Conference in July 1960 by <b>Brooker</b> and Morris. This was subsequently implemented on the <a href="/wiki/Ferranti" title="Ferranti">Ferranti</a> ATLAS and used for high-level language development. The ATLAS was regarded as the world's most powerful computer when it was brought into service in December 1962.</p>/n<p>In the mid-1960s Tony helped to inaugurate the UK's first Computer Science degree course at Manchester. He moved to <a href="/wiki/Essex_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Essex University">Essex University</a> in 1967 to take up the University's founding Chair of Computer Science. The first Essex Computer Science graduates obtained their degrees in the summer of 1970.
