href="/wiki/Neuropsychology" title="Neuropsychology">neuropsychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_language" <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">those used to program computers</a> or to study <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-john_lyons_1-0" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Defining natural language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Though the exact definition varies between scholars, natural language can broadly be defined in contrast to artificial or <a href="/wiki/Constructed_language" title="Constructed language">constructed languages</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">computer programming languages</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_auxiliary_language" title="International auxiliary language">international auxiliary languages</a>) and to other <a href="/wiki/Biocommunication_(science)" title="Biocommunication (science)">communication systems in nature</a>. Such examples include bees' <a href="/wiki/Waggle_dance" title="Waggle dance">waggle dance</a><sup id="cite_ref-fernandes_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fernandes-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Whale_vocalization" title="Whale vocalization">whale song</a>, to which researchers have found or applied the linguistic cognates of <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialect</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a>.</p>/n<p>All <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">language varieties</a> of <a href="/wiki/World_language" title="World language">world languages</a> are natural languages, although some varieties are subject to greater degrees of published <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">prescriptivism</a> or <a href="/wiki/List_of_language_regulators" title="List of language regulators">language regulation</a> than others. Thus <a href="/wiki/Nonstandard_dialect" title="Nonstandard dialect">nonstandard dialects</a> can be viewed as a <a href="/wiki/Wild_type" title="Wild type">wild type</a> in comparison with <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">standard languages</a>. But even an <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">official language</a> with a regulating academy, such as <a href="/wiki/Standard_French" title="Standard French">Standard French</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_fran%C3%A7aise" title="Académie française">French Academy</a>, is classified as a natural language (for example, in the field of <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_processing" title="Natural language processing">natural language processing</a>), as its prescriptive points do not make it either constructed enough to be classified as a <a href="/wiki/Constructed_language" title="Constructed language">constructed language</a> or controlled enough to be classified as a <a href="/wiki/Controlled_natural_language" title="Controlled natural language">controlled natural language</a>.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controlled_languages">Controlled languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Controlled languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Controlled_natural_language" title="Controlled natural language">Controlled natural language</a></div>/n<p>Controlled natural languages are subsets of natural languages whose grammars and dictionaries have been restricted in order to reduce or eliminate both ambiguity and complexity (for instance, by cutting down on rarely used superlative or adverbial forms or <a href="/wiki/Irregular_verbs" class="mw-redirect" title="Irregular verbs">irregular verbs</a>). The purpose behind the development and implementation of a controlled natural language typically is to aid non-native speakers of a natural language in understanding it, or to ease computer processing of a natural language. An example of a widely used controlled natural language is <a href="/wiki/Simplified_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Simplified English">Simplified English</a>, which was originally developed for <a href="/wiki/Aerospace" title="Aerospace">aerospace</a> industry maintenance manuals.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Constructed_languages_and_international_auxiliary_languages">Constructed languages and international auxiliary languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Constructed languages and international auxiliary languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Constructed_language" title="Constructed language">Constructed language</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_auxiliary_language" title="International auxiliary language">International auxiliary language</a></div>/n<p>Constructed <a href="/wiki/International_auxiliary_language" title="International auxiliary language">international auxiliary languages</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">Esperanto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interlingua" title="Interlingua">Interlingua</a> (even those that have <a href="/wiki/Native_speaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Native speaker">native speakers</a>) are not generally considered natural languages.<sup id="cite_ref-Gopsill.2C_F._P._1990_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gopsill.2C_F._P._1990-3">[3]</a></sup> Natural languages have been used to communicate and have evolved in a natural way, whereas Esperanto was designed by <a href="/wiki/L.L._Zamenhof" class="mw-redirect" title="L.L. Zamenhof">L.L. Zamenhof</a> selecting elements from natural languages, not grown from natural fluctuations in vocabulary and syntax. Some natural languages have become naturally "standardized" by children's natural tendency to correct for illogical grammatical structures in their parents' speech, which can be seen in the development of <a href="/wiki/Pidgin" title="Pidgin">pidgin</a> languages into <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">creole languages</a> (as explained by Steven Pinker in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Language_Instinct" title="The Language Instinct">The Language Instinct</a></i>), but this is not the case in many languages, including constructed languages such as Esperanto, where strict rules are in place as an attempt to consciously remove such irregularities. The possible exception to this are true native speakers of such languages.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> More substantive basis for this designation is that the vocabulary, grammar, and orthography of Interlingua are natural; they have been standardized and presented by a <a href="/wiki/International_Auxiliary_Language_Association" title="International Auxiliary Language Association">linguistic research body</a>, but they predated it and are not themselves considered a product of human invention.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Most experts, however, consider Interlingua to be naturalistic rather than natural.<sup id="cite_ref-Gopsill.2C_F._P._1990_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gopsill.2C_F._P._1990-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Latino_Sine_Flexione" class="mw-redirect" title="Latino Sine Flexione">Latino Sine Flexione</a>, a second naturalistic auxiliary language, is also naturalistic in content but is no longer widely spoken.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<ul>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Language_acquisition" title="Language acquisition">Language acquisition</a></li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Origin_of_language" title="Origin of language">Origin of language</a></li>/n</ul>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
