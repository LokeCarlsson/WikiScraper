See <a href="/wiki/Phrase_structure_rules" title="Phrase structure rules">phrase structure rules</a>.</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p><i><b>Colorless green ideas sleep furiously</b></i> is a sentence composed by <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a> in his 1957 book <i><a href="/wiki/Syntactic_Structures" title="Syntactic Structures">Syntactic Structures</a></i> as an example of a <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence (linguistics)">sentence</a> that is <a href="/wiki/Grammatically" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammatically">grammatically</a> correct, but <a href="/wiki/Semantically" class="mw-redirect" title="Semantically">semantically</a> <a href="/wiki/Nonsense" title="Nonsense">nonsensical</a>. The sentence was originally used in his 1955 thesis "Logical Structure of Linguistic Theory" and in his 1956 paper "Three Models for the Description of Language".<sup id="cite_ref-chomsky1956_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chomsky1956-1">[1]</a></sup> Although the sentence is grammatically correct, no obvious <a href="/wiki/Understandable" class="mw-redirect" title="Understandable">understandable</a> meaning can be derived from it, and thus it demonstrates the distinction between <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a> and <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantics</a>. As an example of a <a href="/wiki/Category_mistake" title="Category mistake">category mistake</a>, it was used to show the inadequacy of the then-popular probabilistic models of grammar, and the need for more structured models.</p>/n<p></p>/n<div id="toc" class="toc">/n<div class="toctitle">/n<h2>Contents</h2>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Details"><span id="Details">Details</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colorless_green_ideas_sleep_furiously&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Details">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Chomsky writes in his 1957 book <i><a href="/wiki/Syntactic_Structures" title="Syntactic Structures">Syntactic Structures</a></i>:</p>/n<blockquote class="templatequote">/n<ol>/n<li>Colorless green ideas sleep furiously.</li>/n<li>*Furiously sleep ideas green colorless.</li>/n</ol>/n<p>It is fair to assume that neither sentence (1) nor (2) (nor indeed any part of these sentences) has ever occurred in an English discourse. Hence, in any statistical model for grammaticalness, these sentences will be ruled out on identical grounds as equally "remote" from English. Yet (1), though nonsensical, is grammatical, while (2) is not grammatical.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>/n</blockquote>/n<p>While the meaninglessness of the sentence is often considered fundamental to Chomsky's point, Chomsky was only relying on the sentences having never been spoken before. Thus, even if one were to ascribe a likely and reasonable meaning to the sentence, the <a href="/wiki/Grammaticality" title="Grammaticality">grammaticality</a> of the sentence is concrete despite being the first time a person had ever uttered the statement, or any part thereof in such a combination. This was used then as a counter-example to the idea that the human speech engine was based upon statistical models, such as a <a href="/wiki/Markov_chain" title="Markov chain">Markov chain</a>, or simple statistics of words following others.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attempts_at_meaningful_interpretations">Attempts at meaningful interpretations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colorless_green_ideas_sleep_furiously&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Attempts at meaningful interpretations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>The sentence can be partially interpreted through <a href="/wiki/Polysemy" title="Polysemy">polysemy</a>. Both <i>green</i> and <i>colorless</i> have figurative meanings, which allow <i>colorless</i> to be interpreted as "nondescript" and <i>green</i> as either "immature" or pertaining to environmental consciousness. The sentence can therefore be construed as "nondescript immature ideas have violent nightmares", a phrase with less oblique semantics. In particular, the phrase can have legitimate <a href="/wiki/Meaning_(linguistic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Meaning (linguistic)">meaning</a> too, if <i>green</i> is understood to mean "newly formed" and <i>sleep</i> can be used to figuratively express mental or verbal dormancy. "Furiously" remains problematic when applied to the verb "sleep", since "furiously" denotes "angrily", "violently", and "intensely energetically", meanings which are generally incompatible with sleep, dormancy, and unconscious agents typically construed as conscious ones, e.g. animals or humans, which truly "sleep".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Writers have attempted to provide the sentence meaning through context, the first of which was written by Chinese linguist <a href="/wiki/Yuen_Ren_Chao" title="Yuen Ren Chao">Yuen Ren Chao</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1985, a literary competition was held at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> in which the contestants were invited to make Chomsky's sentence meaningful using not more than 100 words of prose or 14 lines of verse.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> An example entry from the competition, from C.M. Street, is:</p>/n<blockquote>/n<p>It can only be the thought of verdure to come, which prompts us in the autumn to buy these dormant white lumps of vegetable matter covered by a brown papery skin, and lovingly to plant them and care for them. It is a marvel to me that under this cover they are labouring unseen at such a rate within to give us the sudden awesome beauty of spring flowering bulbs. While winter reigns the earth reposes but these colourless green ideas sleep furiously.</p>/n</blockquote>/n<p>Using the triangle-box method, <i>Colorless green ideas sleep furiously</i> is divided into two phrases: [Colorless green ideas] and [sleep furiously] per the LINZ algorithm based on parts of intent. In the first phrase the word 'ideas' is the core (ZC), the main focus of the phrase. In the second phrase, there are no core but rather an action (TAP), which is the focus of the second phrase. Returning to the first phrase, the desire or intent of this phrase is 'ideas' (ZC). 'Colorless' and 'green' are boolean. Specifically description booleans (green) and its modifier (colorless). In the second phrase, 'sleep' is the action or intent of the phrase. Furiously is an intensity action type (ZAI). All triangle-box phrases consist of either a core, boolean and or TAP. This sentence contains the intent/desire or focus of ideas and sleep. It can be inferred that sleep when done furiously produces colorless green ideas or ideas that are not clear and concrete.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistical_challenges">Statistical challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colorless_green_ideas_sleep_furiously&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Statistical challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Fernando Pereira of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a> has fitted a simple statistical Markov model to a body of newspaper text, and shown that under this model, <i>Furiously sleep ideas green colorless</i> is about 200,000 times less probable than <i>Colorless green ideas sleep furiously</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<p>This statistical model defines a similarity metric, whereby sentences which are more like those within a corpus in certain respects are assigned higher values than sentences less alike. Pereira's model assigns an ungrammatical version of the same sentence a lower probability than the syntactically correct form demonstrating that statistical models can learn grammaticality distinctions with minimal linguistic assumptions. However, it is not clear that the model assigns every ungrammatical sentence a lower probability than every grammatical sentence. That is, <i>colorless green ideas sleep furiously</i> may still be statistically more "remote" from English than some ungrammatical sentences. To this, it may be argued that <i>no</i> current theory of grammar is capable of distinguishing <i>all</i> grammatical English sentences from ungrammatical ones.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_and_similar_examples">Related and similar examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colorless_green_ideas_sleep_furiously&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Related and similar examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>The pioneering <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntactician</a> <a href="/wiki/Lucien_Tesni%C3%A8re" title="Lucien Tesnière">Lucien Tesnière</a> came up with the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French language</a> sentence "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Le_silence_vert%C3%A9bral_indispose_la_voile_licite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Le silence vertébral indispose la voile licite (page does not exist)">Le silence vertébral indispose la voile licite</a>" ("The vertebral silence indisposes the licit sail").</p>/n<p>The game of <i><a href="/wiki/Exquisite_corpse" title="Exquisite corpse">exquisite corpse</a></i> is a method for generating nonsense sentences. It was named after the first sentence generated in the game in 1925 <i>Le cadavre exquis boira le vin nouveau</i> (the exquisite corpse will drink the new wine).</p>/n<p>In the popular game of "<a href="/wiki/Mad_Libs" title="Mad Libs">Mad Libs</a>", a chosen player asks each other player to provide parts of speech without providing any contextual information (e.g., "Give me a proper noun", or "Give me an adjective"), and these words are inserted into pre-composed sentences with a correct grammatical structure, but in which certain words have been omitted. The humor of the game is in the generation of sentences which are grammatical but which are meaningless or have absurd or ambiguous meanings (such as 'loud sharks'). The game also tends to generate humorous <a href="/wiki/Double_entendres" class="mw-redirect" title="Double entendres">double entendres</a>.</p>/n<p>There are likely earlier examples of such sentences, possibly from the philosophy of language literature, but not necessarily uncontroversial ones, given that the focus has been mostly on borderline cases. For example, followers of <a href="/wiki/Logical_positivism" title="Logical positivism">logical positivism</a> held that "metaphysical" (i.e. not <a href="/wiki/Empirical_validation" class="mw-redirect" title="Empirical validation">empirically verifiable</a>) statements are simply meaningless; e.g. <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Carnap" title="Rudolf Carnap">Rudolf Carnap</a> wrote an article where he argued that almost every sentence from <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heidegger" title="Martin Heidegger">Heidegger</a> was grammatically correct, yet meaningless. Of course, some philosophers who were not logical positivists disagreed with this.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (April 2013)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup></p>/n<p>The philosopher <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">Bertrand Russell</a> used the sentence "Quadruplicity drinks procrastination" in his “An Inquiry into Meaning and Truth” from 1940, to make a similar point; <a href="/wiki/W.V._Quine" class="mw-redirect" title="W.V. Quine">W.V. Quine</a> took issue with him on the grounds that for a sentence to be false is nothing more than for it not to be true; and since quadruplicity doesn't drink <i>anything</i>, the sentence is simply false, not meaningless.</p>/n<p>In a sketch about linguistics, British comedy duo <a href="/wiki/Fry_and_Laurie" title="Fry and Laurie">Fry and Laurie</a> used the nonsensical sentence "Hold the newsreader's nose squarely, waiter, or friendly milk will countermand my trousers."</p>/n<p><a href="/wiki/John_Hollander" title="John Hollander">John Hollander</a> wrote a poem titled <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://web.mit.edu/24.900/www/poem.html">"Coiled Alizarine"</a> in his book <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Night_Mirror&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Night Mirror (page does not exist)">The Night Mirror</a></i>. It ends with Chomsky's sentence.</p>/n<p><a href="/wiki/Clive_James" title="Clive James">Clive James</a> wrote a poem titled "<a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Line_and_a_Theme_from_Noam_Chomsky&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="A Line and a Theme from Noam Chomsky (page does not exist)">A Line and a Theme from Noam Chomsky</a>" in his book <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Other_Passports:_Poems_1958-1985&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Other Passports: Poems 1958-1985 (page does not exist)">Other Passports: Poems 1958-1985</a></i>. It opens with Chomsky's second meaningless sentence and discusses the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>.</p>/n<p>Another approach is to create a syntactically-correct, easily parsable sentence using nonsense words; a famous such example is "<a href="/wiki/Gostak" title="Gostak">The gostak distims the doshes</a>". <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Jabberwocky" title="Jabberwocky">Jabberwocky</a> is also famous for using this technique, although in this case for literary purposes; similar sentences used in neuroscience experiments are called <a href="/wiki/Jabberwocky_sentence" title="Jabberwocky sentence">Jabberwocky sentences</a>. In Russian schools of linguistics, the <i><a href="/wiki/Glokaya_kuzdra" title="Glokaya kuzdra">glokaya kuzdra</a></i> example has similar characteristics.</p>/n<p>Other arguably "meaningless" utterances are ones that make sense, are grammatical, but have no reference to the present state of the world, such as Russell's <i>The present <a href="/wiki/King_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="King of France">King of France</a> is bald</i> from <a href="/wiki/On_Denoting" title="On Denoting">On Denoting</a> (also see <a href="/wiki/Definite_description" title="Definite
