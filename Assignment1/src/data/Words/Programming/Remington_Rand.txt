class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Remington Rand</b> (1927–1955) was an early <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_machine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Business machine (page does not exist)">business machines</a> manufacturer, best known originally as a <a Building at 315 <a href="/wiki/Park_Avenue_(Manhattan)" class="mw-redirect" title="Park Avenue (Manhattan)">Park Avenue</a> South in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> is a 20-floor <a href="/wiki/Skyscraper" title="Skyscraper">skyscraper</a> completed in 1911.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>/n<p></p>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NorwalkCTRockLedgeRowayton08112007.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nRock Ledge estate in <a href="/wiki/Rowayton" title="Rowayton">Rowayton</a>, Connecticut, company headquarters from 1943 to 1964. Retired General <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Groves" title="Leslie Groves">Leslie Groves</a>, who had headed the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a>, served as chief of research and development during part of this time.</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:M1911A1.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/M1911A1.png/250px-M1911A1.png" width="250" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/M1911A1.png/375px-M1911A1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/M1911A1.png/500px-M1911A1.png 2x" data-file-width="1530" data-file-height="958" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:M1911A1.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nM1911A1 U.S. Army semi-automatic pistol manufactured by Remington Rand.</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>Remington Rand was formed in 1927 by the merger of the <a href="/wiki/Remington_Typewriter_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Remington Typewriter Company">Remington Typewriter Company</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Rand,_Jr.#Rand_Ledger_and_American_Kardex" class="mw-redirect" title="James Rand, Jr.">Rand Kardex Corporation</a>. One of its earliest factories, the former <a href="/wiki/Herschell%E2%80%93Spillman_Motor_Company_Complex" title="Herschell–Spillman Motor Company Complex">Herschell–Spillman Motor Company Complex</a>, was listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-NysNrhpNom_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NysNrhpNom-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nps_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nps-3">[3]</a></sup> Within the first year, Remington Rand acquired the Dalton Adding Machine Company, the <a href="/wiki/Powers_Accounting_Machine_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Powers Accounting Machine Company">Powers Accounting Machine Company</a>, the Baker-Vawter Company and the Kalamazoo Loose-Leaf Binder Company.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>/n<p>From 1942 to 1945, Remington Rand was one manufacturer of the <a href="/wiki/M1911A1" class="mw-redirect" title="M1911A1">M1911A1</a> .45 caliber semi-automatic pistol used by the <a href="/wiki/Military_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Military of the United States">United States Armed Forces</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Remington Rand produced more M1911A1 pistols than any other wartime manufacturer.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Remington Rand ranked 66th among United States corporations in the value of World War II military production contracts.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>/n<p>In 1950, Remington Rand acquired the <a href="/wiki/Eckert-Mauchly_Computer_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Eckert-Mauchly Computer Corporation">Eckert-Mauchly Computer Corporation</a>, founded by the makers of the <a href="/wiki/ENIAC" title="ENIAC">ENIAC</a>, and in 1952, they acquired <a href="/wiki/Engineering_Research_Associates" title="Engineering Research Associates">Engineering Research Associates</a> (ERA), both of which were pioneers in electronic computing. At that time, Remington Rand was one of the biggest computer companies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-RR1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RR1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Remington Rand was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Sperry_Corporation" title="Sperry Corporation">Sperry Corporation</a> in 1955 to form a company then known as Sperry Rand (later shortened to Sperry). However the brand name of "Remington Rand" remained as a subdivision for many years.<sup id="cite_ref-midtown_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-midtown-8">[8]</a></sup> Sperry merged in 1986 with <a href="/wiki/Burroughs_Corporation" title="Burroughs Corporation">Burroughs</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Unisys" title="Unisys">Unisys</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RR1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RR1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a id="Typewriters">Typewriters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Remington_Rand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit href="/wiki/File:Touch_typing_on_a_Remington.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Touch_typing_on_a_Remington.JPG/150px-Touch_typing_on_a_Remington.JPG" width="150" height="200" class="thumbimage" class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Touch_typing_on_a_Remington.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nA Remington "Quiet-Riter" made for the British domestic market in the late 1950s</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>Initially produced by <a href="/wiki/E._Remington_and_Sons" title="E. Remington and Sons">E. Remington and Sons</a>, the Remington Typewriters were the first to use the <a href="/wiki/QWERTY" title="QWERTY">QWERTY</a> keyboard layout. Remington had bought the design from <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Sholes" class="mw-redirect" title="Christopher Sholes">Christopher Sholes</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Sholes_and_Glidden_typewriter" title="Sholes and Glidden typewriter">Remington No.1</a> was the first model released. All keys were uppercase. Remington spun off Remington Typewriter Company in 1886, and after the 1927 merger, the Remington Rand Corp. continued to manufacture and sell typewriters.<sup id="cite_ref-RR2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RR2-9">[9]</a></sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_UNIVAC">The UNIVAC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Remington_Rand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The UNIVAC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>The <a href="/wiki/UNIVAC_I" title="UNIVAC I">UNIVAC I</a> <i>(UNIVersal Automatic Computer I)</i> was the second commercial computer made in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It was designed principally by <a href="/wiki/J._Presper_Eckert" title="J. Presper Eckert">J. Presper Eckert</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Mauchly" title="John Mauchly">John Mauchly</a>, the inventors of the <a href="/wiki/ENIAC" title="ENIAC">ENIAC</a>. Design work was begun by their company, <a href="/wiki/Eckert-Mauchly_Computer_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Eckert-Mauchly Computer Corporation">Eckert-Mauchly Computer Corporation</a>, and was completed after the company had been acquired by Remington Rand. (In the years before successor models of the UNIVAC I appeared, the machine was simply known as "the UNIVAC".)<sup id="cite_ref-RR2_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RR2-9">[9]</a></sup></p>/n<p>The first UNIVAC was delivered to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a> on March 31, 1951, and was dedicated on June 14 that year.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The fifth machine (built for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Atomic_Energy_Commission" title="United States Atomic Energy Commission">U.S. Atomic Energy Commission</a>) was used by <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> to predict the result of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1952" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. presidential election, 1952">1952 U.S. presidential election</a>. With a sample of 1% of the voting population it predicted Eisenhower's win.<sup id="cite_ref-RR2_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RR2-9">[9]</a></sup></p>/n<p>In 1949, Remington Rand designed the <a href="/wiki/Remington_Rand_409" title="Remington Rand 409">Remington Rand 409</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Plugboard" title="Plugboard">control panel</a> programmed <a href="/wiki/Punched_card" title="Punched card">punched card</a> calculator (but not introduced as a product until 1952 as the UNIVAC 60 then in 1953 as the UNIVAC 120 with double the memory).<sup id="cite_ref-RR2_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RR2-9">[9]</a></sup></p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_products">Other products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Remington_Rand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p>Remington Rand also made electric <a href="/wiki/Razor" title="Razor">razors</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Remington_Products" title="Remington Products">Remington</a> brand of razor was originally produced by a division of Remington Rand, starting in 1937. Sperry Corporation sold the division in 1979 to <a href="/wiki/Victor_Kiam" title="Victor Kiam">Victor Kiam</a>, who became the company spokesman of the new <a href="/wiki/Remington_Products" title="Remington Products">Remington Products</a> Company. His line, "I liked the shaver so much, I bought the company" became one of the more memorable advertising slogans of the early 1980s. Remington Products was sold in 2003 to the <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">battery</a> manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Rayovac" class="mw-redirect" title="Rayovac">Rayovac</a>. Rayovac is now <a href="/wiki/Spectrum_Brands" title="Spectrum Brands">Spectrum Brands</a>.</p>/n<p>They also sold <a href="/wiki/Punched_card" title="Punched card">punched card</a> systems, beginning with the 1928 acquisition of the <a href="/wiki/Powers_Accounting_Machine_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Powers Accounting Machine Company">Powers Accounting Machine Company</a> and ending in the 1950s.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Depiction_in_popular_culture">Depiction in popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Remington_Rand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Depiction in popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>The Remington Rand Co. and the Remington Rand Building are depicted as the Knox Co. and the Knox Building in <a href="/wiki/Richard_Yates_(novelist)" title="Richard Yates (novelist)">Richard Yates</a>' 1961 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_Road" title="Revolutionary Road">Revolutionary Road</a></i>.</p>/n<p>In 1921, Rand Kardex sponsored the <a href="/wiki/Tonawanda_Kardex" class="mw-redirect" title="Tonawanda Kardex">Tonawanda Kardex</a> all-star team of <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">football</a> players from <a href="/wiki/City_of_Tonawanda" class="mw-redirect" title="City of Tonawanda">Tonawanda, New York</a>; known to have formed in 1916 and coached for its entire existence by <a href="/wiki/Tam_Rose" title="Tam Rose">Tam Rose</a>. The team joined the NFL that season but folded after playing in just one game as a league member.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>/n<p>The novelist-philosopher <a href="/wiki/Ayn_Rand" title="Ayn Rand">Ayn Rand</a> said that she chose her Americanized name based on her Remington Rand typewriter.</p>/n<p>The 1980s television series <i><a href="/wiki/Remington_Steele" title="Remington Steele">Remington Steele</a></i> had Laura Holt (<a href="/wiki/Stephanie_Zimbalist" title="Stephanie Zimbalist">Stephanie Zimbalist</a>) draw the personal name for her detective agency's fictitious male chief-executive official (whose identity <a href="/wiki/Pierce_Brosnan" title="Pierce Brosnan">Pierce Brosnan</a>'s character assumed in the first installment after discovering her elaborate ruse) from her old Remington typewriter.
