<b>American National Standards Institute</b> (<b>ANSI</b>, <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/iː/ or /ɪ/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><small title="English pronunciation respelling"><i><b>AN</b>-see</i></small></a>) is a private <a href="/wiki/Non-profit_organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-profit organization">non-profit organization</a> that oversees the development of <a href="/wiki/Standardization" title="Standardization">voluntary consensus standards</a> for products, services, processes, systems, and personnel in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The organization also coordinates U.S. standards with international standards so that American products can be used worldwide.</p>/n<p>ANSI accredits standards that are developed by representatives of other <a href="/wiki/Standards_organization" title="Standards organization">standards organizations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Government_agency" title="Government agency">government agencies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Consumer_organization" title="Consumer organization">consumer groups</a>, companies, and others. These standards ensure that the characteristics and performance of products are consistent, that people use the same definitions and terms, and that products are tested the same way. ANSI also accredits organizations that carry out product or personnel certification in accordance with requirements defined in international standards.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>/n<p>The organization's headquarters are in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> ANSI's operations office is located in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. The ANSI annual operating budget is funded by the sale of publications, membership dues and fees, accreditation services, fee-based programs, and international standards programs.</p>/n<p></p>/n<div href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">7</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>/n</ul>/n</div>/n<p></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_National_Standards_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>ANSI was originally formed in 1918, when five engineering societies and three government agencies founded the <b>American Engineering Standards Committee</b> (<b>AESC</b>).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1928, the AESC became the <b>American Standards Association</b> (<b>ASA</b>). In 1966, the ASA was reorganized and became <b>United States of America Standards Institute</b> (<b>USASI</b>). The present name was adopted in 1969.</p>/n<p>Prior to 1918, these five founding engineering societies:</p>/n<ul>/n<li><a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Electrical_Engineers" title="American Institute of Electrical Engineers">American Institute of Electrical Engineers</a> (AIEE, now <a href="/wiki/IEEE" class="mw-redirect" title="IEEE">IEEE</a>)</li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Mechanical_Engineers" class="mw-redirect" title="American Society of Mechanical Engineers">American Society of Mechanical Engineers</a> (ASME)</li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Civil_Engineers" title="American Society of Civil Engineers">American Society of Civil Engineers</a> (ASCE)</li>/n<li>American Institute of Mining Engineers (AIME, now <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Mining,_Metallurgical,_and_Petroleum_Engineers" title="American Institute of Mining, Metallurgical, and Petroleum Engineers">American Institute of Mining, Metallurgical, and Petroleum Engineers</a>)</li>/n<li>American Society for Testing and Materials (now <a href="/wiki/ASTM_International" title="ASTM International">ASTM International</a>)</li>/n</ul>/n<p>had been members of the United Engineering Society (UES). At the behest of the AIEE, they invited the U.S. government Departments of War, Navy (combined in 1947 to become the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> or DOD) and Commerce<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> to join in founding a national standards organization.</p>/n<p>According to Adam Stanton, the first permanent secretary and head of staff in 1919, AESC started as an ambitious program and little else. Staff for the first year consisted of one executive, Clifford B. LePage, who was on loan from a founding member, ASME. An annual budget of $7,500 was provided by the founding bodies.</p>/n<p>In 1931, the organization (renamed ASA in 1928) became affiliated with the U.S. National Committee of the <a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">International Electrotechnical Commission</a> (<a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">IEC</a>), which had been formed in 1904 to develop electrical and electronics standards.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Members">Members</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_National_Standards_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Members">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>ANSI's members are government agencies, organizations, corporations, academic and international bodies, and individuals. In total, the Institute represents the interests of more than 125,000 companies and 3.5 million professionals.<sup id="cite_ref-membership_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-membership-2">[2]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Process">Process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_National_Standards_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Though ANSI itself does not develop standards, the Institute oversees the development and use of standards by accrediting the procedures of standards developing organizations. ANSI accreditation signifies that the procedures used by standards developing organizations meet the Institute's requirements for openness, balance, consensus, and due process.</p>/n<p>ANSI also designates specific standards as American National Standards, or ANS, when the Institute determines that the standards were developed in an environment that is equitable, accessible and responsive to the requirements of various stakeholders.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Voluntary consensus standards quicken the market acceptance of products while making clear how to improve the safety of those products for the protection of consumers. There are approximately 9,500 American National Standards that carry the ANSI designation.</p>/n<p>The American National Standards process involves:</p>/n<ul>/n<li>consensus by a group that is open to representatives from all interested parties</li>/n<li>broad-based public review and comment on draft standards</li>/n<li>consideration of and response to comments</li>/n<li>incorporation of submitted changes that meet the same consensus requirements into a draft standard</li>/n<li>availability of an appeal by any participant alleging that these principles were not respected during the standards-development process.</li>/n</ul>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_activities">International activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_National_Standards_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: International activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>In addition to facilitating the formation of standards in the United States, ANSI promotes the use of U.S. standards internationally, advocates U.S. policy and technical positions in international and regional standards organizations, and encourages the adoption of international standards as national standards where appropriate.</p>/n<p>The Institute is the official U.S. representative to the two major international standards organizations, the <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> (ISO), as a founding member,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">International Electrotechnical Commission</a> (IEC), via the U.S. National Committee (USNC). ANSI participates in almost the entire technical program of both the ISO and the IEC, and administers many key committees and subgroups. In many instances, U.S. standards are taken forward to ISO and IEC, through ANSI or the USNC, where they are adopted in whole or in part as international standards.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Standards_panels">Standards panels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_National_Standards_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Standards panels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<p><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The Institute administers nine standards panels:</p>/n<ul>/n<li>ANSI Homeland Defense and Security Standardization Collaborative (HDSSC)</li>/n<li>ANSI Nanotechnology Standards Panel (ANSI-NSP)</li>/n<li>ID Theft Prevention and ID Management Standards Panel (IDSP)</li>/n<li>ANSI Energy Efficiency Standardization Coordination Collaborative (EESCC)</li>/n<li>Nuclear Energy Standards Coordination Collaborative (NESCC)</li>/n<li>Electric Vehicles Standards Panel (EVSP)</li>/n<li>ANSI-NAM Network on Chemical Regulation</li>/n<li>ANSI Biofuels Standards Coordination Panel</li>/n<li>Healthcare Information Technology Standards Panel (HITSP)</li>/n</ul>/n<p>Each of the panels works to identify, coordinate, and harmonize voluntary standards relevant to these areas.</p>/n<p>In 2009, ANSI and the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> (NIST) formed the Nuclear Energy Standards Coordination Collaborative (NESCC). NESCC is a joint initiative to identify and respond to the current need for standards in the nuclear industry.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_national_standards">American national standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_National_Standards_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: American national standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<ul>/n<li>The <a href="/wiki/ASA_film_speed" class="mw-redirect" title="ASA film speed">ASA</a> (as for American Standards Association) photographic exposure system, originally defined in ASA Z38.2.1 (since 1943) and ASA PH2.5 (since 1954), together with the <a href="/wiki/DIN_film_speed" class="mw-redirect" title="DIN film speed">DIN system (DIN 4512 since 1934)</a>, became the basis for the <a href="/wiki/ISO_film_speed" class="mw-redirect" title="ISO film speed">ISO</a> system (since 1974), currently used worldwide (<a href="/wiki/ISO_6" class="mw-redirect" title="ISO 6">ISO 6</a>, <a href="/wiki/ISO_2240" class="mw-redirect" title="ISO 2240">ISO 2240</a>, <a href="/wiki/ISO_5800" class="mw-redirect" title="ISO 5800">ISO 5800</a>, <a href="/wiki/ISO_12232" class="mw-redirect" title="ISO 12232">ISO 12232</a>).</li>/n<li>A standard for the set of values used to represent characters in digital computers. The ANSI code standard extended the previously created <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a> seven bit code standard (ASA X3.4-1963), with additional codes for European alphabets (see also <a href="/wiki/Extended_Binary_Coded_Decimal_Interchange_Code" class="mw-redirect" title="Extended Binary Coded Decimal Interchange Code">Extended Binary Coded Decimal Interchange Code</a> or EBCDIC). In <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>, the phrase "ANSI" refers to the <a href="/wiki/Windows_code_page" title="Windows code page">Windows ANSI code pages</a> (even though they are not ANSI standards).<sup id="cite_ref-ms_glossary_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ms_glossary-11">[11]</a></sup> Most of these are fixed width, though some characters for <a href="/wiki/Ideographic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ideographic language">ideographic languages</a> are variable width. Since these characters are based on a draft of the <a href="/wiki/ISO-8859" class="mw-redirect" title="ISO-8859">ISO-8859</a> series, some of Microsoft's symbols are visually very similar to the ISO symbols, leading many to falsely assume that they are identical.</li>/n<li>The first computer <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming language</a> standard was "American Standard <a href="/wiki/Fortran" title="Fortran">Fortran</a>" (informally known as "FORTRAN 66"), approved in March 1966 and published as ASA X3.9-1966.</li>/n<li>The programming language <a href="/wiki/COBOL" title="COBOL">COBOL</a> had ANSI standards in 1968, 1974, and 1985. The COBOL 2002 standard was issued by <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a>.</li>/n<li>The original standard implementation of the programming language <a href="/wiki/C_(computer_language)" class="mw-redirect" title="C (computer language)">C</a> was standardized as ANSI X3.159-1989, becoming the well-known <a href="/wiki/ANSI_C" title="ANSI C">ANSI C</a>.</li>/n<li>A popular <a href="/wiki/Unified_Thread_Standard" title="Unified Thread Standard">Unified Thread Standard</a> for nuts and bolts is ANSI/ASME B1.1 which was defined in 1935, 1949, 1989, and 2003.</li>/n<li>The ANSI-NSF International standards used for commercial kitchens, such as restaurants, cafeterias, delis, etc.</li>/n<li>The ANSI/APSP (Association of Pool &amp; Spa Professionals) standards used for pools, spas, hot tubs, barriers, and suction entrapment avoidance.</li>/n<li>The ANSI/HI (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydraulic_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hydraulic Institute (page does not exist)">Hydraulic Institute</a>) standards used for pumps.</li>/n<li>The ANSI for <a href="/wiki/Eye_protection" title="Eye protection">eye protection</a> is Z87.1, which gives a specific impact resistance rating to the eyewear. This standard is commonly used for shop glasses, shooting glasses, and many other examples of protective eyewear.</li>/n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Paper_size#ANSI_paper_sizes" title="Paper size">ANSI paper sizes</a> (ANSI/ASME Y14.1).</li>/n</ul>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_initiatives">Other initiatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_National_Standards_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other initiatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>/n<ul>/n<li>In 2008 ANSI, in partnership<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Citation_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Citation Technologies (page does not exist)">Citation Technologies</a>, created the first dynamic, online web library for <a href="/wiki/ISO_14000" title="ISO 14000">ISO 14000</a> standards.<sup id="cite_ref-iPackages_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iPackages-13">[13]</a></sup></li>/n<li>On June 23, 2009, ANSI announced a product and services agreement with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Citation_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Citation Technologies (page does not exist)">Citation Technologies</a> to deliver all ISO Standards on a web-based platform. Through the ANSI-Citation partnership, 17,765 International Standards developed by more than 3,000 ISO technical bodies will be made available on the citation platform, arming subscribers with powerful search tools and collaboration, notification, and change-management functionality.<sup id="cite_ref-ANSI_Press_Release_07.23.09_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ANSI_Press_Release_07.23.09-14">[14]</a></sup></li>/n<li>ANSI, in partnership with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Citation_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Citation Technologies (page does not exist)">Citation Technologies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Medical_Instrumentation" title="Association for the Advancement of Medical Instrumentation">AAMI</a>, <a href="/wiki/ASTM" class="mw-redirect" title="ASTM">ASTM</a>, and <a href="/wiki/DIN" class="mw-redirect" title="DIN">DIN</a>, created a single, centralized database for medical device standards on September 9, 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-Medical_Device_Standards_Press_Release_09.2F09.2F09_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Medical_Device_Standards_Press_Release_09.2F09.2F09-15">[15]</a></sup></li>/n<li>In early 2009, ANSI launched a new Certificate Accreditation Program (ANSI-CAP) to provide neutral, third-party attestation that a given certificate program meets the American National Standard ASTM E2659-09.</li>/n<li>In 2009, ANSI began accepting applications for certification bodies seeking accreditation according to requirements defined under the Toy Safety Certification Program (TSCP) as the official third-party accreditor of TSCP’s product certification bodies.</li>/n<li>In 2006, ANSI launched <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.standardsportal.org/">www.StandardsPortal.org</a>, an online resource for facilitating more open and efficient trade between international markets in the areas of standards, conformity assessment, and technical regulations. The site currently features content for China, India, and Korea, with additional countries and regions planned for future content.</li>/n<li>ANSI design standards have also been incorporated into building codes encompassing several specific building sub-sets, such as the ANSI/SPRI ES-1, which pertains to "Wind Design Standard for Edge Systems Used With Low Slope Roofing Systems", for example.<sup id="cite_ref-The_ANSI.2FSPRI_ES-1_Standard_Explained_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_ANSI.2FSPRI_ES-1_Standard_Explained-16">[16]</a></sup></li>/n</ul>/n<h2><span
