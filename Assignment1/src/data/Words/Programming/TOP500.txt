href="/wiki/Distributed_computing" title="Distributed computing">distributed</a> <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a> systems in the world. The project was started in 1993 and publishes an updated list of the <a href="/wiki/Supercomputer" title="Supercomputer">supercomputers</a> twice a year. The first of these updates always coincides with the <a href="/wiki/International_Supercomputing_Conference" title="International Supercomputing Conference">International Supercomputing Conference</a> in June, and the second is presented at the <a href="/wiki/ACM/IEEE_Supercomputing_Conference" title="ACM/IEEE Supercomputing Conference">ACM/IEEE Supercomputing Conference</a> in November. The project aims to provide a reliable basis for tracking and detecting trends in high-performance computing and bases rankings on HPL,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> a portable implementation of the high-performance <a href="/wiki/LINPACK" title="LINPACK">LINPACK</a> <a href="/wiki/Benchmark_(computing)" title="Benchmark (computing)">benchmark</a> written in Fortran for distributed-memory computers. In the most recent list (November 2016), the <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> <a href="/wiki/Sunway_TaihuLight" title="Sunway TaihuLight">Sunway TaihuLight</a> is the world's most powerful supercomputer, reaching 93.015 peta<a href="/wiki/FLOPS" title="FLOPS">FLOPS</a> on the <a href="/wiki/LINPACK_benchmarks" title="LINPACK benchmarks">LINPACK benchmarks</a>.</p>/n<p>The TOP500 list is compiled by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Dongarra" title="Jack Dongarra">Jack Dongarra</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tennessee" title="University of Tennessee">University of Tennessee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Knoxville,_Tennessee" title="Knoxville, Tennessee">Knoxville</a>, Erich Strohmaier and Horst Simon of the <a href="/wiki/National_Energy_Research_Scientific_Computing_Center" title="National Energy Research Scientific Computing Center">National Energy Research Scientific Computing Center</a> (NERSC) and <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Berkeley_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory">Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory</a> (LBNL), and from 1993 until his death in 2014, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Meuer" title="Hans Meuer">Hans Meuer</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Mannheim" title="University of Mannheim">University of Mannheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" top500.org site. The logarithmic <i>y</i>-axis shows performance in G<a href="/wiki/FLOPS" title="FLOPS">FLOPS</a>./n<div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color:DarkBlue; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Combined performance of 500 largest supercomputers</div>/n<div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color:Red; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Fastest supercomputer</div>/n<div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color:Yellow; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Supercomputer in 500th place</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>In the early 1990s, a new definition of supercomputer was needed to produce meaningful statistics. After experimenting with metrics based on processor count in 1992, the idea arose at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Mannheim" title="University of Mannheim">University of Mannheim</a> to use a detailed listing of installed systems as the basis. In early 1993, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Dongarra" title="Jack Dongarra">Jack Dongarra</a> was persuaded to join the project with his <a href="/wiki/LINPACK_benchmarks" title="LINPACK benchmarks">LINPACK benchmarks</a>. A first test version was produced in May 1993, partly based on data available on the Internet, including the following sources:<sup id="cite_ref-hpcwire_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hpcwire-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-netlib_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-netlib-3">[3]</a></sup></p>/n<ul>/n<li>"List of the World's Most Powerful Computing Sites" maintained by Gunter Ahrendt<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/David_Kahaner" title="David Kahaner">David Kahaner</a>, the director of the Asian Technology Information Program (ATIP);<sup id="cite_ref-kahaner_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kahaner-5">[5]</a></sup> published a report in 1992, titled <i>"Kahaner Report on Supercomputer in Japan"</i><sup id="cite_ref-netlib_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-netlib-3">[3]</a></sup> which had an immense amount of data.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>/n</ul>/n<p>The information from those sources was used for the first two lists. Since June 1993, the TOP500 is produced bi-annually based on site and vendor submissions only.</p>/n<p>Since 1993, performance of the #1 ranked position has grown steadily in accord with <a href="/wiki/Moore%27s_law" title="Moore's law">Moore's law</a>, doubling roughly every 14 months. As of November 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=TOP500&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tianhe-2" title="Tianhe-2">Tianhe-2</a></i> was fastest with an Rpeak<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> of 54.9024&#160;P<a href="/wiki/FLOPS" title="FLOPS">FLOPS</a>, is over 419,102 times faster than the fastest system in November 1993, the <a href="/wiki/Connection_Machine" title="Connection Machine">Connection Machine</a> CM-5/1024 (1024 cores) with Rpeak of 131.0&#160;G<a href="/wiki/FLOPS" title="FLOPS">FLOPS</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>/n<div style="clear:both;"></div>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture_and_operating_systems">Architecture and operating systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TOP500&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Architecture and operating systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>In June 2016, a Chinese computer made the top based on <a href="/wiki/SW26010" title="SW26010">SW26010</a> processors, a new, radically modified, model in the <a href="/wiki/Sunway" title="Sunway">Sunway</a> (or ShenWei) line.</p>/n<p>As of November 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=TOP500&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, TOP500 supercomputers are now all <a href="/wiki/64-bit_computing" title="64-bit computing">64-bit</a>, mostly based on <a href="/wiki/X86-64" title="X86-64">x86-64</a> <a href="/wiki/CPU" class="mw-redirect" title="CPU">CPUs</a> (<a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> <a href="/wiki/EMT64" class="mw-redirect" title="EMT64">EMT64</a> and <a href="/wiki/AMD" class="mw-redirect" title="AMD">AMD</a> <a href="/wiki/AMD64" class="mw-redirect" title="AMD64">AMD64</a> <a href="/wiki/Instruction_set_architecture" title="Instruction set architecture">instruction set architecture</a>), with few exceptions (all based on <a href="/wiki/Reduced_instruction_set_computing" class="mw-redirect" title="Reduced instruction set computing">reduced instruction set computing</a> (RISC) architectures) including 22 supercomputers based on <a href="/wiki/Power_Architecture" title="Power Architecture">Power Architecture</a> used by <a href="/wiki/IBM_POWER_microprocessors" title="IBM POWER microprocessors">IBM POWER microprocessors</a>, seven <a href="/wiki/SPARC" title="SPARC">SPARC</a> (all with <a href="/wiki/Fujitsu" title="Fujitsu">Fujitsu</a>-designed SPARC-chips, one of which surprisingly made the top in 2011 without a <a href="/wiki/Graphics_processing_unit" title="Graphics processing unit">GPU</a>, currently ranked seventh), and two, seemingly related, Chinese designs: the <a href="/wiki/ShenWei" class="mw-redirect" title="ShenWei">ShenWei</a>-based (ranked 11 in 2011, ranked 158th in November 2016) and <a href="/wiki/Sunway#Sunway_SW26010" title="Sunway">Sunway SW26010</a>-based ranked 1 in 2016, making up the remainder (another non-US design is PEZY-SC, while it's an accelerator paired with Intel's Xeon). Before the ascendance of <a href="/wiki/32-bit" title="32-bit">32-bit</a> <a href="/wiki/X86" title="X86">x86</a> and later <a href="/wiki/64-bit_computing" title="64-bit computing">64-bit</a> <a href="/wiki/X86-64" title="X86-64">x86-64</a> in the early 2000s, a variety of RISC processor families made up most TOP500 supercomputers, including RISC architectures such as <a href="/wiki/SPARC" title="SPARC">SPARC</a>, <a href="/wiki/MIPS_architecture" title="MIPS architecture">MIPS</a>, <a href="/wiki/PA-RISC" title="PA-RISC">PA-RISC</a> and <a href="/wiki/DEC_Alpha" title="DEC Alpha">Alpha</a>.</p>/n<p>In recent years <a href="/wiki/Heterogeneous_computing" title="Heterogeneous computing">heterogeneous computing</a>, mostly using <a href="/wiki/Nvidia" title="Nvidia">Nvidia</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Graphics_processing_unit" title="Graphics processing unit">graphics processing units</a> (GPU) as <a href="/wiki/Coprocessor" title="Coprocessor">coprocessors</a>, has become a popular way to reach a better <a href="/wiki/Performance_per_watt" title="Performance per watt">performance per watt</a> ratio and higher absolute performance; it is almost required for good performance and to make the top (or top 10), with some exceptions, such as the mentioned SPARC computer without any coprocessors. An x86-based coprocessor, <a href="/wiki/Xeon_Phi" title="Xeon Phi">Xeon Phi</a>, has also been used.</p>/n<div class="center">/n<div class="thumb tnone">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Processor_families_in_TOP500_supercomputers.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Processor_families_in_TOP500_supercomputers.svg/600px-Processor_families_in_TOP500_supercomputers.svg.png" width="600" height="426" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Processor_families_in_TOP500_supercomputers.svg/900px-Processor_families_in_TOP500_supercomputers.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Processor_families_in_TOP500_supercomputers.svg/1200px-Processor_families_in_TOP500_supercomputers.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1179" data-file-height="838" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Processor_families_in_TOP500_supercomputers.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nShare of processor architecture families in TOP500 supercomputers by time trend</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>All the fastest supercomputers in the decade since the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Simulator" title="Earth Simulator">Earth Simulator</a> supercomputer have used operating systems based on <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>. As of June 2017<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=TOP500&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, 498 or 99.6% of the world's fastest supercomputers (99.88% of the performance share) use the <a href="/wiki/Linux_kernel" title="Linux kernel">Linux kernel</a>. The two computers, Sierra and <a href="/wiki/Summit_(supercomputer)" title="Summit (supercomputer)">Summit</a>, will each exceed Tianhe-2's 55 peak petaflops. Summit, the more powerful of the two, will deliver 150–300 peak petaflops.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> On 10 April 2015, US government agencies banned selling chips, from Nvidia, to supercomputing centers in China as "acting contrary to the <a href="/wiki/National_security" title="National security">national security</a>... interests of the United States";<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and Intel Corporation from providing Xeon chips to China due to their use, according to the US, in researching nuclear weapons&#160;– research to which US <a href="/wiki/Export_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Export control">export control</a> law bans US companies from contributing&#160;– "The Department of Commerce refused, saying it was concerned about nuclear research being done with the machine."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>/n<p>On 29 July 2015, <a href="/wiki/President_Obama" class="mw-redirect" title="President Obama">President Obama</a> signed an executive order creating a <a href="/wiki/National_Strategic_Computing_Initiative" title="National Strategic Computing Initiative">National Strategic Computing Initiative</a> calling for the accelerated development of an <a href="/wiki/Exascale_computing" title="Exascale computing">exascale</a> (1000 petaflop) system and funding research into post-semiconductor computing.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>/n<p>In June 2016, Japanese firm Fujitsu announced at the <a href="/wiki/International_Supercomputing_Conference" title="International Supercomputing Conference">International Supercomputing Conference</a> that its future <a href="/wiki/Exascale_computing" title="Exascale computing">exascale</a> supercomputer will feature processors of its own design that implement the <a href="/wiki/ARMv8" class="mw-redirect" title="ARMv8">ARMv8</a> architecture. The Flagship2020 program, by Fujitsu for RIKEN plans to break the exaflops barrier by 2020 (and "it looks like China and France have a chance to do so and that the United States is content&#160;– for the moment at least&#160;– to wait until 2023 to break through the exaflops barrier."<sup id="cite_ref-Flagship2020_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Flagship2020-23">[23]</a></sup>) These processors will also implement extensions to the ARMv8 architecture equivalent to HPC-ACE2 that Fujitsu is developing with <a href="/wiki/ARM_Holdings" class="mw-redirect" title="ARM Holdings">ARM Holdings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Flagship2020_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Flagship2020-23">[23]</a></sup></p>/n<p><a href="/wiki/Inspur" title="Inspur">Inspur</a> has been one of the largest HPC system manufacturer based out of <a href="/wiki/Jinan" title="Jinan">Jinan</a>, China. As of May 2017, <a href="/wiki/Inspur" title="Inspur">Inspur</a> has become the third manufacturer to have manufactured 64 way system – a record which has been previously mastered by <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hewlett_Packard_Enterprise" title="Hewlett Packard Enterprise">HP</a>. The company has registered over $10B in revenues and have successfully provided a number of HPC systems to countries outside China such as Sudan, Zimbabwe, Saudi Arab, Venezuela. Inspur was also a major technology partner behind both the supercomputers from China, namely <a href="/wiki/Tianhe-2" title="Tianhe-2">Tianhe-2</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sunway_TaihuLight" title="Sunway TaihuLight">Taihu</a> which leads the top 2 positions of Top500 supercomputer list of December 2016. Inspur and <a href="/wiki/Supermicro" class="mw-redirect" title="Supermicro">Supermicro</a> released a few platforms aimed at HPC using GPU such as SR-AI and AGX-2 in May 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Large_machines_not_on_the_list">Large machines not on the list</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=TOP500&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Large machines not on the list">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Some major systems are not listed on the list. The largest example is the NCSA's <a href="/wiki/Blue_Waters" title="Blue Waters">Blue Waters</a> which publicly announced the decision not to participate in the list<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> because they do not feel it accurately indicates the ability for any system to be able to do useful work.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Other organizations decide not to list systems for security and/or commercial competitiveness reasons. Additional purpose-built machines that are not capable or do not run the benchmark were not included, such as <a href="/wiki/RIKEN_MDGRAPE-3" title="RIKEN MDGRAPE-3">RIKEN MDGRAPE-3</a> and <a href="/wiki/MDGRAPE-4" title="MDGRAPE-4">MDGRAPE-4</a>.</p>/n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computers_and_architectures_that_drop_off_the_list">Computers and architectures that drop off the class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> is no longer on the list (or any other using the <a href="/wiki/Cell_(microprocessor)" title="Cell (microprocessor)">Cell</a> coprocessor, or <a href="/wiki/PowerXCell" class="mw-redirect" title="PowerXCell">PowerXCell</a> as in the Roadrunner supercomputer), but it is an example of a computer that would easily be included, if it had not been decommissioned, as it is faster than the one ranked 500th.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Conversely, computers, such as the <a title="Itanium">Itanium</a> based systems (including the one which reached second rank in 2004<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>)<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and (non-<a href="/wiki/SIMD" title="SIMD">SIMD</a>-style) <a href="/wiki/Vector_processor" title="Vector processor">vector processors</a> (NEC-based such as the <a href="/wiki/Earth_simulator" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth simulator">Earth simulator</a> that was fastest in 2002<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a
