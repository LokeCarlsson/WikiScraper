class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disk_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p><a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">Audio</a> information was originally recorded by analog methods (see <a href="/wiki/Sound_recording_and_reproduction" title="Sound recording and reproduction">Sound recording and reproduction</a>). Similarly the <a href="/wiki/Laserdisc" class="mw-redirect" title="Laserdisc">first video disc</a> used an analog recording method. In the music industry, analog recording has been mostly replaced by digital <a href="/wiki/Optical" class="mw-redirect" title="Optical">optical</a> technology where the data are recorded in a digital format with optical information.</p>/n<p>The first commercial digital disk storage device was the <a href="/wiki/IBM_350" class="mw-redirect" title="IBM 350">IBM 350</a> which shipped in 1956 as a part of the <a href="/wiki/IBM_305_RAMAC" title="IBM 305 RAMAC">IBM 305 RAMAC</a> computing system. The <a href="/wiki/Random_access_memory" class="mw-redirect" title="Random access memory">random-access</a>, low-density storage of disks was developed to complement the already used <a href="/wiki/Sequential_access" title="Sequential access">sequential-access</a>, high-density storage provided by <a href="/wiki/Tape_drive" title="Tape drive">tape drives</a> using <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_tape" title="Magnetic tape">magnetic tape</a>. Vigorous innovation in disk storage technology, coupled with less vigorous innovation in tape storage, has reduced the difference in acquisition cost per <a href="/wiki/Terabyte" title="Terabyte">terabyte</a> between disk storage and tape storage; however, the total cost of ownership of data on disk including power and management remains larger than that of tape.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Disk storage is now used in both computer storage and consumer electronic storage, e.g., <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">audio CDs</a> and video discs (<a href="/wiki/DVD-ROM" class="mw-redirect" title="DVD-ROM">standard DVD</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" class="mw-redirect" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a>).</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Access_methods">Access methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disk_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Access methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Digital disk drives are <a href="/wiki/Computer_data_storage" title="Computer data storage">block storage devices</a>. Each disk is divided into logical <a href="/wiki/Block_(data_storage)" title="Block (data storage)">blocks</a> (collection of sectors). Blocks are addressed using their <a href="/wiki/Logical_block_addressing" title="Logical block addressing">logical block addresses</a> (LBA). Read from or writing to disk happens at the granularity of blocks.</p>/n<p>Originally the disk capacity was quite low and has been improved in one of several ways. Improvements in mechanical design and manufacture allowed smaller and more precise heads, meaning that more tracks could be stored on each of the disks. Advancements in data compression methods permitted more information to be stored in each of the individual sectors.</p>/n<p>The drive stores data onto <a href="/wiki/Cylinder-head-sector" title="Cylinder-head-sector">cylinders, heads, and sectors</a>. The sectors unit is the smallest size of data to be stored in a hard disk drive and each file will have many sectors units assigned to it. The smallest entity in a CD is called a frame, which consists of 33 bytes and contains six complete 16-bit stereo samples (two bytes × two channels × six samples = 24 bytes). The other nine bytes consist of eight CIRC error-correction bytes and one <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc_subcode" class="mw-redirect" title="Compact disc subcode">subcode</a> byte used for control and display.</p>/n<p>The information is sent from the computer processor to the BIOS into a chip controlling the data transfer. This is then sent out to the hard drive via a multi-wire connector. Once the data are received onto the circuit board of the drive, they are translated and compressed into a format that the individual drive can use to store onto the disk itself. The data are then passed to a chip on the circuit board that controls the access to the drive. The drive is divided into sectors of data stored onto one of the sides of one of the internal disks. An HDD with two disks internally will typically store data on all four surfaces.</p>/n<p>The hardware on the drive tells the actuator arm where it is to go for the relevant track and the compressed information is then sent down to the head which changes the physical properties, optically or magnetically for example, of each byte on the drive, thus storing the information. A file is not stored in a linear manner, rather, it is held in the best way for quickest retrieval.</p>/n<p><span id="CAV-CLV"></span></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rotation_speed_and_track_layout">Rotation speed and track layout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disk_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rotation speed and track layout">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<div class="thumb tright">/n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Comparison_disk_storage.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Comparison_disk_storage.svg/300px-Comparison_disk_storage.svg.png" width="300" height="300" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Comparison_disk_storage.svg/450px-Comparison_disk_storage.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Comparison_disk_storage.svg/600px-Comparison_disk_storage.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="512" /></a>/n<div class="thumbcaption">/n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Comparison_disk_storage.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nComparison of several forms of disk storage showing tracks (not-to-scale); green denotes start and red denotes end.<br />/n* Some CD-R(W) and DVD-R(W)/DVD+R(W) recorders operate in ZCLV, CAA or CAV modes.</div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>Mechanically there are two different motions occurring inside the drive. One is the rotation of the disks inside the device. The other is the side-to-side motion of the head across the disk as it moves between tracks.</p>/n<p>There are two types of disk rotation methods:</p>/n<ul>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Constant_linear_velocity" title="Constant linear velocity">constant linear velocity</a> (used mainly in optical storage) varies the rotational speed of the optical disc depending upon the position of the head, and</li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Constant_angular_velocity" title="Constant angular velocity">constant angular velocity</a> (used in HDDs, standard FDDs, a few optical disc systems, and <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" class="mw-redirect" title="Gramophone record">vinyl audio records</a>) spins the media at one constant speed regardless of where the head is positioned.</li>/n</ul>/n<p>Track positioning also follows two different methods across disk storage devices. Storage devices focused on holding computer data, e.g., HDDs, FDDs, Iomega <a href="/wiki/Zip_drive" title="Zip drive">zip drives</a>, use concentric tracks to store data. During a sequential read or write operation, after the drive accesses all the sectors in a track it repositions the head(s) to the next track. This will cause a momentary delay in the flow of data between the device and the computer. In contrast, optical audio and video discs use a single <a href="/wiki/Spiral" title="Spiral">spiral</a> track that starts at the inner most point on the disc and flows continuously to the outer edge. When reading or writing data there is no need to stop the flow of data to switch tracks. This is similar to vinyl records except vinyl records started at the outer edge and spiraled in toward the center.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interfaces">Interfaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disk_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Interfaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>The disk drive interface is the mechanism/protocol of communication between the rest of the system and the disk drive itself. Storage devices intended for desktop and mobile computers typically use <a href="/wiki/AT_Attachment" class="mw-redirect" title="AT Attachment">ATA</a> (<a href="/wiki/Parallel_Advanced_Technology_Attachment" class="mw-redirect" title="Parallel Advanced Technology Attachment">PATA</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Serial_ATA" title="Serial ATA">SATA</a> interfaces. Enterprise systems and high-end storage devices will typically use <a href="/wiki/SCSI" title="SCSI">SCSI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serial_Attached_SCSI" title="Serial Attached SCSI">SAS</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fibre_Channel" title="Fibre Channel">FC</a> interfaces in addition to some use of SATA.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_terminology">Basic terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disk_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Basic terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<ul>/n<li>Disk - Generally refers to magnetic media and devices.</li>/n<li>Disc - Required by trademarks for certain optical media and devices.</li>/n<li>Platter – An individual recording disk. A hard disk drive contains a set of platters. Developments in optical technology have led to multiple recording layers on <a href="/wiki/Comparison_of_high_definition_optical_disc_formats" title="Comparison of high definition optical disc formats">DVDs</a>.</li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Spindle_(hard_disk_drive)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spindle (hard disk drive)">Spindle</a> – the spinning <a href="/wiki/Axle" title="Axle">axle</a> on which the platters are mounted.</li>/n<li>Rotation – Platters rotate; two techniques are common:/n<ul>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Constant_angular_velocity" title="Constant angular velocity">Constant angular velocity</a> (CAV) keeps the disk spinning at a fixed rate, measured in revolutions per minute (RPM). This means the heads cover more distance per unit of time on the outer tracks than on the inner tracks. This method is typical with computer hard drives.</li>/n<li><a href="/wiki/Constant_linear_velocity" title="Constant linear velocity">Constant linear velocity</a> (CLV) keeps the distance covered by the heads per unit time fixed. Thus the disk has to slow down as the arm moves to the outer tracks.
