class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Z3_Deutsches_Museum.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>/nZuse Z3 replica on display at <a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Museum" title="Deutsches Museum">Deutsches Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a></div>/n</div>/n</div>/n<p>The <b>Z3</b> was an <a href="/wiki/Electromechanical_computer" class="mw-redirect" title="Electromechanical computer">electromechanical computer</a> designed by <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Zuse" title="Konrad Zuse">Konrad Zuse</a>. It was the world's first working <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">programmable</a>, fully automatic <a href="/wiki/Digital_computer" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital computer">digital computer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The Z3 was built with 2,000 <a href="/wiki/Relay" title="Relay">relays</a>, implementing a 22-<a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bit</a> <a href="/wiki/Word_(data_type)" class="mw-redirect" title="Word (data type)">word</a> length that operated at a <a href="/wiki/Clock_frequency" class="mw-redirect" title="Clock frequency">clock frequency</a> of about 5–10&#160;<a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">Hz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Program code<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and constant data were stored on punched <a href="/wiki/Celluloid" title="Celluloid">film</a>.</p>/n<p>The Z3 was completed in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> in 1941. The <a href="/wiki/German_Aerospace_Center" title="German Aerospace Center">German Aircraft Research Institute</a> used it to perform statistical analyses of <a href="/wiki/Wing_flutter" class="mw-redirect" title="Wing flutter">wing flutter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Zuse asked the German government for funding to replace the relays with fully electronic switches, but funding was denied during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> since such development was deemed "not war-important".<sup id="cite_ref-kognitive-strukturen_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kognitive-strukturen-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:148</sup> The original Z3 was destroyed in 1943 during an <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Berlin_during_World_War_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Bombing of Berlin during World War II">Allied bombardment of Berlin</a>. The Z3 was originally called V3 (<i>Versuchsmodell 3</i> or Experimental Model 3) but was renamed to not be confused with Germany's <a href="/wiki/V-weapons" title="V-weapons">V-weapons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A fully functioning replica was built in the 1960s by Zuse's company, Zuse <a href="/wiki/Kommanditgesellschaft" title="Kommanditgesellschaft">KG</a>, and is on permanent display at <a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Museum" title="Deutsches Museum">Deutsches Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>. The Z3 was demonstrated in 1998 to be, in principle, <a href="/wiki/Turing-complete" class="mw-redirect" title="Turing-complete">Turing-complete</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rojas-ieee_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rojas-ieee-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rojas-universal_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rojas-universal-8">[8]</a></sup> However, because it lacked conditional branching, the Z3 only meets this definition by speculatively computing all possible outcomes of a calculation.</p>/n<p>Thanks to this machine and its predecessors, <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Zuse" title="Konrad Zuse">Konrad Zuse</a> is often regarded as the inventor of the computer.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>/n<p></p>/n<div id="toc" class="toc">/n<div class="toctitle">/n<h2>Contents</h2>/n</div>/n<ul>/n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a class="tocnumber">8</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>/n</ul>/n</div>/n<p></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_development">Design and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Z3_(computer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>Zuse designed the <a href="/wiki/Z1_(computer)" title="Z1 (computer)">Z1</a> in 1935 to 1936 and built it from 1936 to 1938. The Z1 was wholly mechanical and only worked for a few minutes at a time at most. <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Schreyer" title="Helmut Schreyer">Helmut Schreyer</a> advised Zuse to use a different technology. As a doctoral student at the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Institute_of_Technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Berlin Institute of Technology">Berlin Institute of Technology</a> in 1937 he worked on the implementation of Boolean operations and (in today's terminology) <a href="/wiki/Flip-flop_(electronics)" title="Flip-flop (electronics)">flip-flops</a> on the basis of <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tubes</a>. In 1938 Schreyer demonstrated a circuit on this basis to a small audience, and explained his vision of an electronic computing machine – but since the largest operational electronic devices contained far fewer tubes this was considered practically infeasible.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-13">[13]</a></sup> In that year when presenting the plan for a computer with 2,000 electron tubes, Zuse and Schreyer, who was an assistant at Prof. Wilhelm Stäblein's Telecommunication Institute at the Technical University of Berlin, were discouraged by members of the institute who knew about the problems with electron tube technology.<sup id="cite_ref-hellige-springer_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hellige-springer-14">[14]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Zuse decided to implement the next design based on relays. The realization of the <a href="/wiki/Z2_(computer)" title="Z2 (computer)">Z2</a> was helped financially by Dr. Kurt Pannke, who manufactured small calculating machines. The Z2 was completed in 1939 and presented to an audience of the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Deutsche Versuchsanstalt für Luftfahrt</i></span> ("German Laboratory for Aviation") in 1940 in Berlin-Adlershof. Zuse was lucky – this presentation was one of the few instances where the Z2 actually worked and could convince the DVL to partly finance the next design.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-13">[13]</a></sup></p>/n<p>Improving on the basic Z2 machine, he built the Z3 in 1941, which was a highly secret project of the German government.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Dr. Joseph Jennissen (1905–1977),<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> member of the "Research-Leadership" (<i>Forschungsführung</i>) in the <a href="/wiki/Air_Ministry_(Germany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Air Ministry (Germany)">Reich Air Ministry</a><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> acted as a government supervisor for orders of the ministry to Zuse's company <i>ZUSE Apparatebau</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> A further intermediary between Zuse and the Reich Air Ministry was the aerodynamicist <a href="/wiki/Herbert_A._Wagner" title="Herbert A. Wagner">Herbert A. Wagner</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>/n<p>The Z3 was completed in 1941 and was faster and far more reliable than the Z1 and Z2. The Z3 <a href="/wiki/Floating-point_arithmetic" title="Floating-point arithmetic">floating-point arithmetic</a> was improved over that of the Z1 in that it implemented exception handling. The exceptional values plus infinity, minus infinity and undefined could be generated and passed through operations. The Z3 stored its program on an external tape, thus no rewiring was necessary to change programs.</p>/n<p>On 12 May 1941 the Z3 was presented to an audience of scientists including the professors Alfred Teichmann and Curt Schmieden<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> of the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Deutsche Versuchsanstalt für Luftfahrt</i></span> ("German Laboratory for Aviation") in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> today known as the <a href="/wiki/German_Aerospace_Center" title="German Aerospace Center">German Aerospace Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>.</p>/n<p>Zuse moved on to the <a href="/wiki/Z4_(computer)" title="Z4 (computer)">Z4</a> design, which was built just days before World War II ended.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Z3_as_a_universal_Turing_machine">The Z3 as a universal Turing machine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Z3_(computer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Z3 as a universal Turing machine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>It was possible to construct loops on the Z3, but there was no <a href="/wiki/Conditional_branch" class="mw-redirect" title="Conditional branch">conditional branch</a> instruction. Nevertheless, the Z3 was <a href="/wiki/Turing-complete" class="mw-redirect" title="Turing-complete">Turing-complete</a> – how to implement a universal <a href="/wiki/Turing_machine" title="Turing machine">Turing machine</a> on the Z3 was shown in 1998 by <a href="/wiki/Ra%C3%BAl_Rojas" title="Raúl Rojas">Raúl Rojas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rojas-ieee_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rojas-ieee-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rojas-universal_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rojas-universal-8">[8]</a></sup> He proposes that the tape program would have to be long enough to execute every possible path through both sides of every branch. It would compute all possible answers, but the unneeded results would be canceled out (a kind of <a href="/wiki/Speculative_execution" title="Speculative execution">speculative execution</a>). Rojas concludes, "We can therefore say that, from an abstract theoretical perspective, the computing model of the Z3 is equivalent to the computing model of today's computers. From a practical perspective, and in the way the Z3 was really programmed, it was not equivalent to modern computers."</p>/n<p>From a pragmatic point of view, however, the Z3 provided a quite <i>practical</i> <a href="/wiki/Instruction_set" class="mw-redirect" title="Instruction set">instruction set</a> for the typical engineering applications of the 1940s – Zuse was a <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineer" title="Civil engineer">civil engineer</a> who only started to build his computers to facilitate his work in his main profession.</p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_other_work">Relation to other work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Z3_(computer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relation to other work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>/n<p>The success of Zuse's Z3 is often attributed to its use of the simple binary system.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> This was invented roughly three centuries earlier by <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz" class="mw-redirect" title="Gottfried Leibniz">Gottfried Leibniz</a>; <a href="/wiki/Boole" class="mw-redirect" title="Boole">Boole</a> later used it to develop his <a href="/wiki/Boolean_algebra_(logic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boolean algebra (logic)">Boolean algebra</a>. Zuse was inspired by <a href="/wiki/David_Hilbert" title="David Hilbert">Hilbert</a>'s and <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Ackermann" title="Wilhelm Ackermann">Ackermann</a>'s book on elementary mathematical logic (cf. <a href="/wiki/Principles_of_Mathematical_Logic" title="Principles of Mathematical Logic">Principles of Mathematical Logic</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-hellige-springer_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hellige-springer-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1937, <a href="/wiki/Claude_Shannon" title="Claude Shannon">Claude Shannon</a> <a href="/wiki/A_Symbolic_Analysis_of_Relay_and_Switching_Circuits" title="A Symbolic Analysis of Relay and Switching Circuits">introduced</a> the idea of mapping Boolean algebra onto electronic relays in a seminal work on <a href="/wiki/Digital_circuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital circuit">digital circuit</a> design. Zuse however did not know Shannon's work and developed the groundwork independently<sup id="cite_ref-kognitive-strukturen_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kognitive-strukturen-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:149</sup> for his first computer <a href="/wiki/Z1_(computer)" title="Z1 (computer)">Z1</a> which he designed and built from 1935 to 1938.</p>/n<p>Zuse's coworker Helmut Schreyer built an electronic digital experimental model of a computer using 100 vacuum tubes<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> in 1942, but it was lost at the end of the war.</p>/n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Flowers" title="Tommy Flowers">Tommy Flowers</a>-built <a href="/wiki/Colossus_computer" title="Colossus computer">Colossus</a> (1943)<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Atanasoff%E2%80%93Berry_Computer" class="mw-redirect" title="Atanasoff–Berry Computer">Atanasoff–Berry Computer</a> (1942) used <a href="/wiki/Thermionic_valve" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermionic valve">thermionic valves (vacuum tubes)</a> and binary representation of numbers. Programming was by means of re-plugging patch panels and setting switches.</p>/n<p>The <a href="/wiki/ENIAC" title="ENIAC">ENIAC</a> computer, completed after the war, used <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tubes" class="mw-redirect" title="Vacuum tubes">vacuum tubes</a> to implement switches and used decimal representation for numbers. Until 1948 programming was, as with Colossus, by patch leads and switches.</p>/n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Small-Scale_Experimental_Machine" class="mw-redirect" title="Small-Scale Experimental Machine">Manchester Baby</a> of 1948 and the <a href="/wiki/EDSAC" class="mw-redirect" title="EDSAC">EDSAC</a> of 1949 were the world's first computers that stored program instructions and data in the same space. In this they implemented the <a href="/wiki/Von_Neumann_architecture#Development_of_the_stored-program_concept" title="Von Neumann architecture">stored-program concept</a> which is frequently (but erroneously) attributed to <a href="/wiki/First_Draft_of_a_Report_on_the_EDVAC" title="First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC">a 1945 paper</a> by <a href="/wiki/John_von_Neumann" title="John von Neumann">John von Neumann</a> and colleagues.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Von Neumann is said to have given due credit to <a href="/wiki/Alan_Turing" title="Alan Turing">Alan Turing</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and the concept had actually been mentioned earlier by Konrad Zuse himself, in a 1936 patent application (that was rejected).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Konrad Zuse himself remembered in his memoirs: "During the war it would have barely been possible to build efficient stored program devices anyway."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_L._Bauer" title="Friedrich L. Bauer">Friedrich L. Bauer</a> wrote: "His visionary ideas (live programs) which were only to be published years afterwards aimed at the right practical direction but were never implemented by him."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>/n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specifications">Specifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
